# Origin Story — ELA Lesson Plans

**10 mini-lessons · 30–45 min each**

Each lesson follows the BreakFree-style structure:
- **Do Now / Bellringer** (~3 min) — short writing or sketch prompt that hooks into the day's theme
- **Mini Lesson** (~10–12 min) — teacher explains content; often anchored by a short text, video, or set of examples
- **Guided Practice** — class or pair work with teacher support
- **Independent Practice** — students apply on their own
- **Closing** — Exit Ticket with reflective sentence stems

Each lesson includes a **Differentiation Considerations** block and is aligned to **Massachusetts Curriculum Framework for ELA & Literacy (grades 9–10)** standards. Audience: 15–18 year olds in juvenile justice classrooms; expect wide literacy ranges within a single class.

---

## ENGLISH / LANGUAGE ARTS — 10 Mini-Lessons

**Arc:** Joseph Campbell's Hero's Journey as the literary spine. Students study the pattern across a wide range of **superhero stories**, then write their own hero's origin story. The four anchor heroes (Miles, Shuri, Storm, Kamala) appear as recurring touchstones, and the unit also pulls in other heroes — public-domain Golden Age (Captain Marvel/Shazam in Whiz Comics #2), Marvel mainstays narrated by the teacher (Peter Parker, T'Challa, Aunt May, Uncle Ben), and contemporary heroes who broaden representation (Static Shock / Virgil Hawkins, Ironheart / Riri Williams, Sam Wilson Captain America, Spider-Gwen). The aim is for students to see the Hero's Journey as a universal pattern across the superhero canon, not a Marvel-only template.

**Final artifact for the Hero Pitch:** Origin Story (1–2 pages, ~300–500 words).

### Day 1 — What Is a Hero?

**Lesson objectives:**
- Define "hero" using historical, cultural, and personal lenses.
- Distinguish heroism from fame, power, and celebrity.
- Identify 5 traits that, in their judgment, are essential to heroism.
- Connect the abstract definition of a hero to a real person in their own life or community.

**Assessment:**
- Hero Traits Worksheet
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- Students enter the unit through a personal hook — they have to name a *real* person they consider a hero before any superhero is mentioned.
- Class compares three working definitions of "hero" (ancient Greek root, modern dictionary, a contemporary quote) to surface that the word has always been contested.
- Through a paired ranking activity, students build a working "hero traits" list that the whole unit will return to when they design their original hero in the Hero Pitch.
- A short profile-writing task forces them to apply their own traits to a real human, not a fantasy.

| Component | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| **Do Now** | Write the name of **one real-life hero** (NOT a fictional one — no Marvel, no DC, no movies). In three sentences: who they are, what they did, and why *you* call that heroic. | Handout or journal |
| **Mini Lesson** | "What does *hero* actually mean?" Project three definitions side-by-side: (1) **Greek root *hērōs*** — a mortal of god-like courage, often half-divine; (2) **Merriam-Webster (modern)** — "a person admired for achievements and noble qualities"; (3) **Maya Angelou** — "I think a hero is any person really intent on making this a better place for all people." Class brainstorm on the board: what's the difference between *famous*, *powerful*, and *heroic*? Teacher captures emerging traits as students name them. | Slides (3 definitions), board space, marker |
| **Guided Practice** | **Hero Traits — Top 5 Ranking.** In pairs, students take the class trait list and cut it down to their personal **Top 5**. Rules: no duplicates, must defend each pick out loud to their partner. Partners challenge: "Why is *courage* on your list but not *honesty*?" Teacher circulates and pushes back on weak defenses. | Hero Traits Worksheet |
| **Independent Practice** | **Real-Life Hero Profile.** Using the person from their Do Now, students write a **5-sentence profile** that names at least 2 of their Top 5 traits as the reason this person counts as a hero. Bridge sentence at the end: "If I had to design a hero, the first trait I'd give them is ___ because ___." | Hero Traits Worksheet |
| **Closing** | **Exit Ticket** — finish one of the following: <br>• "The most important thing a hero has to be is ___ because ___." <br>• "I never used to think of ___ as a hero, but ___." <br>• "My hero is ___ — and the most important trait they have is ___." | Handout or journal |

**Differentiation Considerations:**
- **Hero Bank handout** for students who can't think of a real-life hero on the spot (12 examples — community organizers, civil rights leaders, family-member archetypes, athletes-turned-activists, teachers, mentors).
- Sentence frames provided for all written work ("My hero is ___ because they ___").
- Students may dictate the 5-sentence profile orally to a partner or teacher if writing is a barrier.
- For students who decline to share in pairs, the Top 5 ranking can be done individually with a brief teacher conference.
- Vocabulary preview for English learners: *hero, noble, heroic, fame vs. heroism, courage, integrity*.
- Exit ticket may be completed in writing, verbally to a teacher, or as a quick sketch with a one-line caption.

**Standards (Massachusetts Curriculum Framework for ELA & Literacy, 9–10):**
- **SL.9–10.1** — Initiate and participate effectively in a range of collaborative discussions with diverse partners on grades 9–10 topics, building on others' ideas and expressing their own clearly and persuasively.
- **W.9–10.4** — Produce clear and coherent writing in which the development, organization, and style are appropriate to task, purpose, and audience.
- **L.9–10.6** — Acquire and use accurately general academic and domain-specific words and phrases sufficient for reading, writing, speaking, and listening at the college and career readiness level.
- **W.9–10.10** — Write routinely over extended time frames and shorter time frames for a range of tasks, purposes, and audiences.

### Day 2 — Hero's Journey Overview

**Lesson objectives:**
- Identify the 8 stages of Joseph Campbell's Hero's Journey (simplified for high school).
- Recognize the Hero's Journey as a recurring narrative pattern across cultures and centuries.
- Map a familiar hero story onto the 8-stage wheel.
- Commit to a hero (real or fictional) they'll continue to reference across the unit.

**Assessment:**
- Hero's Journey Wheel handout
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- Students learn that the Hero's Journey isn't a Marvel invention — Joseph Campbell identified the pattern in 1949 across world mythologies (*The Hero with a Thousand Faces*). The same shape shows up in *The Odyssey*, *Star Wars*, *Black Panther*, *Spider-Man*, *Ms. Marvel*, and the original 1940s Captain Marvel / Shazam comics — every superhero origin students already know.
- The TED-Ed animated explainer "What Makes a Hero?" by Matthew Winkler does the heavy lifting on the pattern (~4 min). Teacher debriefs and connects to the unit.
- Class mapping of a shared hero (Luke Skywalker or Miles Morales — pick whichever the class knows better) makes the stages concrete before students try it independently.
- Independent mapping forces students to commit to a hero they already have feelings about — that hero becomes a reference point for the rest of the unit.

| Component | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| **Do Now** | Quickwrite: "Think of a movie, book, show, or video game where the main character ends up TOTALLY different from how they started. In 3–4 sentences: who were they at the start, and who were they at the end?" | Handout or journal |
| **Mini Lesson** | Project the 8-stage Hero's Journey wheel: (1) Ordinary World, (2) Call to Adventure, (3) Refusal / Mentor, (4) Crossing the Threshold, (5) Trials, Allies, Enemies, (6) The Ordeal, (7) Reward / Return, (8) The Gift. One-sentence definition each. <br><br>Play **TED-Ed: "What Makes a Hero?" by Matthew Winkler** (~4 min). After: thumbs-up / thumbs-sideways check — "which stage made the most sense to you?" Quick verbal share-out. | Slides (8-stage wheel + definitions); **TED-Ed video** — https://ed.ted.com/lessons/what-makes-a-hero-matthew-winkler (free, captions on). Optional follow-up materials at https://ed.ted.com/lessons/what-makes-a-hero-matthew-winkler/digdeeper |
| **Guided Practice** | **Class Mapping.** Together, fill in the 8 stages using a superhero the whole class knows. Default: **Spider-Man / Peter Parker** — the cleanest superhero Hero's Journey on record (ordinary kid → bitten by spider → refuses responsibility → loses Uncle Ben → becomes Spider-Man → returns as protector of his neighborhood). Secondary option: **Miles Morales** (anchor hero, *Into the Spider-Verse* arc). Teacher narrates each stage and writes student responses on the board. | Hero's Journey Wheel handout (the same template students will use independently) |
| **Independent Practice** | **Map Your Hero.** Each student picks ONE hero — real, fictional, mythological, anyone — and maps them onto the 8-stage wheel on their own handout. Stick figures, bullet points, or full sentences — student's choice. | Hero's Journey Wheel handout |
| **Closing** | **Exit Ticket** — "The stage of the Hero's Journey I think is MOST IMPORTANT is ___ because ___." | Handout or journal |

**Differentiation Considerations:**
- **Hero Bank handout** for students who can't pick a hero on the spot — 15 superhero examples spanning the anchors (Miles Morales, Shuri, Storm, Kamala Khan), classic mainstays (Spider-Man / Peter Parker, Black Panther / T'Challa, Captain Marvel / Carol Danvers, Wonder Woman, Captain America / Steve Rogers, Captain America / Sam Wilson), public-domain Golden Age (Captain Marvel / Shazam / Billy Batson), and contemporary heroes (Static Shock / Virgil Hawkins, Ironheart / Riri Williams, Spider-Gwen, Falcon).
- TED-Ed video played with captions; full transcript available for students who want to re-read.
- Simplified **5-stage** version of the wheel (Ordinary → Call → Struggle → Win → Return) available for students who need it.
- Partner mapping option for Independent Practice.
- Verbal mapping option — student talks through the stages aloud to a teacher instead of writing them.

**Standards (Massachusetts Curriculum Framework for ELA & Literacy, 9–10):**
- **RL.9–10.2** — Determine a theme or central idea of a text and analyze its development over the course of the text.
- **RL.9–10.3** — Analyze how complex characters develop over the course of a text, interact with other characters, and advance the plot or develop the theme.
- **RL.9–10.9** — Analyze how an author draws on and transforms source material in a specific work.
- **SL.9–10.2** — Integrate multiple sources of information presented in diverse media or formats, evaluating the credibility and accuracy of each source.

### Day 3 — The Ordinary World & Call to Adventure

**Lesson objectives:**
- Define "Ordinary World" and "Call to Adventure" as stages of the Hero's Journey.
- Identify how authors establish a character's normal life through concrete sensory details.
- Apply that skill to draft their hero's opening passage.
- Connect the Hero's Journey pattern to a real-life primary source.

**Assessment:**
- Ordinary World Draft (5–7 sentences)
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- Primary text is the OPENING SEQUENCE of **Whiz Comics #2** (Feb 1940, public domain) — the panels establishing Billy Batson's life before he becomes Captain Marvel/Shazam. Billy is a kid sleeping in the subway, selling newspapers on the street, no parents, no home. Pedagogically powerful: an Ordinary World told in comic panels, with concrete sensory details (the cold, the city, the newspaper stack, the lonely figure) that students can visually identify.
- Students see the Ordinary World stage rendered in two forms — comic panels (Whiz Comics #2 opening) and teacher-narrated modern prose (the opening of Miles Morales's life pre-spider-bite).
- The craft takeaway: a memorable Ordinary World comes from CONCRETE SENSORY DETAILS (names, smells, sounds, routines, worries) — not summary.
- Students draft their own hero's Ordinary World using a required-detail checklist, moving them from abstract to specific.
- The Call to Adventure is named and previewed here; full drafting of it happens in Day 4.

| Component | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| **Do Now** | Quickwrite: "Describe your OWN ordinary morning in 3 sentences. Make it boring on purpose — name three specific things (a sound, a smell, an object)." | Handout or journal |
| **Mini Lesson** | Define on slides: **Ordinary World** = the hero's everyday life before everything changes — what's at stake to lose. **Call to Adventure** = the moment that disrupts ordinary life and demands a choice. <br><br>Look at TWO Ordinary World examples: <br>• **Comic panels:** First 2–3 pages of **Whiz Comics #2** (Captain Marvel / Shazam origin) — the panels of Billy Batson on the streets, no parents, no home, selling newspapers. Public domain. Teacher reads captions and dialogue aloud while projecting. <br>• **Narration:** Teacher narrates the opening of **Miles Morales's** life pre-spider-bite — the Brooklyn apartment, his mom Rio and dad Jeff, the new school, the cousin, the music in the background. (Narration only — no copyrighted text shown.) <br><br>After each: students circle / list the CONCRETE DETAILS — names, sounds, smells, objects, feelings, fears. | Slides; **printed Whiz Comics #2 opening pages handout** (public domain, free scans at Comic Book Plus — https://comicbookplus.com — and Digital Comic Museum — https://digitalcomicmuseum.com); short bio note on Miles Morales for the teacher |
| **Guided Practice** | **Concrete-Detail Hunt.** In pairs, students annotate the Whiz Comics #2 opening pages — circle every concrete detail (people, places, objects, sounds, feelings). Share out. Discussion: why does specificity matter for the Ordinary World? What's lost if the opening is general? How does the COMIC do this differently from prose? | Annotated Whiz Comics handout; pencils/colored pencils |
| **Independent Practice** | **Ordinary World Draft.** Students draft their own hero's Ordinary World in 5–7 sentences. Required: AT LEAST 4 of — a place name, a person, a sound or smell, an object the hero owns, a daily routine, a worry. | Ordinary World Drafting handout |
| **Closing** | **Exit Ticket** — "The 'ordinary world' for my hero is set in ___. The detail in it that matters most is ___ because ___." | Handout or journal |

**Differentiation Considerations:**
- **Captions-only handout** for students who'd benefit from reading just the text of the Whiz Comics page (without the visual processing demand) — pull the dialogue and narration boxes onto a single sheet.
- **Visual-only handout** for students who'd benefit from working with the panels without text (extract the art and have them generate concrete-detail labels themselves).
- Vocabulary preview for any 1940s phrasing in the Whiz Comics text: *peddle, urchin, newsstand, ragged*.
- Sentence frames for the draft: "My hero lives in ___. Every morning they ___. The thing that always happens in their life is ___."
- Students may sketch + caption their Ordinary World instead of writing full prose (must still include the required details). This becomes a nice cross-link to Day 8 (comics literacy).
- Voice-typed / dictated drafts accepted.
- For students who'd rather build off a character they already know than invent a new one: they may draft the Ordinary World of an anchor hero (Miles, Shuri, Storm, or Kamala) and borrow that structure for their own hero in a later session.

**Standards (Massachusetts Curriculum Framework for ELA & Literacy, 9–10):**
- **RL.9–10.1** — Cite strong and thorough textual evidence to support analysis of what the text says explicitly as well as inferences drawn from the text.
- **RL.9–10.3** — Analyze how complex characters develop, interact with other characters, and advance the plot.
- **RL.9–10.7** — Analyze the representation of a subject or a key scene in two different artistic mediums (here: comic panels vs. teacher-narrated prose).
- **W.9–10.3.a / .d** — Engage and orient the reader by setting out a situation and its significance; use precise words, telling details, and sensory language to convey a vivid picture.

### Day 4 — Refusal, Mentor, Crossing the Threshold

**Lesson objectives:**
- Define the Refusal, Mentor, and Crossing the Threshold stages.
- Recognize the mentor archetype across real, mythological, and modern hero stories.
- Identify a mentor figure for their own hero.
- Draft a "moment everything changed" (Threshold) scene for their hero.

**Assessment:**
- Mentor Profile + Threshold Scene draft
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- Primary text is the **wizard / transformation sequence** of Whiz Comics #2 (Captain Marvel / Shazam). The wizard Shazam IS the textbook mentor: the mysterious stranger appears, takes Billy underground, reveals an ancient power, and pushes him across the threshold by giving him the magic word. Billy says "SHAZAM!" and there is no going back — he is now the hero. The whole sequence is Refusal → Mentor → Crossing the Threshold compressed into a few panels. Verified public domain.
- Three contrasting superhero mentor archetypes anchor the discussion: **the magical mentor** (Shazam → Billy Batson), **the chosen-family mentor** (Uncle Ben → Peter Parker / Aunt May → Miles Morales), and **the institutional mentor** (Professor X → the X-Men, including Storm).
- The Mentor Trait Carousel gives students six mentor archetypes to compare before designing their own.
- Drafting the Threshold scene is the second creative deliverable of the unit and feeds directly into the final Origin Story.

| Component | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| **Do Now** | Quickwrite: "Name ONE person who taught you something that stuck with you. (Don't name them out loud — just label, like 'cousin' or 'old coach.') Two sentences: who they are and what they taught you." | Handout or journal |
| **Mini Lesson** | Define on slides: **Refusal** (the hero says no at first — fear, doubt, comfort), **Mentor** (the person who shows up with knowledge, tools, or a push), **Threshold** (the moment of no going back). <br><br>Three superhero mentor examples: <br>• **Comic panel read-along:** The **wizard Shazam → Billy Batson** sequence in **Whiz Comics #2** (public domain). Project / hand out the panels: stranger appears on the street → Billy follows him underground → ancient wizard reveals himself → bestows the magic word → Billy hesitates → says "SHAZAM!" → transformation. Refusal, Mentor, AND Threshold in one sequence. <br>• **Chosen-family mentors:** **Uncle Ben → Spider-Man / Peter Parker** ("with great power comes great responsibility") and **Aunt May → Miles Morales** (the steady voice telling him what kind of hero he wants to be). Teacher narrates — no copyrighted text. <br>• **Institutional mentor:** **Professor X → Storm and the X-Men** — the school as a mentor, not just one person. Teacher narrates. <br><br>Discussion: what's the difference between a *teacher* and a *mentor*? Why is a mentor often someone the hero RESISTS at first? | Slides; **Whiz Comics #2 wizard-sequence handout** (public domain — free scans at Comic Book Plus, https://comicbookplus.com, and Digital Comic Museum, https://digitalcomicmuseum.com) |
| **Guided Practice** | **Mentor Trait Carousel.** 6 short superhero-mentor profiles posted around the room (or as stations cards): **the wizard Shazam** (magical / chosen-one mentor), **Uncle Ben** (the steady moral mentor), **Aunt May** (the surviving caretaker who keeps mentoring after Ben), **T'Chaka → T'Challa & Shuri** (the royal-father mentor), **Professor X** (the institutional mentor / school), and **Yondu → Peter Quill** (the *complicated* mentor — tough, criminal, loyal). Students walk through and on a handout note: **What does each mentor offer?** (knowledge, tough love, opportunity, a key, a warning). | 6 Mentor Profile cards (printable, teacher-written from publicly available character summaries); Carousel handout |
| **Independent Practice** | Two-part: <br>**(1) Mentor Profile** — Students name one mentor for their hero (invented). 4 sentences: who they are and what they offer. <br>**(2) Threshold Scene** — 3–5 sentences describing the moment the hero crosses the line and there's no going back. | Mentor Profile + Threshold Drafting handout |
| **Closing** | **Exit Ticket** — "The mentor in my hero's story shows up by ___. The moment my hero crosses the threshold is ___." | Handout or journal |

**Differentiation Considerations:**
- **Mentor Bank handout** with 12 superhero mentor examples for students who blank (the six in the Carousel plus: Frigga → Thor, Mar-Vell → Carol Danvers, Mary Jane → Spider-Gwen, J. Jonah Jameson as complicated mentor → Peter Parker, Pepper Potts → Riri Williams / Ironheart, and Misty Knight → various street-level heroes).
- Sentence stems for both the Mentor Profile and the Threshold Scene.
- Students may sketch the threshold moment as a single labeled panel with a caption instead of prose (cross-link to Day 8).
- Pair work option for the Carousel.
- Vocabulary preview: *mentor, threshold, refusal, archetype*.

**Standards (Massachusetts Curriculum Framework for ELA & Literacy, 9–10):**
- **RL.9–10.3** — Analyze how complex characters develop over the course of a text.
- **RL.9–10.9** — Analyze how an author draws on and transforms source material (the mentor archetype across periods and cultures).
- **W.9–10.3.b** — Use narrative techniques (dialogue, pacing, description, reflection) to develop experiences, events, and characters.
- **SL.9–10.1** — Initiate and participate effectively in collaborative discussions.

### Day 5 — Trials, Allies, Enemies

**Lesson objectives:**
- Define the Trials, Allies, and Enemies stage of the Hero's Journey.
- Recognize how supporting characters reveal who the hero is.
- Move beyond simple "good guy / bad guy" framing to design a "Complicated Person."
- Build a 3-character supporting cast for their hero.

**Assessment:**
- Supporting Cast handout (Ally / Enemy / Complicated Person)
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- The teaching move: a great hero story isn't hero vs. villain — it's hero among people who reveal them. The Complicated Person is the most important and the most often missing in student drafts.
- Students draw on their own lives (Do Now) before being asked to invent characters — anchoring the abstract in real social complexity.
- The Lightning Round Synthesis (borrowed from the BreakFree *Unacknowledged* format) keeps participation fast and high.
- The deliverable is the seed of the supporting cast that appears in their full Origin Story.

| Component | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| **Do Now** | "List 3 people in your life (just label them — 'cousin,' 'old friend,' 'teacher'): one ALLY, one ENEMY, one COMPLICATED. One word each for what they are to you." | Handout or journal |
| **Mini Lesson** | This stage = the hero's TRIALS and the ALLIES and ENEMIES who shape them. Key teaching point: in a great hero story, the supporting characters exist to REVEAL the hero. The hero's choices around them show who they really are. <br><br>Three superhero examples: <br>• **Miles Morales** — ally (Ganke, the best friend who keeps Miles human), enemy (the Kingpin), **complicated person** (his **Uncle Aaron / the Prowler** — family AND the villain Miles is hunting). One of the cleanest "complicated person" arcs in modern comics. <br>• **Peter Parker / Spider-Man** — ally (Ned Leeds, Mary Jane), enemy (Green Goblin), complicated person (Harry Osborn — best friend, son of his enemy, becomes an enemy himself). <br>• **T'Challa / Black Panther** — ally (Okoye, Shuri), enemy (Klaue), **complicated person (N'Jadaka / Killmonger)** — a cousin, a rival heir, raised in pain that T'Challa's family caused. The villain who isn't entirely wrong. <br><br>Discussion: "Why isn't a good story just hero vs. villain? What makes the COMPLICATED person matter more than the enemy?" | Slides |
| **Guided Practice** | **Allies & Enemies Lightning Round.** Project 6 short scenarios — each is 2 sentences describing a moment between a hero and someone else. For each: students get 15 seconds, then teacher cold-calls — "Is this an ally moment, an enemy moment, or a complicated moment? Defend in one sentence using the sentence stem." | Slides with 6 scenario cards; sentence-stem handout ("This is a ___ moment because ___") |
| **Independent Practice** | **Supporting Cast Design.** Students design 3 characters for their hero: <br>• **1 Ally** — name, relationship, what they bring to the hero <br>• **1 Enemy** — name, what they WANT that's opposite to the hero <br>• **1 Complicated Person** — someone who is BOTH for and against the hero (used to be one, becoming another, or refuses to pick) <br>Each in 3 sentences. | Supporting Cast handout (3 boxes) |
| **Closing** | **Exit Ticket** — "The 'complicated person' in my hero's story is ___. What makes them complicated is ___." | Handout or journal |

**Differentiation Considerations:**
- **Complicated Person Bank** handout — short examples of complicated relationships in stories: the parent who tries to protect you by holding you back; the friend who wants what's best but has their own agenda; the rival who pushes you to be better; the older person you used to look up to who let you down.
- Sentence stems for each of the 3 design boxes.
- Students may sketch portraits with annotations instead of prose.
- Lightning Round can use thumbs (up / down / sideways) for non-verbal participation.
- Vocabulary preview: *ally, archetype, foil, complicated*.

**Standards (Massachusetts Curriculum Framework for ELA & Literacy, 9–10):**
- **RL.9–10.3** — Analyze how complex characters develop and interact with other characters.
- **W.9–10.3.b** — Use narrative techniques to develop characters.
- **SL.9–10.1.c** — Propel conversations by posing and responding to questions that relate the current discussion to broader themes.

### Day 6 — The Ordeal & Transformation

**Lesson objectives:**
- Define the Ordeal — the hero's lowest moment — and its function in the story.
- Analyze how a real author uses an ordeal to mark a character's transformation.
- Identify what their hero stands to LOSE in their ordeal.
- Draft their hero's darkest moment.

**Assessment:**
- Hero's Ordeal Draft (5–8 sentences)
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- The Ordeal is the central lesson of the whole unit: the hero has to LOSE something or fail before they can become who they're meant to be. The loss isn't punishment — it's transformation.
- Two superhero ordeals anchor the lesson: **Storm's Cairo origin** (an anchor hero — a clean, well-documented superhero ordeal that demonstrates *internal* transformation, not just violence), and **Peter Parker losing Uncle Ben** (the most-cited Ordeal in superhero literature). Both are teacher-narrated from publicly available character summaries — no copyrighted text shown.
- The primary read-and-annotate text is a **curriculum-written Ordeal Exemplar** (see Materials) — a ~155-word original passage about an invented superhero at her lowest point. It models the exact craft moves students will use in their own draft.
- The drafting prompt requires students to name what their hero stands to lose and where the internal change happens — keeping the focus on transformation.

| Component | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| **Do Now** | Quickwrite: "Think of a superhero who LOSES BIG at some point. What did they lose? Did the loss change them? In 3 sentences." | Handout or journal |
| **Mini Lesson** | Define on slides: **The Ordeal** = the moment when everything falls apart for the hero. In a great story, this isn't where the hero gives up — it's where they become someone new. <br><br>Two superhero ordeals (teacher narrates from publicly available summaries — no copyrighted text shown): <br>• **Storm / Ororo Munroe** (anchor hero) — at 6 years old in Cairo, a plane crashes into her family's apartment. Her parents are killed. Storm is trapped under rubble for days. She survives — but the claustrophobia she develops never leaves her. Her powers emerge years later. Her ordeal is what shapes the leader she becomes. <br>• **Peter Parker / Spider-Man** — gets the powers, refuses responsibility, lets a thief go by — and that same thief kills his Uncle Ben. The grief and the guilt make him the hero he becomes. <br><br>Then: read aloud the **Ordeal Exemplar** — a curriculum-written ~155-word passage about an invented hero ("Maya") in a warehouse, captured, injured, holding onto the moment she decides not to give up. Class follows along on the handout. <br><br>Discussion: in each example, **what did the hero lose?** **What did they gain?** **Why does the worst moment become the turning point?** | Slides; **Ordeal Exemplar handout** (curriculum-written original passage — to be drafted as part of the materials packet; see note below) |
| **Guided Practice** | **Ordeal Annotation.** In pairs, students mark the Ordeal Exemplar with three colors: <br>• **Red** — the physical / external danger <br>• **Blue** — the emotional cost <br>• **Green** — the line where something shifts INSIDE the hero <br>Pairs share their three lines and defend their picks. | Annotated Ordeal Exemplar handout; colored pencils or highlighters |
| **Independent Practice** | **Hero's Ordeal Draft.** Students draft their hero's lowest moment in 5–8 sentences. Required: <br>(a) what the hero stands to lose <br>(b) one external threat (physical, social, or systemic) <br>(c) one internal cost (fear, doubt, betrayal) <br>(d) the line where something changes inside them | Ordeal Drafting handout |
| **Closing** | **Exit Ticket** — complete one of: <br>• "My hero's darkest moment is ___." <br>• "What my hero learns from losing is ___." <br>• "If my hero gives up here, they will ___." | Handout or journal |

**Differentiation Considerations:**
- A simplified version of the Ordeal Exemplar (shorter sentences, plainer vocabulary) for students reading below grade level — preserve the four required craft elements.
- Vocabulary preview: *ordeal, turning-point, defiant, resolve, transformation*.
- An **audio recording** of the Ordeal Exemplar (teacher-recorded) for students who learn better by listening.
- Students may record their Ordeal Draft as an audio monologue instead of writing it.
- Sentence frames for all four required elements of the draft.

**Sensitive content note:** Both superhero ordeals named in the Mini Lesson involve **loss of family** (Storm: parents in a plane crash; Spider-Man: uncle killed). For incarcerated students, these themes may resonate with their own experiences of loss. Preview the lesson openly: "We're going to talk about superheroes losing big. If something here feels close, you can step out without explaining." Pair with the class agreement that loss-into-strength is what the unit is about, and the room is a place to handle that with care. The curriculum-written Ordeal Exemplar deliberately uses a less personal kind of loss (a stolen object / a betrayal) to give a softer entry point for the writing work.

**Note on the Ordeal Exemplar:** A ~155-word original passage will be drafted as part of the materials packet for this lesson. It should hit all four required craft elements (external threat, emotional cost, internal shift, what's at stake to lose) in a way students can directly emulate. *Action item: draft this exemplar separately and add it to the materials folder, alongside the Day 9 Model Origin Story.*

- **RL.9–10.3** — Analyze how complex characters develop and change over the course of a text.
- **W.9–10.3.b** — Use narrative techniques (dialogue, pacing, description, reflection) to develop experiences and events.

### Day 7 — Return & The Gift

**Lesson objectives:**
- Define the "Return with the Elixir" stage.
- Identify what their hero brings back from the journey — the "gift."
- Connect their hero's gift to a real-world need (cross-link to Social Studies civic mission).
- Recognize that heroism is what the hero DOES with what they've been through.

**Assessment:**
- Hero's Gift Draft (3 prompts)
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- The Return is what separates a hero from a survivor — the gift has to go to someone other than the hero.
- Four superhero examples ground the abstract — each "elixir" is knowledge, a stance, or a change, not an object: Spider-Man / Miles Morales (Brooklyn community); Black Panther / T'Challa (Wakanda → world); Ms. Marvel / Kamala Khan (Jersey City, her Muslim community, and the broader Avengers); Captain Marvel / Billy Batson (kid-hero protecting other kids in his city). - This lesson is the deliberate handshake with Social Studies Day 10 (Civic Mission One-Pager) — the gift IS the civic mission.

### Day 8 — Graphic Novel Literacy

**Lesson objectives:**
- Identify the basic elements of comic / graphic novel narrative (panel, gutter, framing, speech vs. thought vs. narration).
- Analyze how visual choices change the meaning of a scene.
- Apply those tools to storyboard one scene from their hero's origin.
- Recognize comics as a literary form, not "less than" prose.

**Assessment:**
- 4-Panel Storyboard
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- Comics work because the READER fills in the gutter — the space between panels. That's a piece of literary theory most students haven't been told before.
- The classroom comic is **Whiz Comics #2 (February 1940)** — the debut of **Captain Marvel / Shazam**: a 12-year-old kid named Billy Batson who, after being chosen by an ancient wizard, transforms into an adult superhero by saying the word "SHAZAM!" This is a textbook Hero's Journey moment AND on-theme for the unit. The comic is verified public domain (Fawcett Publications, copyright lapsed) and freely available on Comic Book Plus and Digital Comic Museum.
- The transformation-from-kid-to-hero theme connects directly to the identity arc of the whole unit.
- Students don't need to be "good at drawing" — the focus is panel CHOICES, not execution.
- The 4-panel storyboard is built from a moment students ALREADY drafted in Days 4 or 6 — they're not inventing new content; they're translating it.

| Component | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| **Do Now** | Look at the projected comic page (one page from Billy Batson's origin sequence in Whiz Comics #2 — the panels where he follows a stranger into the subway, meets the wizard Shazam, and transforms). Without instruction yet: write 3 things you notice. What did you "read" first — image or words? | Slides with one Whiz Comics #2 page; handout or journal |
| **Mini Lesson** | **Comics Vocabulary** — project on slides: <br>• **Panel** — a single framed image <br>• **Gutter** — the space BETWEEN panels (where the reader supplies what happens) <br>• **Framing** — close-up, medium, wide; what we see and what we don't <br>• **Speech bubble** vs. **thought bubble** vs. **narration box** <br>• **Sound effects** as visual storytelling (KAPOW, SHAZAM!) <br>Walk through the same Whiz Comics page and call out each element in action. Key teaching point: the reader does work too — the GUTTER is where meaning is made (e.g., the panel after Billy says "SHAZAM!" — what does the gutter HIDE?). | Slides; **printed page(s) from Whiz Comics #2** (Feb 1940, public domain). Free scans available at: <br>• **Comic Book Plus** — https://comicbookplus.com (search "Whiz Comics") <br>• **Digital Comic Museum** — https://digitalcomicmuseum.com (search "Whiz Comics") <br>Recommended: print the 2–3 pages covering Billy's origin (the subway / wizard / transformation sequence). |
| **Guided Practice** | **Panel Re-Read.** Class re-reads the Whiz Comics page together. This time, the questions are: <br>• What does the gutter between Billy's "before" and "after" panels HIDE? Why? <br>• What does the framing of the biggest / most dramatic panel EMPHASIZE? <br>• Where does Billy's face change — and how is the change shown visually (size of the figure, lightning bolts, posture)? <br>Students mark answers on their handout copy. | Marked-up Whiz Comics #2 handout |
| **Independent Practice** | **4-Panel Storyboard.** Students translate ONE moment from their hero's origin (likely the Day 4 Threshold or Day 6 Ordeal) into 4 panels. Stick figures fine — the point is the CHOICES: <br>• Panel 1: Wide establishing shot <br>• Panel 2: Medium — character + something happening <br>• Panel 3: Close-up on a face, hand, or object — the EMOTIONAL beat <br>• Panel 4: Resolution — student's choice of framing <br>Each panel needs one piece of text (speech, thought, or narration). | 4-Panel Storyboard handout (blank panels); pencils, colored pencils |
| **Closing** | **Exit Ticket** — "The hardest panel to draw was ___. What made it hard was ___." | Handout or journal |

**Differentiation Considerations:**
- Panel **stencils** for students who don't draw confidently (rectangles, basic figure shapes).
- **Verbal walkthrough** option — student explains their 4 panels orally to a partner or teacher instead of drawing.
- **Collage** / cut-and-paste from old magazines allowed instead of drawing.
- **Caption-only option** — student writes the 4 panel CAPTIONS without drawing; captures the same narrative choices.
- For students with strong visual skills, optional extension: add a 5th "splash panel" twice as big as the others — what single moment deserves that?
- **Alternate PD superhero options** if a teacher wants variety across classes (all on Comic Book Plus and Digital Comic Museum, all verified public domain): Daredevil Comics #1 (Lev Gleason, 1941 — different character from Marvel's Daredevil), Black Terror appearances in *Exciting Comics* (Nedor, 1940s), and Phantom Lady (Fox Feature Syndicate, 1940s — note: review for content before using; Golden Age comics sometimes carry dated racial/gender content that needs framing).

**Standards (Massachusetts Curriculum Framework for ELA & Literacy, 9–10):**
- **RL.9–10.7** — Analyze the representation of a subject or a key scene in two different artistic mediums.
- **RL.9–10.6** — Analyze a particular point of view or cultural experience reflected in a work.
- **W.9–10.3.d** — Use precise words and phrases, telling details, and sensory language to convey a vivid picture.

### Day 9 — Drafting the Origin Story

**Lesson objectives:**
- Combine Days 3, 4, 6, and 7 drafts into a flowing 1–2 page Origin Story.
- Apply narrative techniques (concrete detail, dialogue, internal thought, bridge sentences) to a continuous piece.
- Use a model origin story to identify structural choices that work.
- Produce a complete first draft of the Hero Pitch ELA artifact.

**Assessment:**
- Full Origin Story First Draft (300–500 words)
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- Students don't write a new piece — they STITCH together what they already have. That reframe lowers the writing-from-scratch anxiety.
- The **Model Origin Story handout** is a curriculum-written 1-page exemplar (to be drafted as part of the materials packet — see note below) that demonstrates Ordinary World → Call → Mentor → Threshold → Ordeal → Gift in a clean structure.

### Day 10 — Revision & Peer Feedback (ELA Artifact)

**Lesson objectives:**
- Apply a structured peer feedback protocol to a partner's draft.
- Identify and revise three specific weak spots in their own draft.
- Produce a polished final Origin Story for the Hero Pitch portfolio.
- Reflect on the writing process as a whole.

**Assessment:**
- Final Origin Story (the ELA artifact for the Hero Pitch portfolio)
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- "2 Stars and 1 Wish" is the BreakFree-style peer feedback protocol — specific, low-threat, repeatable.
- Students must read the partner's draft TWICE before commenting (first for story, second for feedback). That's the move that separates substantive feedback from "good job!"
- Students must make THREE deliberate revision changes and mark each with a margin star — converting vague feedback into specific edits.
- The Exit Ticket reflects on the unit as a whole, not just the day.

| Component | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| **Do Now** | "Re-read your draft from yesterday. What's one sentence a reader would tell you needs work? Underline it. We'll come back to it." | Yesterday's draft |
| **Mini Lesson** | **The 2 Stars and 1 Wish Protocol.** Project the protocol on slides: <br>• **2 Stars** = two SPECIFIC things that are working ("the detail about the smell of the diner — real and specific," NOT "good job") <br>• **1 Wish** = one specific thing you'd like to see more of ("I wish I knew what the mentor SAID in the moment") <br>Read instructions: read the partner's draft TWICE before commenting (first for story, second for feedback). Teacher models giving 2 stars + 1 wish on a projected sample paragraph. | Slides with the protocol + worked example |
| **Guided Practice** | **Peer Feedback Round.** Pairs trade drafts. 6 minutes each to read + write 2 stars and 1 wish on the feedback handout. Then 3 minutes each for in-person debrief between partners. Teacher circulates and quietly redirects feedback that drifts to vague praise. | Peer Feedback handout (boxes for 2 stars + 1 wish) |
| **Independent Practice** | **Revision Pass — Three Deliberate Changes.** Students go back to their draft and make THREE specific changes based on the feedback. Each change marked with a star in the margin. Revised version submitted as their **Final Origin Story** for the Hero Pitch portfolio. | Students' drafts + Final Submission folder |
| **Closing** | **Exit Ticket** — "The change I made that I'm most proud of is ___. The hardest part of this whole unit was ___." | Handout or journal |

**Differentiation Considerations:**
- For students not done with the draft: revision can be partial — strongest section in detail rather than the whole piece.
- **Read-aloud peer pairs** option — partners read each other's drafts ALOUD before commenting (helps both reader and writer hear the prose).
- Sentence frames for the feedback: "One thing that really worked was ___ because ___. One thing I wish I saw more of was ___."
- **Teacher-conference alternative** to peer feedback for students who don't want to share.
- Audio-recorded final submission accepted for students for whom written final is a barrier (a 2-minute read-aloud is acceptable, with their written draft attached).

- **W.9–10.5** — Develop and strengthen writing as needed by planning, revising, editing, rewriting.
- **W.9–10.4** — Produce clear and coherent writing in which the development, organization, and style are appropriate to task, purpose, and audience.
- **SL.9–10.1.d** — Respond thoughtfully to diverse perspectives.
- **L.9–10.1 / L.9–10.2** — Demonstrate command of conventions of standard English grammar, usage, capitalization, punctuation, and spelling (applied in the revision pass).
