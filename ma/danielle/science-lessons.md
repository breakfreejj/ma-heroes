# Superheroes — Science Lesson Plans

**10 mini-lessons · 30–45 min each · Grades 6–12 (ages 12–19)**
**Final artifact: Power Science Brief** — students explain their original hero's power using real science.

---

## Science Arc

**Driving question:** *Could this power actually work?*

Across 10 lessons (5 themes × 2 days each), students learn the science that *almost* makes superpowers possible — then explain their own hero's power with real science vocabulary.

| Day | Theme | Focus | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | DNA & Mutations | The strawberry DNA lab — *what* DNA is | Danielle |
| 2 | DNA & Mutations | Plausibility — could these origins actually work? | Danielle |
| 3 | Heredity & Genetics | Punnett squares — single trait | Danielle |
| 4 | Heredity & Genetics | Multi-trait inheritance + Hero Family Tree | Danielle (extended) |
| 5 | Materials Science | Real materials — Kevlar, titanium, nanotubes | Coworker |
| 6 | Materials Science | Material design challenge | Coworker (extended) |
| 7 | Neuroscience | The brain on fear — amygdala & fight/flight | Danielle |
| 8 | Neuroscience | Training courage — the prefrontal cortex | Danielle (extended) |

| 10 | Power Science Brief | Peer review + finalize for portfolio | Synthesis |

# Superheroes: Lesson 1
## DNA & Mutations — Where Powers Come From

### Lesson objectives:
- Identify DNA as the genetic instruction manual found in every cell.
- Define mutation and explain how mutations can change a trait.
- Extract visible DNA from a strawberry and describe the observation.

### Assessment:
- Completed Worksheet
- Exit Ticket

### Key Points:
- Many superhero origins (Spider-Man, X-Men, Hulk) are grounded in a single idea: their DNA changed. This lesson asks students to take that idea seriously and test it against real biology.
- Students learn DNA is the instruction manual in every cell, and mutations are changes to those instructions — most do nothing, some are harmful, a rare few change a trait in a useful way.
- Students perform a strawberry DNA extraction — a low-cost, dramatic lab that lets them physically see DNA.
- This sets up Day 2's plausibility analysis.

| Component: | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| Do Now | Question: *"Spider-Man got his powers from a radioactive spider bite. Could that actually work? Yes / No / Maybe — one sentence why."* Students write, then class hand-vote tally. | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |
| Introduction of Superheroes | Introduce the science unit: across 10 lessons, students will learn the science behind powers — and by Lesson 10, write a Power Science Brief for their own hero. | Slides |
| Mini-Lesson | - DNA is the instruction manual inside every cell. <br>- A **mutation** is a change in those instructions. Most do nothing. Some kill the cell. A few rare ones change a trait. <br>- Real example: the **CCR5-delta32** mutation makes some people resistant to HIV — a real "superpower" caused by a real mutation. <br>- Today: pull DNA out of a strawberry so we can see it. | Slides |
| Guided Practice | **Strawberry DNA Extraction Lab.** In groups of 3–4: <br>1. Crush strawberry in zip-top bag with dish soap + salt (breaks cell membranes). <br>2. Filter into clear plastic cup through coffee filter. <br>3. Slowly pour chilled rubbing alcohol down the side of the cup. <br>4. Watch white stringy DNA precipitate. Lift it out with a stir stick. <br>**Safety:** No flames near alcohol. Goggles if available. | Slides, Handouts, strawberries, zip-top bags, dish soap, table salt, rubbing alcohol (chilled), coffee filters, plastic cups, stir sticks |
| Independent Practice | Each group sketches what they see in their science notebook and answers: <br>- What did you actually see? (observation, not interpretation) <br>- The strawberry had DNA. So do you. Name one trait of yours that DNA codes for. | Handouts, science notebooks |
| Closing | Exit Ticket: *"True or false: A radioactive spider bite could rewrite your DNA to give you spider powers. Defend your answer in two sentences using something we did or said today."* | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |

### Differentiation Considerations:
- Provide a fill-in-the-blank lab notes sheet for students who need scaffolding.
- Students may work in groups of 3–4 or with extra teacher support during the lab.
- Lab can be demonstrated by teacher front-of-class if hands-on group work is not feasible.
- Visual learners: project a labeled diagram of a cell showing where DNA is stored.

### Standard(s):

**Next Generation Science Standards**
- **MS-LS3-1** — Develop and use a model to describe why structural changes to genes (mutations) located on chromosomes may affect proteins and may result in harmful, beneficial, or neutral effects to the structure and function of the organism.
- **HS-LS3-1** — Ask questions to clarify relationships about the role of DNA and chromosomes in coding the instructions for characteristic traits passed from parents to offspring.

**Common Core Standards ELA**
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RST.6-8.1** — Cite specific textual evidence to support analysis of science and technical texts.
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.6-12.1** — Initiate and participate effectively in a range of collaborative discussions.

# Superheroes: Lesson 2
## Plausibility — Could These Origins Actually Work?

### Lesson objectives:
- Apply yesterday's DNA knowledge to evaluate the plausibility of fictional origins.
- Distinguish between *plausible*, *requires breakthrough*, and *impossible* mechanisms.
- Use scientific reasoning (hypothesis, evidence, mechanism) to defend a claim.

### Assessment:
- Completed Worksheet (Origin Plausibility Sort)
- Exit Ticket

### Key Points:
- Today students take what they learned about DNA and mutation and apply it: when a comic book says "radioactive spider," is there any real science there?
- Students learn the difference between *plausible* (a real mechanism could produce this), *requires breakthrough* (we'd need a major scientific advance to make this work), and *impossible* (this violates physics or biology).
- The activity is a sort: 6 famous origins, students place each in one of the three categories and justify with a one-sentence reason.
- This lesson teaches scientific *reasoning*, not just content. The discussion is the deepest part.

| Component: | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| Do Now | Question: *"List 3 superpowers. Star the one you think is most scientifically possible. Why?"* | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |
| Mini-Lesson | - Quick recall: DNA + mutation from yesterday. <br>- Introduce the three categories: <br>  - **Plausible** — a real mechanism exists (e.g., CCR5-delta32 → HIV resistance). <br>  - **Requires breakthrough** — possible in theory but we'd need a major scientific advance (e.g., regrowing limbs like a lizard). <br>  - **Impossible** — violates physics or biology (e.g., turning invisible by "bending light" with no mechanism). <br>- Walk through one example as a class: Spider-Man. The radioactive bite mutating cells = *implausible* (radiation usually kills cells or causes cancer). But spider-like silk glands = *requires breakthrough* (we know spiders do it, scaling to humans is far off). | Slides |
| Guided Practice | Show 2 more examples on the board and have students vote with fingers (1 = plausible, 2 = breakthrough, 3 = impossible). Discuss as a class. <br>- Captain America's super-serum: most students will say 2 or 3 — get them to justify. <br>- Wolverine's healing: connect to real regenerative biology (axolotls regenerate limbs). | Slides |
| Independent Practice | **Origin Plausibility Sort.** Students get a handout with 6 comic-book origins. They sort each into the three categories and write a one-sentence reason: <br>1. Radioactive spider bite (Spider-Man) <br>2. Gamma rays exposure (Hulk) <br>3. Super-serum injection (Captain America) <br>4. Lab accident with chemicals (Flash) <br>5. Alien DNA hybrid (Superman) <br>6. Lightning strike (Black Lightning) | Handouts |
| Closing | Exit Ticket: *"Pick one origin from today. Argue for or against its plausibility in 2 sentences. Use one scientific term we've learned (DNA, mutation, gene, cell, etc.)."* | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |

### Differentiation Considerations:
- Reduce to 3 origins for students who need a smaller cognitive load.
- Allow verbal explanation in place of writing for the sort.
- Sentence frames: *"___ is plausible/impossible because ___."*
- Advanced extension: research CRISPR and write a paragraph: *if we can edit DNA, what origins move from "impossible" to "plausible"?*
- Sort can be done as a whole-class debate with students physically moving to corners of the room labeled with each category.

### Standard(s):

**Next Generation Science Standards**
- **HS-LS3-2** — Make and defend a claim that inheritable genetic variations may result from new genetic combinations, viable errors during replication, and/or mutations.
- **SP6** — Constructing explanations and designing solutions.
- **SP7** — Engaging in argument from evidence.

**Common Core Standards ELA**
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.W.6-12.1** — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence.
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.6-12.1** — Initiate and participate effectively in a range of collaborative discussions.

# Superheroes: Lesson 3
## Heredity — Punnett Squares (Single Trait)

### Lesson objectives:
- Define dominant and recessive alleles.
- Construct a Punnett square to predict trait inheritance for a single trait.
- Calculate probability ratios from a Punnett square.

### Assessment:
- Completed Worksheet
- Exit Ticket

### Key Points:
- This lesson builds on the DNA foundation: now that students know traits come from DNA, they learn how those traits get passed from parent to child.
- Students learn dominant/recessive alleles, capital/lowercase letter notation, and how to build a Punnett square.
- Single-trait focus today; multi-trait comes in Day 4.
- The "hero" framing: if Storm's mother had weather powers (dominant) and her father did not, what's the chance Storm has them?

| Component: | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| Do Now | Question: *"Why do some siblings look so different even though they have the same parents?"* | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |
| Mini-Lesson | - Refresh: DNA → genes → traits. <br>- Define **dominant** (always shows up if present) and **recessive** (only shows up if both copies are recessive). <br>- Notation: capital S = dominant super-strength, lowercase s = no super-strength. <br>- Build a Punnett square together for *Ss × ss*: half the offspring get S from one parent, all get s from the other. Result: 50% Ss (super), 50% ss (not). | Slides, Handouts |
| Guided Practice | Teacher walks through one full example with the class: *Ss × Ss*. Class predicts ratios: 1 SS : 2 Ss : 1 ss = 75% super-strong, 25% not. Why? Because S is dominant. Ask: *"What does it mean that the SS and Ss kids both look super-strong?"* | Slides, Handouts |
| Independent Practice | **Single-Trait Practice.** Students complete 4 Punnett square problems on a worksheet: <br>1. Storm's parents — one Ww, one ww (W = weather powers, dominant). What's the chance Storm has powers? <br>2. Two ss parents — what's the chance of a super-strong kid? <br>3. Two SS parents — same question. <br>4. Student-created: invent your own parents and one trait. Predict the offspring. | Slides, Handouts |
| Closing | Exit Ticket: *"What does 'dominant' mean in genetics? Use one of today's hero examples."* | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |

### Differentiation Considerations:
- Provide pre-filled partial Punnett squares (1 or 2 boxes done) for students who need scaffolding.
- Allow students to work in pairs to reduce cognitive load.
- Visual learners: provide a printed grid template rather than drawing from scratch.
- Advanced extension: introduce **codominance** (both alleles partially express — like blood types).
- Sentence stems for the exit ticket: *"A dominant trait is one that ___ even when ___."*

### Standard(s):

**Next Generation Science Standards**
- **MS-LS3-2** — Develop and use a model to describe why asexual reproduction results in offspring with identical genetic information and sexual reproduction results in offspring with genetic variation.
- **HS-LS3-3** — Apply concepts of statistics and probability to explain the variation and distribution of expressed traits in a population.

**Common Core Standards Math**
- **CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.7.SP.C.7** — Develop a probability model and use it to find probabilities of events.

**Common Core Standards ELA**
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.6-12.1** — Initiate and participate effectively in a range of collaborative discussions.

# Superheroes: Lesson 4
## Heredity — Multi-Trait Inheritance & The Hero Family Tree

### Lesson objectives:
- Construct Punnett squares for multiple independent traits.
- Calculate the compound probability of inheriting multiple traits.
- Explain why a trait can be inherited and still not appear (heterozygous carriers).

### Assessment:
- Hero Family Tree project (Handout)
- Exit Ticket

### Key Points:
- Today students extend yesterday's single-trait Punnett work to multiple traits at once.
- They learn that probabilities multiply when traits are independent (e.g., 50% × 50% = 25% chance of inheriting both).
- The big project: invent two hero parents with three traits each, predict the offspring, and roll dice to simulate the actual result.
- Critical concept: a trait can be inherited (the gene is present) but not show up (the dominant allele masks it). Heroes can be *carriers* of powers they don't display.

| Component: | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| Do Now | Question: *"If a hero's parent has super-strength, does that mean the kid will too? Why or why not?"* | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |
| Mini-Lesson | - Recap single-trait Punnett squares. <br>- Introduce **multi-trait inheritance**. For two independent traits, multiply probabilities: <br>  - Trait 1: 50% chance of super-strength <br>  - Trait 2: 50% chance of flight <br>  - Both at once: 50% × 50% = 25% <br>- Concept of a **carrier**: a person who has the recessive gene but doesn't show the trait. They can still pass it on. <br>- Most real powers wouldn't be single-gene — but this model is how scientists predict rare-trait inheritance in real medicine. | Slides, Handouts |
| Guided Practice | Teacher walks through a 2-trait example on the board: <br>- Parent 1: Ss (super) Ff (flight) <br>- Parent 2: ss (no super) ff (no flight) <br>- Build a 4-cell Punnett showing all 4 possible offspring combinations. | Slides |
| Independent Practice | **Hero Family Tree Project.** In pairs, students: <br>1. Invent two hero parents with three traits each (e.g., flight, super-strength, telepathy). <br>2. Decide if each trait is dominant or recessive. <br>3. Build three single-trait Punnett squares. <br>4. Calculate the % chance their offspring inherits all three powers. <br>5. Roll dice to "simulate" the actual offspring's genotype (odd = dominant, even = recessive). <br>6. Sketch the resulting child and label which traits they have. | Slides, Handouts, dice (2 per pair), colored pencils |
| Closing | Exit Ticket: *"A trait can be inherited and still not show up. Explain why in one sentence using the word 'carrier' or 'recessive.'"* | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |

### Differentiation Considerations:
- Provide pre-set parental genotypes for pairs who struggle to invent their own.
- Reduce to 2 traits instead of 3 for students needing simpler problems.
- Visual learners: allow the resulting child to be drawn rather than described in writing.
- Advanced extension: introduce a **second-trait dependency** (e.g., flight only works *if* the strength gene is also present) — explore *epistasis* in simple terms.
- Pairs or small groups for students who benefit from collaborative idea generation.

### Standard(s):

**Next Generation Science Standards**
- **HS-LS3-3** — Apply concepts of statistics and probability to explain the variation and distribution of expressed traits in a population.
- **SP5** — Using mathematics and computational thinking.

**Common Core Standards Math**
- **CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.7.SP.C.8** — Find probabilities of compound events using organized lists, tables, tree diagrams, and simulation.

**Common Core Standards ELA**
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.6-12.1** — Initiate and participate effectively in a range of collaborative discussions.

# Superheroes: Lesson 5
## Materials Science — Vibranium & Beyond

### Lesson objectives:
- Identify three real-world advanced materials (Kevlar, titanium, carbon nanotubes) and their properties.
- Explain how a material's structure determines its function.
- Distinguish between "biological powers" (Days 1–4) and "material powers" (today).

### Assessment:
- Completed Worksheet
- Exit Ticket

### Key Points:
- Not all superpowers come from biology — many come from **materials**. Shuri's Vibranium, Iron Man's suit, Spider-Man's webs, Captain America's shield: all materials science.
- Students learn that real engineers design materials for specific jobs. Kevlar stops bullets because of how its molecules are aligned. Titanium is strong because of how its atoms pack. Carbon nanotubes are stronger than steel and lighter than paper because of geometry.
- Vibranium is fictional — but its properties (absorbs vibration, lightweight, nearly unbreakable) are what real engineers chase.
- This lesson connects to Shuri (Black Panther's sister, Wakanda's chief engineer): the hero as scientist.

| Component: | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| Do Now | Question: *"Name 3 things stronger than human bone."* Quick share-out. | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |
| Mini-Lesson | - Powers don't always come from the body. They can come from **what the hero wears, holds, or builds**. <br>- Show 3 real-world materials: <br>  - **Kevlar** — bullet-resistant. Used in police/military body armor. Stronger than steel by weight. <br>  - **Titanium** — strong + lightweight. Used in jet engines, spacecraft, surgical implants. <br>  - **Carbon nanotubes** — stronger than steel, lighter than paper. Being researched for space elevators. <br>- Each is special because of its **structure**. Materials science = designing structure to get desired properties. <br>- Vibranium is fictional, but its properties (absorbs vibration, lightweight, nearly unbreakable) are what real engineers are still chasing. <br>- Connect to Shuri: the hero as scientist/engineer. | Slides |
| Guided Practice | Class compare-and-contrast on chart paper: List the 3 real materials and Vibranium side-by-side. For each: what does it do? Why is it special? What problem does it solve? | Slides, chart paper |
| Independent Practice | **Materials Identification Worksheet.** Students match 6 real-world applications to the right material: <br>- Police vest → Kevlar <br>- Bicycle frame → Titanium (or carbon) <br>- Spacecraft body → Titanium / composites <br>- Bridge cables → Steel <br>- Surgical implant → Titanium <br>- Cell phone screen → Strengthened glass <br><br>Then a short reflection: *which 2 properties would your hero's gear need?* | Handouts |
| Closing | Exit Ticket: *"Pick one real-world material from today. Name one job it does. What property makes it good at that job?"* | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |

### Differentiation Considerations:
- Provide a properties glossary handout (with terms like "lightweight," "flexible," "high tensile strength" defined).
- Allow students to draw the materials in use rather than write about them.
- Sentence frames: *"___ is good for ___ because it is ___."*
- Advanced extension: research a cutting-edge material (graphene, aerogel, shape-memory alloys) and present a 1-minute explainer.
- Pairs or small groups for the matching task.

### Standard(s):

**Next Generation Science Standards**
- **MS-PS1-3** — Gather and make sense of information to describe that synthetic materials come from natural resources and impact society.
- **HS-PS2-6** — Communicate scientific and technical information about why the molecular-level structure is important in the functioning of designed materials.

**Common Core Standards ELA**
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RST.6-12.4** — Determine the meaning of symbols, key terms, and other domain-specific words and phrases used in a specific scientific context.
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.6-12.1** — Initiate and participate effectively in a range of collaborative discussions.

# Superheroes: Lesson 6
## Materials Science — Design Challenge

### Lesson objectives:
- Apply the engineering design process to invent a fictional material.
- Specify three properties of a designed material and justify each choice.
- Identify a real-world problem the material would solve.

### Assessment:
- Material Design One-Pager
- Exit Ticket

### Key Points:
- Today students *use* yesterday's materials science: they design their own fictional material.
- Introduces the **engineering design cycle**: problem → constraints → design → critique.
- Critical move: every property is a trade-off. A material that's bulletproof but heavy works for tanks, not for a hero who flies. Students must justify their choices.
- Connects to Day 5's hero-as-engineer framing. Today every student is Shuri.

| Component: | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| Do Now | Question: *"If you could invent a material that didn't exist yet, what would you make it do? Be specific."* | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |
| Mini-Lesson | - Introduce the **engineering design cycle**: <br>  1. **Problem** — what real-world need does the material solve? <br>  2. **Constraints** — what limits the design (cost, weight, environment)? <br>  3. **Properties** — what features will the material have? <br>  4. **Trade-offs** — every choice has a cost. <br>- Show one teacher exemplar: a fictional material called "Lumen" that is lightweight, transparent, and self-healing. Problem: airplane windshields that don't crack. Trade-off: expensive to manufacture. <br>- Connect to Shuri: real engineers design like this every day. | Slides, teacher exemplar |
| Guided Practice | Class brainstorm on chart paper: real-world problems that need new materials. Examples: <br>- Buildings that survive earthquakes <br>- Clothing that adapts to temperature <br>- Bandages that monitor wounds <br>- Roads that absorb pothole damage <br>- Spacesuit fabric for Mars | Slides, chart paper |
| Independent Practice | **Material Design Challenge.** Each student (or pair) completes a Design One-Pager: <br>1. Material name <br>2. 3 properties (chosen and justified) <br>3. The real-world problem it solves <br>4. 1 trade-off or cost <br>5. Sketch of the material in use, with labels <br>6. Which hero could use it, and how? | Handouts, colored pencils |
| Closing | Exit Ticket: *"What is the trade-off of your designed material? Every real material has one."* | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |

### Differentiation Considerations:
- Provide a property menu (with 8–10 pre-listed properties) for students who struggle with brainstorming.
- Allow drawing in place of writing for the sketch portion.
- Sentence frames: *"My material is called ___. It is ___, ___, and ___. It solves the problem of ___. The trade-off is ___."*
- Advanced extension: students present their design as a 1-minute "Shark Tank" style pitch.
- Pairs or small groups for collaborative design.

### Standard(s):

**Next Generation Science Standards**
- **MS-ETS1-1** — Define the criteria and constraints of a design problem with sufficient precision to ensure a successful solution.
- **HS-ETS1-2** — Design a solution to a complex real-world problem by breaking it down into smaller, more manageable problems.
- **HS-PS2-6** — Communicate scientific and technical information about why the molecular-level structure is important in the functioning of designed materials.

**Common Core Standards ELA**
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.W.6-12.2** — Write informative/explanatory texts to examine a topic and convey ideas, concepts, and information.

# Superheroes: Lesson 7
## The Brain on Fear — Courage as Neuroscience

### Lesson objectives:
- Label the parts of the brain involved in the fear response (amygdala, hypothalamus).
- Describe the fight/flight/freeze response and the physical changes it triggers.
- Explain why courage is *acting while* the fear response is firing, not the absence of fear.

### Assessment:
- Brain Diagram Handout
- Exit Ticket

### Key Points:
- Not all powers are biological or material — some live in the brain. The most universal hero trait is **courage**, and courage has real neuroscience behind it.
- Students learn the fear response chain: amygdala detects threat → hypothalamus signals adrenal glands → adrenaline + cortisol flood the body → heart rate up, blood to muscles, fight/flight/freeze.
- Critical reframe: **the fear response is not weakness. It's your body trying to save your life.** Courage is acting *while* this is firing.
- This lesson lands especially hard for students who have been told (or told themselves) that fear means failure. **Teachers should not rush this lesson.**

| Component: | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| Do Now | Question: *"Your heart is pounding. Your hands shake. Your stomach drops. Is this fear, excitement, or both? What's the difference?"* | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |
| Mini-Lesson | Build the chain on the board: <br>- Threat detected → **amygdala** fires <br>- Amygdala signals → **hypothalamus** → adrenal glands → **adrenaline + cortisol** <br>- Body: heart rate up, blood to muscles, digestion off, pupils dilate <br>- This is **fight, flight, or freeze**. <br><br>Key idea: **the fear response is not weakness. It's your body trying to save your life.** Courage isn't the *absence* of this response — courage is acting *while* it's firing. That's literally what every soldier, firefighter, EMT, and parent in a crisis does. | Slides, brain diagram handout |
| Guided Practice | Pass out brain diagram. As a class, label the amygdala and hypothalamus. Briefly note: the **prefrontal cortex** (above the eyes) is what *overrides* the fear response. That's the part you train through practice and reflection. (Day 8 picks this up in depth.) | Brain diagram handout |
| Independent Practice | **Fear Scenario Activity.** In groups of 3, students read 5 scenario cards: <br>- "Speaking in front of a judge" <br>- "Running into a burning building" <br>- "Telling someone you love them" <br>- "Asking for help" <br>- "Standing up to a friend who's wrong" <br><br>For each: <br>- Mark whether the body's fear response would fire. <br>- Mark whether the action takes *courage*. <br><br>Notice the overlap: every act of courage has the body screaming "don't." | Slides, scenario cards, Handouts |
| Closing | Exit Ticket: *"Name one thing that scared you this year that you did anyway."* (Students may write privately — this is not for share-out unless they volunteer.) | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |

### Differentiation Considerations:
- Provide a pre-labeled brain diagram with shorter scenarios for students who need scaffolding.
- Allow students to whisper or draw their exit ticket response if writing about personal fear is difficult.
- Be mindful: this lesson can surface real emotional material. Have a plan for students who may need a break or one-on-one time.
- Advanced extension: research how chronic stress alters the amygdala over time.
- Group size flexibility: 3–4 for richer discussion, individual work if group dynamics are off that day.

### Standard(s):

**Next Generation Science Standards**
- **MS-LS1-3** — Use argument supported by evidence for how the body is a system of interacting subsystems composed of groups of cells.
- **HS-LS1-2** — Develop and use a model to illustrate the hierarchical organization of interacting systems.
- **HS-LS1-3** — Plan and conduct an investigation to provide evidence that feedback mechanisms maintain homeostasis.

**Common Core Standards ELA**
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.6-12.1** — Initiate and participate effectively in a range of collaborative discussions.

# Superheroes: Lesson 8
## Training Courage — The Prefrontal Cortex

### Lesson objectives:
- Identify the prefrontal cortex as the brain region that regulates the fear response.
- Describe 3 evidence-based ways to strengthen the prefrontal cortex (practice, mindfulness, social support).
- Apply the model to their own hero: what does the hero train, and how?

### Assessment:
- Hero Training Plan Handout
- Exit Ticket

### Key Points:
- Yesterday: fear is automatic. Today: courage can be *trained*.
- The **prefrontal cortex** (PFC) — the part of the brain right behind the forehead — is what regulates the amygdala. A stronger PFC means more control under stress.
- 3 evidence-based ways to strengthen the PFC: <br>  1. **Deliberate practice** — repeatedly facing manageable challenges. <br>  2. **Mindfulness** — focused attention on the present (even 5 min/day shows brain changes). <br>  3. **Social support** — talking through fear with a trusted person reduces amygdala activation in real time.
- Every hero in fiction has a *training arc* — Karate Kid, Rocky, Spider-Man learning his powers. These aren't filler. They reflect real neuroscience.
- Students design a "training plan" for their hero: what skill, what practice, what support.

| Component: | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| Do Now | Question: *"Name a hero who had to train before they were good at their power. What did they do?"* | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |
| Mini-Lesson | - Recap from yesterday: amygdala fires automatically. We don't choose to feel fear. <br>- New today: **the prefrontal cortex (PFC)** is the brain's "brake" — it can override the amygdala. <br>- The PFC can be strengthened. Three evidence-based methods: <br>  1. **Deliberate practice** — manageable challenges, repeated. The brain literally rewires. <br>  2. **Mindfulness** — focused attention. Even 5 min/day shows measurable brain changes after 8 weeks (Harvard studies). <br>  3. **Social support** — talking to a trusted person reduces amygdala activation. Connection is regulation. <br>- Every fictional hero training montage is showing real neuroscience. Daniel-san in Karate Kid is building his PFC. | Slides |
| Guided Practice | As a class, brainstorm: what's one skill (not a superpower — a real skill) that you've gotten better at over time? Chart on board. Identify which of the 3 methods (practice, mindfulness, support) you used. | Slides, chart paper |
| Independent Practice | **Hero Training Plan.** Each student designs a training plan for their hero: <br>1. What skill or power needs training? <br>2. Practice plan — what does the hero do daily/weekly? <br>3. Mindfulness component — how does the hero stay focused/calm? <br>4. Social support — who helps the hero, and how? <br>5. Setback plan — what does the hero do when training fails? | Handouts |

# Superheroes: Lesson 9
## Power Science Brief — Drafting Day

### Lesson objectives:
- Synthesize learning from Days 1–8 to explain a fictional hero's power with real science.
- Use domain-specific vocabulary (mutation, allele, materials, amygdala, etc.) accurately in writing.
- Identify the **mechanism** and **limit** of a power, not just its effect.

### Assessment:
- Power Science Brief First Draft

### Key Points:
- This is the synthesis day. Students have spent 8 lessons learning the science toolkit. Today they apply it.
- The Power Science Brief is a one-page explainer that names a hero's power, identifies which scientific category explains it (genetics / materials / neuroscience / a blend), describes the *mechanism*, and names a *limit*.
- Critical concept: **every real power has a cost.** A power with no limit isn't a power — it's a story-killer. The Brief must include at least one realistic constraint.
- Today is for *drafting*. Tomorrow is for peer review + revision.

| Component: | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| Do Now | Question: *"What's your hero's #1 power? Write one sentence on how it might work."* | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |
| Mini-Lesson | Review the **science toolkit** from the past 8 lessons: <br>1. **Genetics & mutation** (Days 1–2) <br>2. **Heredity & inheritance** (Days 3–4) <br>3. **Materials science** (Days 5–6) <br>4. **Neuroscience & courage** (Days 7–8) <br><br>Introduce the **Power Science Brief template**: <br>- Hero name <br>- Power (one sentence) <br>- Scientific category (which lessons' science explains it?) <br>- Mechanism (3–5 sentences using real vocabulary) <br>- Limit (what's the cost or constraint?) <br>- Real-world example (closest existing comparison) <br><br>Show one teacher exemplar: a finished Brief for a sample hero. | Slides, Brief template handout, teacher exemplar |
| Independent Practice | Students draft their Power Science Brief. Teacher circulates and conferences. Encourage use of vocabulary from all 4 lesson categories. | Brief template handout, science notebooks (for reference back to lessons) |
| Closing | Exit Ticket: *"What scientific category best explains your hero's power? Defend in one sentence."* | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |

# Superheroes: Lesson 10
## Power Science Brief — Peer Review & Finalize

### Lesson objectives:
- Give and receive structured peer feedback using the TAG protocol.
- Revise a draft based on feedback.
- Produce a final, polished Power Science Brief for the Hero Codex portfolio.

### Assessment:
- **Final product: Power Science Brief** (goes into the Hero Codex portfolio)
- Exit Ticket

### Key Points:
- Today is the finalization day. Drafts from yesterday get peer-reviewed using the **TAG protocol** (Tell, Ask, Give), then revised, then finalized for the portfolio.
- Peer review teaches students to give *specific* feedback — "I liked it" isn't feedback. "I liked how you explained the mutation mechanism" is feedback.
- The final Brief joins the Hero Codex portfolio alongside the Origin Story (ELA), Stat Sheet (Math), and Cultural Lineage (Social Studies) panels.
- Celebrate! This is the end of the science arc.

| Component: | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| Do Now | Question: *"Read your draft from yesterday. Underline your strongest sentence."* | Yesterday's Brief draft |
| Mini-Lesson | Introduce the **TAG peer review protocol**: <br>- **T**ell — one specific thing you liked (be specific, not "good job"). <br>- **A**sk — one clarifying question. <br>- **G**ive — one suggestion. <br><br>Show how to apply it. Read a sample Brief together and demonstrate TAG feedback live. | Slides, Peer Review handout |
| Partner Practice | Students swap drafts with a partner and complete a TAG review on the Peer Review handout. Both partners review both drafts (10 min per draft). | Peer Review handout |
| Independent Practice | Students revise their Brief based on partner feedback. Teacher circulates and conferences. Briefs are finalized — handwritten on construction paper or typed and printed — for the Hero Codex portfolio. | Brief template, construction paper, markers, glue |
| Closing | Exit Ticket: *"What did your peer reviewer help you notice that you wouldn't have caught yourself?"* + *"What's the cost of your hero's power? Every real thing in nature has one."* | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |

### Differentiation Considerations:
- Provide TAG sentence stems on the handout: *"I liked how you ___."* / *"What did you mean by ___?"* / *"Have you tried ___?"*
- Pair students intentionally — strong writers with students who need support.
- Allow students to revise verbally with a teacher before writing the final version.
- Advanced extension: students present their Brief to the class in a 1-minute "Shark Tank" style pitch.
- Celebrate completion — display Briefs on a science board, or share with the ELA/Math/Social Studies teachers as part of the cross-curricular portfolio.

- **SP7** — Engaging in argument from evidence.

**Common Core Standards ELA**
