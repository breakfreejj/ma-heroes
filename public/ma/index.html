The Big Idea

Superheroes are the door. The real curriculum walks through it.

Every iconic hero starts from loss, isolation, or being misunderstood — and chooses what to do with the circumstances they didn't ask for. Origin Story turns that arc into a four-subject project where students don't just study heroes; they design one of their own, with the math, science, writing, and civic mission to back it up.

What Students Learn

10 lessons per subject. One artifact each. All four feed the pitch.

Every mini-lesson follows the BreakFree-style structure: a short Do Now, a teacher-led mini-lesson with worked examples, guided pair practice, independent application, and a reflective exit ticket. Each lesson is differentiated for wide skill ranges and aligned to Massachusetts standards. By Day 10 of each subject, students walk away with one polished artifact for their final pitch portfolio.

Mathematics

A 10-lesson deep dive into proportional reasoning, applied to superhero design.

View Full Lesson Plans →
10-Day Arc
A focused deep dive into proportional reasoning — ratios, proportions, percentages, and rates — all applied to superhero design. Each day introduces one new skill while reinforcing the previous days, so a student who masters Day 1's "ratio as X out of Y" can ride the arc all the way to Day 10's Hero Stat Sheet. By the artifact, students mathematically justify why their hero stands up in a fight.
Lesson Map
1The Hero Power Index — ratios in three forms
Do Now
Rank these 4 heroes 1–4 on strength using your gut. Defend your #1.
Mini Lesson
Define a ratio in three equivalent forms — X/Y, X:Y, "X out of Y." Marvel's 5 hero stats on a 1–7 scale; every stat is naturally a ratio.
Activity
Sum each hero's 5 stats as a ratio over 35 — the Hero Power Index. Rank 6 heroes by Index. Does the ranking match intuition?
2Normalizing & ranking — ratio to percentage
Do Now
If Hero A has 70 points out of 100 and Hero B has 14 points out of 20, who scored higher?
Mini Lesson
Convert any ratio to a percentage (multiply by 100, or solve the proportion X/Y = ?/100). Percentages let you compare across different scales.
Activity
Convert Marvel 1–7 stats AND a competing 1–10 universe's stats to percentages, then re-rank. Did anyone's rank change?
3Hero speeds — rate as ratio
Do Now
Miles can swing 30 miles in 1 hour. How far in 2 hours? In 15 minutes?
Mini Lesson
A rate is a ratio with different units (mph = miles/hour). Unit rates. Converting between rate units — mph, km/h, ft/sec, m/s.
Activity
5 heroes' speeds in 5 different units. Convert all to a common unit (mph). Rank from fastest to slowest.
4The Catch-Up — solving proportions
Do Now
If Miles swings 30 miles in 1 hour, how long for 75 miles? Set it up as a proportion before solving.
Mini Lesson
Define a proportion (a/b = c/d). Two solving methods — cross-multiplication and scaling. Walk through a worked catch-up problem.
Activity
3 catch-up problems of increasing difficulty. Set up the proportion AND solve. Verify units and reasonableness.
5Scale & geometry — blueprints and dilation
Do Now
Ant-Man is 6 ft tall and shrinks to ½ inch. By what factor did he shrink?
Mini Lesson
Scale factor as proportion (1:48 means 1 model unit = 48 real units). Brief tease: linear scale k → area k² → volume k³ (Science Day 6 callback).
Activity
Design a piece of hero tech. Given real-world dimensions, calculate the model size at 1:24 and 1:48 scale. Sketch with labels.
6The Web Swing — percent change & comparing routes
Do Now
Miles can swing the long way in 5 minutes; the short way (traffic) takes 7. By what % does the short way take longer?
Mini Lesson
Percent change formula: (new − old)/old × 100%. The denominator is ALWAYS the original value — the most common mistake to avoid.
Activity
Compare two Brooklyn routes (different distances, different speeds). Calculate the percent of time saved by the faster route.
7Hero win rates — comparing ratios with unlike denominators
Do Now
Hero A has 18 wins out of 25 fights. Hero B has 22 wins out of 30. Who's better — and how do you know?
Mini Lesson
Why direct comparison of wins fails. Two methods: convert both to percentages, OR find a common denominator.
Activity
5 heroes with different W/L records (all unlike denominators). Convert each to a percent. Rank by win rate.
8Hero gear budget — parts of a whole
Do Now
If Miles's hero budget is $1,000 and he spends 25% on his costume, how many dollars is that?
Mini Lesson
Percentages of a whole must sum to exactly 100%. Walk through a 5-category budget allocation; discuss the trade-offs when one category grows.
Activity
Design a hero gear budget across 5 categories. Percentages must sum to 100%; calculate dollar amounts; justify the top allocation.
9Cost of heroics — % of large numbers
Do Now
Estimate the cost to replace a skyscraper window. To replace a city block?
Mini Lesson
Percentages work the same on $5K as on $5M. Real Deloitte / Kinetic Analysis estimates of Avengers movie property damage. Estimation matters.
Activity
Calculate total damage cost for a battle scene. Express as % of a city's reconstruction budget. Who pays in real life?
10Build Your Hero's Stat Sheet — artifact
Do Now
Re-read your Day 1 notes. What two stats matter most for your hero's mission? Why?
Mini Lesson
Walk through the Hero Stat Sheet template — 5 sections: stats (ratios + %), speed (rate), win rate (%), gear budget (%), battle cost (%). Each section needs a 1-sentence justification.
Activity
Students complete the Math One-Pager for their original hero. Goes into the pitch portfolio.

Science

Could this power actually work? Real science behind fictional abilities.

View Full Lesson Plans →
10-Day Arc
Students move from sorting fiction by plausibility to explaining real biology, physics, chemistry, and engineering. By the final lesson, they can write a Science Brief justifying how their hero's power could theoretically exist — and what it would cost the body.
Lesson Map
1What makes a power "plausible"? — hypothesis basics
Do Now
List 3 superpowers. Star the one you think is most scientifically possible. Why?
Mini Lesson
Intro to scientific plausibility — hypothesis, evidence, mechanism. Real vs. fictional.
Activity
Sort 10 superpower cards into "Could Happen / Far Off / Impossible" with one-sentence reasoning each.
2Genetics & mutation — the X-gene
Do Now
Why do some siblings look so different?
Mini Lesson
Genes, DNA, mutation. X-Men as the "mutation" hero family. Real mutations (lactose tolerance, sickle cell trait) — mutation isn't always bad.
Activity
Students design a "mutation card": which gene mutated, what changed, is it helpful or harmful?
3Forces & motion — Newton's three laws
Do Now
Push your textbook. Now push it twice as hard. What changes?
Mini Lesson
Newton's 3 laws using hero examples (Miles's web pulls him = equal/opposite reaction).
Activity
Match 6 hero moments to the correct Newton's law and explain why.
4Energy & work — joules in a punch vs. a lightning bolt
Do Now
Which has more energy: Storm's lightning bolt or a thrown brick?
Mini Lesson
Kinetic vs. potential energy; conservation of energy. Real numbers — joules in a lightning bolt vs. a punch.
Activity
Energy ranking task: order 6 hero attacks by joules. Justify with the formulas at a conceptual level.
5Materials science — Vibranium and its real cousins
Do Now
Name 3 things stronger than human bone.
Mini Lesson
Real materials science — what makes Kevlar, titanium, carbon nanotubes special. Shuri's Vibranium as a "thought experiment" version of these.
Activity
Students design a fictional material with 3 properties (e.g., flexible, fireproof, lightweight) — what real-world problem could it solve?
6The biology of strength & speed — square-cube law
Do Now
Could a human really lift a car? Why or why not?
Mini Lesson
Square-cube law (callback to Math Day 5), muscle physiology, biomechanics. Why scaling a body up breaks biology.
Activity
Students calculate or estimate: if Hulk is 8 ft tall and weighs 1,000 lb, what would his actual heart need to do? (Order-of-magnitude OK.)
7Weather & atmosphere — Storm's science
Do Now
Why does lightning happen?
Mini Lesson
Atmospheric layers, weather formation, electric charge separation. Storm's powers as Earth science lesson.
Activity
Storm wants to make it rain over a drought-stricken farm. Students diagram what actually needs to happen in the atmosphere for that rain.
8Chemistry of super-serums — intro to pharmacology
Do Now
What's a medicine you've taken? What did it do in your body?
Mini Lesson
Basic pharmacology — how molecules interact with receptors. Captain America's serum as a "what if?" version.
Activity
Students design a serum: pick 1 enhancement (strength, healing, focus) and describe what system in the body it would have to target, plus 1 realistic side effect.
9Engineering design — Shuri's lab
Do Now
Sketch a gadget that would help someone you care about in their daily life.
Mini Lesson
Engineering design cycle (problem → constraints → prototype → test). Shuri and Iron Man as engineers, not just powered heroes.
Activity
Mini design sprint — students sketch a hero gadget with labeled parts and a one-sentence "what problem it solves."
10Power Science Brief — artifact
Do Now
What's your hero's #1 power? Write one sentence on how it might work.
Mini Lesson
Review the science toolkit (genetics, forces, energy, materials, biology, chemistry, engineering). Students pick which one(s) explain their power.
Activity
Students complete the Power Science Brief — 1 page explaining their hero's power with real science vocabulary. Into the portfolio.

English / Language Arts

The Hero's Journey as a spine for reading, writing, and identity.

View Full Lesson Plans →
10-Day Arc
Joseph Campbell's Hero's Journey as the literary spine. Students study the pattern across a wide range of superhero stories — anchor heroes (Miles, Shuri, Storm, Kamala), Marvel mainstays (Peter Parker, T'Challa, Aunt May, Uncle Ben), and a public-domain Golden Age anchor text (Captain Marvel / Shazam in Whiz Comics #2). They map heroes onto the 8 stages, draft scenes one stage at a time, and finish with a polished 1–2 page Origin Story. Aligned to Massachusetts ELA 9–10 standards.
Lesson Map
1What is a hero? — defining heroism
Do Now
Write the name of one real-life hero (NOT fictional). Three sentences: who, what they did, why it counts as heroic to you.
Mini Lesson
Three working definitions side by side — Greek root hērōs, Merriam-Webster, Maya Angelou. What's the difference between famous, powerful, and heroic?
Activity
Paired Top-5 hero traits ranking. Then a 5-sentence Real-Life Hero Profile using at least 2 of those traits.
2The Hero's Journey — 8 stages mapped
Do Now
Think of a story where the main character ends up TOTALLY different from how they started. 3–4 sentences on the before and after.
Mini Lesson
8-stage Hero's Journey wheel + the TED-Ed lesson "What Makes a Hero?" (Matthew Winkler, ~4 min, free).
Activity
Class maps Spider-Man (or Miles Morales) onto all 8 stages. Then students independently map a superhero of their choice.
3Ordinary World & Call to Adventure
Do Now
Describe your OWN ordinary morning in 3 sentences. Make it boring on purpose — name three specific things.
Mini Lesson
Read the opening pages of Whiz Comics #2 (Captain Marvel / Shazam origin, public domain) — Billy Batson's ordinary world on the streets. Concrete sensory details make a memorable opening.
Activity
Concrete-Detail Hunt in the comic, then draft your hero's Ordinary World in 5–7 sentences using a required-detail checklist.
4Refusal, Mentor, Crossing the Threshold
Do Now
Name ONE person who taught you something that stuck with you. Two sentences.
Mini Lesson
Three superhero mentor types: the wizard Shazam (Whiz Comics #2), Uncle Ben & Aunt May, Professor X. Refusal → Mentor → Threshold compressed in one comic sequence.
Activity
Mentor Trait Carousel with 6 superhero mentors. Then draft a Mentor Profile + Threshold Scene for your hero.
5Trials, Allies, Enemies — the supporting cast
Do Now
Label 3 people in your life: one ally, one enemy, one COMPLICATED. One word each.
Mini Lesson
Supporting characters reveal the hero. The "complicated person" is the key — Miles + Uncle Aaron (Prowler), Peter + Harry Osborn, T'Challa + Killmonger.
Activity
Lightning Round on 6 ally/enemy/complicated scenarios. Then design your hero's 3-person supporting cast.
6The Ordeal — loss as transformation
Do Now
Think of a superhero who LOSES BIG at some point. What did they lose? Did it change them?
Mini Lesson
Two superhero ordeals (teacher-narrated): Storm's Cairo origin; Peter Parker losing Uncle Ben. Then read a curriculum-written Ordeal Exemplar as a model for student writing.
Activity
Three-color annotation of the Ordeal Exemplar (external threat / emotional cost / internal shift). Then draft your hero's lowest moment in 5–8 sentences.
7Return & the Gift — heroism as service
Do Now
If you could give ONE GIFT to your community — not a thing, a piece of knowledge, a skill, or a change — what would it be?
Mini Lesson
Four superhero "Returns" — Miles Morales (Brooklyn), T'Challa (Wakanda → world), Kamala Khan (Jersey City), Billy Batson / Captain Marvel (kids).
Activity
Gift Carousel of 4 superhero profiles. Then draft your hero's gift: what they bring back, who needs it, how they share it.
8Graphic-novel literacy — panels, gutters, framing
Do Now
Look at the projected comic page (Whiz Comics #2). Without instruction yet: what do you "read" first — image or words?
Mini Lesson
Comics vocabulary — panel, gutter, framing, speech vs. thought vs. narration boxes. Anchor text: the wizard-Shazam transformation sequence (public domain).
Activity
Storyboard one scene from your hero's origin in 4 panels (stick figures fine — the point is panel CHOICES).
9Drafting the full Origin Story
Do Now
Pull out your Day 3, 4, 6, 7 drafts. Underline ONE sentence you're proud of.
Mini Lesson
Read a curriculum-written Model Origin Story; identify each Hero's Journey stage. Bridge sentences as the glue between scenes.
Activity
Bridge Sentence Mini-Drill (4 disconnected scene pairs), then draft your full 1–2 page Origin Story (300–500 words) in class.
10Revision & peer feedback — artifact
Do Now
Re-read your draft from yesterday. What's one sentence a reader would tell you needs work?
Mini Lesson
2 Stars and 1 Wish peer-feedback protocol — specific, low-threat, repeatable. Teacher models on a sample paragraph.
Activity
Peer feedback exchange. Then a revision pass making THREE deliberate changes (each marked with a margin star). Final Origin Story → pitch portfolio.

Social Studies

Heroes across cultures, history, and the civic life of a community.

View Full Lesson Plans →
10-Day Arc
Students examine global hero traditions, decode comic-book history as primary-source material, and wrestle with questions of power, oversight, and ethics. By Day 10 they articulate a real civic mission for their own hero, rooted in a real community problem.
Lesson Map
1Heroes across cultures — global mythology
Do Now
Name a hero from a story your family or culture tells.
Mini Lesson
Global hero traditions — Anansi (West African), Sun Wukong (Chinese), La Llorona / Quetzalcoatl (Mesoamerican), Indigenous trickster figures. Heroes don't all look the same.
Activity
Each student picks one global hero from a provided set and writes a 3-fact summary.
2American comic origins & WWII propaganda
Do Now
Why do you think Captain America wears the flag?
Mini Lesson
Captain America #1 (1941) punched Hitler before the U.S. entered WWII. Comics as propaganda and morale-building. Primary source analysis.
Activity
Students compare two WWII comic covers and identify the persuasive techniques used.
3Civil rights allegory — the X-Men
Do Now
What does it mean to be "feared for what you are"?
Mini Lesson
X-Men as a 1960s civil rights allegory. Stan Lee's own words on it. Storm as a Black female leader of the team.
Activity
Students annotate a short X-Men excerpt for civil-rights parallels.
4Two paths, same pain — Magneto & Killmonger
Do Now
Can a villain be "right" about something?
Mini Lesson
Magneto (Holocaust survivor) vs. Professor X. Killmonger (raised in Oakland, witnessed injustice) vs. T'Challa. Same wound, different response.
Activity
Students fill out a T-chart for one pair — what they agree on, where they split.
5Government & oversight — the Civil War debate
Do Now
Should heroes have to follow government rules?
Mini Lesson
The Sokovia Accords / Civil War storyline as a real lesson on accountability, due process, individual rights vs. collective safety.
Activity
Structured debate — half the room argues for oversight, half against. Switch sides after 5 min.
6Community heroes — profiling someone real
Do Now
Who in your life or community is a hero, and why?
Mini Lesson
Heroism without a cape — teachers, organizers, elders, mentors. Read a short profile of a real community hero.
Activity
Students draft a 1-paragraph profile of someone they know who deserves the title "hero."
7Culture & economics — the Black Panther moment
Do Now
Have you seen a movie that made you feel proud of who you are? Which one?
Mini Lesson
Black Panther (2018) — cultural impact, box office, representation. Why representation in media matters as a civic issue.
Activity
Students read 2 short data points (box office, audience demographics) and write a 3-sentence response to "why did this matter?"
8Ethics of power — dilemmas and frameworks
Do Now
"With great power comes great responsibility." Agree, disagree, or it depends?
Mini Lesson
Ethical frameworks at a kid level — duty, consequences, character. Real-world parallels (whistleblowers, civic leaders).
Activity
3 ethical dilemma cards — students choose a stance and defend it in writing.
9Mapping your hero's community
Do Now
Where is your hero from? What's that place like?
Mini Lesson
Place matters — Miles in Brooklyn, Shuri in Wakanda, Kamala in Jersey City. Setting shapes mission.
Activity
Students sketch a "map" of their hero's community — who lives there, what problems exist, what's worth protecting.
10Civic Mission One-Pager — artifact
Do Now
What's one real problem in a community you care about?
Mini Lesson
A hero's mission is rooted in a real problem. Connect their power (Science), backstory (ELA), and resources (Math) to a civic mission.
Activity
Students complete the Civic Mission One-Pager — the real-world problem, why their hero is the one to address it, and what action they take. Into the portfolio.
The Culminating Event

The Hero Pitch.

Every student becomes the founder, scientist, writer, and citizen behind an original hero — and pitches them, Shark-Tank-style, to a panel of classmates, teachers, or invited judges.

The Pitch Portfolio

Each of the four subject-area lessons produces a finished artifact. Together, they are the pitch — math proves the hero is plausible, science explains the power, ELA tells the story, and social studies grounds the mission in the real world.

MATH
Hero Stat Sheet & Battle Analysis — stats, equations, and probability.
SCIENCE
Power Science Brief — real science behind the hero's ability.
ELA
Origin Story — 1–2 page narrative built on the Hero's Journey.
SOC ST
Civic Mission One-Pager — the real community problem the hero takes on.

The Competition

A 3–5 minute live pitch per student. Each classroom selects a winner. A school-level final brings together classroom champions.

  • Original hero with name, look, and identity
  • Live presentation using all four artifacts
  • Judged on craft of each piece and integration across subjects
  • Classroom & school-level winners recognized
  • Optional: showcase booklet of all finalists
Why This Audience, Why This Theme

Origin stories aren't just a literary device for our students. They're personal.

We built Origin Story for the realities of secure classrooms. The theme is engaging on its own — but it's the emotional throughline that makes it land.

01

Identity & Second Chances

Every hero is defined by what they did with circumstances they didn't choose. That frame meets our students where they are.

02

Representation That Matters

Anchor heroes were chosen for diversity in race, gender, and background — students see themselves in the curriculum, not just at the margins of it.

03

Authorship as Engagement

Students aren't analyzing someone else's hero. They're building their own. The competition gives them a reason to make it good.
