# Origin Story — Social Studies Lesson Plans

**10 mini-lessons · 30–45 min each**

Each lesson follows the BreakFree-style structure:
- **Do Now / Bellringer** (~3 min) — short hook prompt anchored in a primary source, image, or personal reflection
- **Mini Lesson** (~10–12 min) — teacher explains content, often by walking students through a historical example or framework
- **Guided Practice** — class or pair work with teacher support (jigsaws, document analysis, structured discussion)
- **Independent Practice** — students apply on their own (worksheets, charts, short writing)
- **Closing** — Exit Ticket with reflective stem

Each lesson includes a **Differentiation Considerations** block and is aligned to **Common Core ELA literacy standards for History/Social Studies (RH)** and the **C3 Framework for Social Studies**. Audience: 12–19 year olds in juvenile justice classrooms; expect wide ranges of prior knowledge and lived experience that intersects directly with this content — handle with care.

---

## SOCIAL STUDIES — 10 Mini-Lessons

**Arc:** *Who counts as a hero — and who decides?* Across 10 lessons, students examine heroism across cultures, eras, and civic life. The arc moves global → national → local → personal, ending with each student writing a Civic Mission One-Pager that grounds their fictional hero in a real community problem.

**Skill progression:** Day 1 global heroes (jigsaw) → Day 2 cultural lineage tracing → Day 3 American comic origins as propaganda → Day 4 civil rights allegory (X-Men) → Day 5 villain analysis (Magneto, Killmonger) → Day 6 framing & primary sources (Harriet Tubman triangulation) → Day 7 Massachusetts heroes → Day 8 ethics of power → Day 9 Civic Mission drafting → Day 10 peer review and finalize.

**Final artifact for the Hero Pitch:** Civic Mission One-Pager.

### Day 1 — Heroes Across Cultures

**Lesson objectives:**
- Identify defining traits of three hero figures from three different cultures.
- Articulate a claim about what each culture *valued* based on its hero stories.
- Recognize that the label "hero" depends on whose story is being told.

**Assessment:**
- Hero Comparison Organizer
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- Every culture invents the heroes it needs. The traits of a culture's heroes tell you what that culture valued: wit, strength, cleverness, survival under power imbalance, courage, devotion.
- Students examine six heroes from world traditions: **Anansi** (West Africa), **Sun Wukong** (China), **Maui** (Polynesia), **Gilgamesh** (Mesopotamia), **Cú Chulainn** (Ireland), **Mulan** (China).
- Critical idea: Anansi the trickster is a *hero* in West African tradition. In an American comic, he'd be coded as a *villain*. **The label depends on who's holding the pen.** This seeds the framing question that runs across the whole unit.

| Component | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| **Do Now** | "Most American superheroes are strong, fast, and fight crime. List two — then write: what does it say about America that those are our heroes?" Two volunteers share. | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |
| **Introduction of Origin Story** | Introduce the social studies unit: across 10 lessons, students will examine who counts as a hero across cultures and history — and by Lesson 10, write a Civic Mission One-Pager for their own hero. | Slides |
| **Mini Lesson** | Framing: *every culture invents the heroes it needs.* Walk through one hero together — **Anansi the Spider** (West African trickster). Powers: cleverness, deception. Flaws: arrogance, greed. *What did West African societies value?* — wit, survival under power imbalance, the right of the small to outsmart the big. Key idea: Anansi is a *hero* in his tradition. In an American comic, he'd be coded as a villain. **The label depends on who's holding the pen.** | Slides |
| **Guided Practice** | **Jigsaw activity (20 min).** Class splits into 5 "home groups." Each group gets 1 of the 5 remaining hero cards (Sun Wukong, Maui, Gilgamesh, Cú Chulainn, Mulan). They fill out their row of the graphic organizer (Hero / Powers / Flaws / What Culture Valued). Then groups reshuffle into "expert groups" (one from each home group meets) and share — everyone fills in all 6 rows. | Slides; Hero Card Set (6 cards); Graphic Organizer handout |
| **Closing** | **Exit Ticket** — "Pick one hero from today. Name one thing their culture valued. Then write: do you think your community values the same thing? Why or why not?" | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |

**Differentiation Considerations:**
- Replace one hero card with a shorter version for students who need less reading.
- Allow drawing in place of writing in the organizer cells.
- Group seating — keep mixed-ability home groups.
- Sentence frame for exit ticket: *"___'s culture valued ___. My community does/does not value the same because ___."*
- Advanced extension: students add a 4th column — *what would this hero be called today by a court or the news?*

**Standards (Common Core ELA + C3 Framework for Social Studies):**
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RH.6-8.2** — Determine the central ideas or information of a primary or secondary source.
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.6-12.1** — Engage effectively in a range of collaborative discussions.
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.W.6-12.10** — Write routinely over extended and shorter time frames.
- **D2.His.2.6-8** — Classify series of historical events and developments as examples of change and/or continuity.
- **D2.Geo.6.6-8** — Explain how the physical and human characteristics of places and regions are connected to human identities and cultures.

### Day 2 — Cultural Lineage: Which Traditions Feed Your Hero

**Lesson objectives:**
- Identify 2–3 hero traditions a fictional hero descends from.
- Explain what was inherited (and what was rejected) from each tradition.
- Connect a personal/community hero figure to a broader cultural lineage.

**Assessment:**
- Cultural Lineage Worksheet
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- Today builds directly on Day 1: now that students know what different cultures' heroes look like, they trace which traditions feed into the heroes they admire — and the hero they themselves will design.
- Critical concept: claiming a lineage is **not unoriginal**. It's how stories get power. Spider-Man is a Greek-style "reluctant chosen one." Black Panther stands in a lineage of African kings and warrior traditions. Miles Morales is in the lineage of Spider-Man + the Latino and Black neighborhoods of Brooklyn.
- Students complete a Cultural Lineage worksheet that will feed into the Hero Codex portfolio.

| Component | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| **Do Now** | "Name a modern hero (fictional or real). Where did they come from? Who came before them?" | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |
| **Mini Lesson** | Introduce the concept of **lineage**. Every hero stands on the shoulders of older ones. Walk through 2 examples on the board: **Miles Morales** — lineage includes Spider-Man (chosen-one hero), the Afro-Latino community in Brooklyn (real-world setting), the Marvel tradition of teen heroes. **Black Panther** — lineage includes Wakandan/African kings, Afrofuturism (Sun Ra, Octavia Butler), real liberation movements. Key idea: claiming a lineage is not unoriginal. It's how stories get power. | Slides |
| **Guided Practice** | As a class, brainstorm one collective hero (real or fictional) on chart paper. Map their lineage in 3 directions: (1) Which older heroes did they descend from? (2) What real-world tradition do they reflect? (3) What did they leave behind? | Slides; chart paper |
| **Independent Practice** | **Cultural Lineage Worksheet.** Students complete a 1-page worksheet for their own hero (the one they're designing for the Hero Codex): (1) Which 2–3 hero traditions does my hero descend from? (2) What did I take from each? (3) What did I leave behind? (4) Sketch a "family tree" of the lineage. | Cultural Lineage Worksheet; colored pencils |
| **Closing** | **Exit Ticket** — "Whose story does your hero make sure is not forgotten?" | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |

**Differentiation Considerations:**
- Sentence frame: *"I took ___ from ___."*
- Allow students to draw the lineage rather than write it.
- Provide a list of hero traditions to choose from (cultural, comic-book, historical) for students who need scaffolding.
- Pairs allowed for the worksheet to lower barrier.
- Advanced extension: require a lineage that crosses at least two continents or eras.

**Standards (Common Core ELA + C3 Framework for Social Studies):**
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.W.6-12.2** — Write informative/explanatory texts.
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RH.6-12.10** — Read and comprehend history/social studies texts.
- **D2.His.4.6-8** — Analyze multiple factors that influenced perspectives of people during different historical eras.
- **D2.Geo.6.6-8** — Explain how human characteristics of places are connected to identities and cultures.

### Day 3 — American Comic Origins & WWII: Heroes as Propaganda

**Lesson objectives:**
- Analyze how the demographics of mainstream American superheroes changed (and didn't) from 1938–2018.
- Identify persuasive techniques used in WWII-era comic propaganda.
- Make an evidence-based claim about why representation lagged.

**Assessment:**
- Timeline Investigation Handout
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- American superheroes did not appear in a vacuum. The first ones (Superman 1938, Captain America 1941, Wonder Woman 1942) were created in response to specific national moments — the Depression, WWII, and women entering the workforce.
- *Captain America #1* (March 1941) shows Cap punching Hitler **9 months before** the U.S. entered WWII. The comic was a piece of pro-war propaganda before the country was officially at war.
- It took **28 years** after Superman for the first Black mainstream superhero (Black Panther, 1966) — published the same year the Black Panther Party was founded.
- Students analyze comic covers in their historical context to see what each era was willing to imagine as heroic.

| Component | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| **Do Now** | "Name 5 American superheroes. Then circle the ones who are NOT white men." | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |
| **Mini Lesson** | Project comic covers in chronological order: 1938 Superman (white, male; an immigrant/refugee allegory — creators Jerry Siegel and Joe Shuster were Jewish); 1941 Captain America #1 punches Hitler 9 months *before* the U.S. enters WWII; 1942 Wonder Woman #1; 1961 Fantastic Four #1; 1966 Black Panther's first appearance — 28 years after Superman, same year the Black Panther Party is founded; 2011 Miles Morales / 2014 Kamala Khan. Key teaching point: *who gets to be a hero in print tells you who America was willing to imagine as heroic at that moment.* | Slides; printed/projected comic covers |
| **Guided Practice** | **Timeline Investigation (jigsaw, 20 min).** Groups of 4. Each group gets one cover and a half-page "context card" with: year, publisher, the main social events that year (1942 internment, 1966 Black Panther Party founded, etc.). Groups answer on chart paper: What does this hero represent? Who is missing from the cover? What was happening in America that year that explains this? Groups post their charts. **Gallery walk** (8 min) — students leave one post-it on each chart with a connection or question. | Slides; comic covers; context cards; chart paper; post-its |
| **Closing** | **Exit Ticket** — "Pick a hero from today. Write one sentence about who they let be a hero, and one sentence about who they erased." | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |

**Differentiation Considerations:**
- Provide a vocabulary key (representation, allegory, mainstream, propaganda) for students who need it.
- Allow students to mark on the cover image rather than write a separate response.
- Smaller jigsaw groups (3 instead of 4) work fine.
- Advanced extension: add a primary-source pairing — a 1940s editor's letter explaining why a certain hero "wouldn't sell."

**Standards (Common Core ELA + C3 Framework for Social Studies):**
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RH.6-8.6** — Identify aspects of a text that reveal an author's point of view or purpose.
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.6-12.1** — Engage effectively in a range of collaborative discussions.
- **D2.His.4.6-8** — Analyze multiple factors that influenced perspectives of people during different historical eras.
- **D2.Civ.2.6-8** — Explain how political, economic, and social contexts shape viewpoints.

### Day 4 — Civil Rights Allegory: X-Men and the 1960s

**Lesson objectives:**
- Explain how the X-Men comics functioned as an allegory for the 1960s civil rights movement.
- Compare two parallel responses to oppression (Professor X and Magneto).
- Identify how stories can carry political ideas without naming them directly.

**Assessment:**
- Annotated X-Men Excerpt
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- The X-Men debuted in 1963, in the middle of the civil rights movement. Stan Lee has said the comic was intentionally about prejudice.
- The mutants are feared and hated *for what they are* — not for what they do. They are forced to hide, to pass, to choose between integration and separation. The parallel to the 1960s is not subtle.
- Storm (Ororo Munroe), the team's longtime Black female leader, was introduced in 1975 and led the X-Men in the 1980s — an unusual position of authority for a Black woman in mainstream comics.
- This lesson sets up Day 5's deeper villain analysis (Magneto and Killmonger).

| Component | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| **Do Now** | "What does it mean to be 'feared for what you are' rather than 'what you've done'? Have you ever felt this way?" (Students may answer privately.) | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |
| **Mini Lesson** | Introduce the X-Men's 1963 origins: civil rights movement at its peak; mutants feared and hated *for what they are* — born different, unable to hide. **Professor X** = integration, working within the system (often compared to MLK). **Magneto** = self-defense, separation from a hostile system (sometimes compared to Malcolm X). **Storm** = a Black woman leader of the team since 1980 — rare in mainstream comics at that time. Key teaching point: stories can carry political ideas without naming them. The X-Men was about prejudice — but it was *also* a comic kids could pick up at the corner store. | Slides |
| **Guided Practice** | Read a short X-Men excerpt together as a class (1–2 pages, teacher-selected for civil-rights parallels). Annotate together: where do you see parallels to real history? Mark them in the margins. | Excerpt handout |
| **Independent Practice** | Students work in pairs to complete a T-chart on their handout: Left column: what's happening in the X-Men story. Right column: what real historical event/idea does it echo? Pairs share one row each with the class. | Handout |
| **Closing** | **Exit Ticket** — "Why might a writer choose to tell a real story through a made-up one?" | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |

**Differentiation Considerations:**
- Provide a shorter excerpt (1 page) and a pre-filled T-chart with 2 rows already done.
- Allow verbal annotation (popcorn reading + class discussion) instead of independent annotation.
- Pre-teach key vocabulary: allegory, prejudice, integration, separation.
- This lesson can surface real feelings about being labeled. Be ready with a plan for students who may need a break.
- Advanced extension: research the actual Stan Lee quotes about civil rights and the X-Men.

**Standards (Common Core ELA + C3 Framework for Social Studies):**
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RH.6-8.6** — Identify aspects of a text that reveal an author's point of view or purpose.
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RL.6-12.9** — Compare and contrast different texts that address similar themes or topics.
- **D2.His.4.6-8** — Analyze multiple factors that influenced perspectives of people during different historical eras.
- **D2.Civ.10.6-8** — Explain the impact of civic participation in communities.

### Day 5 — Two Paths, Same Pain: Magneto & Killmonger

**Lesson objectives:**
- Compare two "villain" characters who share a wound with a "hero" counterpart.
- Identify the real-world experiences each character is built on.
- Articulate where the hero and villain agree, and where they diverge.

**Assessment:**
- Two Paths T-Chart
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- A villain is not always wrong about the **problem**. Sometimes they are right about the problem but choose a path the story rejects. This lesson takes that seriously.
- **Magneto** is a Holocaust survivor. His core argument: humans will *always* turn on us; protect mutants first. Professor X agrees about the prejudice. They split on what to do about it.
- **Killmonger** is an American Black man raised in Oakland after his father was killed by Wakanda. His core argument: Wakanda has resources and ignored Black suffering globally. T'Challa agrees about the suffering. They split on the response (violence vs. reform).
- Critical lesson for justice-involved youth: the villain analysis is intentional. **Letting students take "wrong" characters seriously, then think critically, treats them as adults.** It also opens the question — what makes someone a hero vs. a villain when they're working from the same pain?

| Component | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| **Do Now** | "Can a villain be 'right' about something? Defend with one example from a movie, comic, or real life." | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |
| **Mini Lesson** | Walk through both pairs. **Magneto vs. Professor X** — Magneto: Holocaust survivor. *Humans will always turn on us.* Professor X: Agrees prejudice is real. *We choose understanding anyway.* Same wound (prejudice). Different response. **Killmonger vs. T'Challa** — Killmonger: Raised in Oakland after father killed by Wakanda. *Wakanda hoarded resources while Black people suffered.* T'Challa: Agrees Wakanda failed its responsibility. *We reform from within.* Same wound (injustice). Different response. Key teaching point: the villain isn't always wrong about the *problem*. The story often disagrees with the *response*. | Slides |
| **Guided Practice** | Class discussion: What does Magneto/Killmonger want? What part of their argument is hard to disagree with? What does the story say is wrong with their response? | Slides |
| **Independent Practice** | **Two Paths T-Chart.** In pairs, students pick one of the two pairs (Magneto/X or Killmonger/T'Challa) and fill in: What do they agree on? Where do they split? Who do you think is more right? Defend in 2 sentences. | Handout |
| **Closing** | **Exit Ticket** — "Have you ever met someone whose argument made sense to you but whose actions didn't? Explain in one sentence." | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |

**Differentiation Considerations:**
- Pre-watch clips from *X-Men* and *Black Panther* if students aren't familiar.
- Allow students to argue verbally before writing.
- Sentence frame: *"They agree that ___. They disagree on ___."*
- **Important for this audience:** this lesson can surface real anger and real history. Do not rush. Allow space for students to disagree with the story's verdict.
- Advanced extension: bring in a third pair (Loki/Thor, Catwoman/Batman) and let students apply the same framework.

**Standards (Common Core ELA + C3 Framework for Social Studies):**
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RL.6-12.3** — Analyze how complex characters develop, interact, and advance the plot or theme.
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.6-12.1** — Engage effectively in a range of collaborative discussions.
- **D2.Civ.7.6-8** — Apply civic virtues and democratic principles in school and community settings.
- **D2.His.16.6-8** — Organize applicable evidence into a coherent argument about the past.

### Day 6 — Outlaws, Folk Heroes, and Who Holds the Pen

**Lesson objectives:**
- Identify how the same person can be called a "hero" or "criminal" depending on the source.
- Analyze three primary sources about Harriet Tubman for bias and framing.
- Make an evidence-based claim about the relationship between law and morality.

**Assessment:**
- Source Triangulation Handout
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- Yesterday's villain analysis sets up a deeper question: who **decides** the label?
- Helping enslaved people escape was a *federal crime* under the Fugitive Slave Act of 1850 and a *heroic act* in the moral record. Both descriptions are historically accurate. **The difference is who's holding the pen.**
- Students examine three primary sources about Harriet Tubman from three different perspectives (slave-owner notice, Frederick Douglass's praise letter, modern textbook). They are not equivalent — but they are all part of the historical record.
- Critical question for justice-involved youth: *someone you admire was probably called a criminal by someone at some point. Who, and by whom?*

| Component | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| **Do Now** | "Who decides who is a criminal? Is it the same person who decides who is a hero?" | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |
| **Mini Lesson** | Introduce **framing** in history. Walk through Harriet Tubman: under the Fugitive Slave Act (1850), helping enslaved people escape was a federal crime. In the moral and historical record, Tubman is one of the greatest American heroes. **Both descriptions are historically accurate.** The difference is who's holding the pen, and what laws they accept as legitimate. Key teaching point: laws change. Morality also evolves. Sometimes they line up. Sometimes they don't. Heroes often live in that gap. | Slides |
| **Guided Practice** | Distribute three primary sources about Tubman. Read one aloud together. Annotate together for: Who wrote this? What words do they use (hero, criminal, agitator, conductor, fugitive)? What did they want the reader to do or feel? | Three Tubman primary source excerpts (handouts) |
| **Independent Practice** | **Source Triangulation Worksheet.** In pairs, students read the other two sources and complete a chart for each. Then they write a one-paragraph claim: *Was Harriet Tubman a criminal or a hero — and what's the right answer to that question?* | Source Triangulation Handout |
| **Closing** | **Exit Ticket** — "Someone you admire was probably called a criminal by someone at some point. Who, and by whom?" (Students may answer privately.) | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |

**Differentiation Considerations:**
- Pre-highlight key phrases in each source to guide attention.
- Sentence frames for the closing claim: *"Harriet Tubman was both ___ and ___ because ___."*
- Allow verbal response for students for whom the exit ticket question is sensitive.
- Add a 4th source for advanced students: a contemporary news article about someone framed similarly today (e.g., a whistleblower or organizer).

**Standards (Common Core ELA + C3 Framework for Social Studies):**
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RH.6-8.6** — Identify aspects of a text that reveal an author's point of view.
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RH.6-8.9** — Analyze the relationship between a primary and secondary source on the same topic.
- **D2.His.6.6-8** — Analyze how people's perspectives shaped historical narratives.
- **D2.Civ.5.6-8** — Explain the origins, functions, and structures of different government systems and laws.

### Day 7 — Massachusetts Heroes: Local Origin Stories

**Lesson objectives:**
- Identify three Massachusetts figures whose actions changed American history.
- Locate where in Massachusetts each person lived and worked.
- Argue which qualifies as a "hero" and on whose terms.

**Assessment:**
- Local Heroes Worksheet
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- The arc has gone global (Days 1–2), national (Days 3–6). Today it goes local.
- **Massachusetts has produced an outsized number of people who changed the rules of the country.** Crispus Attucks (Boston Massacre, 1770), Phillis Wheatley (first published African American poet, Boston, 1773), Mum Bett / Elizabeth Freeman (sued for her freedom in 1781 — helping end slavery in MA), William Lloyd Garrison (Boston abolitionist), Frederick Douglass's Boston years, and modern organizer Mel King.
- **Mum Bett's story is the centerpiece.** In 1781, an enslaved woman in Sheffield, MA sued for her freedom under the new Massachusetts constitution — and won. Her case helped end slavery in MA, decades before the Civil War. *Most students have never heard her name.*
- This lesson directly seeds the Civic Mission One-Pager (Days 9–10): if you're going to design a hero, knowing your *place* matters.

| Component | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| **Do Now** | "Name a hero from Massachusetts that isn't a Founding Father or a sports player." | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |
| **Mini Lesson** | Introduce the idea that **Massachusetts has produced an outsized number of people who changed the rules of the country.** Focus on Mum Bett: 1781. An enslaved woman in Sheffield, MA. Hears the new Massachusetts constitution read aloud: *"all men are born free and equal."* Sues her enslaver. **Wins.** Her case (*Brom and Bett v. Ashley*) helps end slavery in MA. Key teaching point: the first of the 13 colonies to functionally end slavery did so because of a Black woman who didn't take "no" for an answer. *Most students have never heard her name.* | Slides |
| **Guided Practice** | **Local Heroes Jigsaw (20 min).** Groups of 3. Each group gets one MA hero card: Crispus Attucks, Phillis Wheatley, Frederick Douglass's Boston years, William Lloyd Garrison, Mel King, Mum Bett / Elizabeth Freeman. Each group fills out: What did they do? Whose rules did they break? Whose lives did they change? Where in MA did they do it? Groups present 90-second summaries. Class places each hero on a shared MA map. | Slides; MA Hero card set (6 cards); Local Heroes Worksheet; large MA state map |
| **Independent Practice** | Each student writes 2–3 sentences answering: *which of these heroes do you think your community needs more of today, and why?* | Handout |
| **Closing** | **Exit Ticket** — "What part of Massachusetts history did your school skip? Take one guess about why." | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |

**Differentiation Considerations:**
- Shorten hero cards and provide guiding questions in the margins.
- Allow students to draw their hero's actions in panels rather than write a summary.
- Provide a Massachusetts map with pins for students to physically place the heroes.
- Advanced extension: add a contemporary MA hero (e.g., a local organizer the teacher trusts) and have students debate whether the person qualifies.
- Sentence frame for independent practice: *"My community needs more people like ___ because ___."*

**Standards (Common Core ELA + C3 Framework for Social Studies):**
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RH.6-8.2** — Determine central ideas of a primary or secondary source.
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.6-12.1** — Engage effectively in collaborative discussions.

- **D2.Civ.10.6-8** — Explain the impact of civic participation and recognition in communities.

### Day 8 — The Ethics of Power: "With Great Power…"

**Lesson objectives:**
- Identify three ethical frameworks for evaluating action (duty, consequences, character).
- Apply each framework to a real or fictional case.
- Take and defend a stance on a contested ethical dilemma.

**Assessment:**
- Ethical Dilemma Cards (worksheet)
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- This lesson translates the famous Spider-Man line ("with great power comes great responsibility") into actual ethical reasoning students can use.
- Three frameworks, in plain language: **Duty** — what is the right thing to do regardless of outcome? **Consequences** — what action produces the best result for the most people? **Character** — what kind of person do my choices make me into?
- Real-world parallels: whistleblowers, civic leaders, soldiers, parents under pressure.
- This lesson is the philosophical bridge between the "who is a hero?" arc (Days 1–7) and the Civic Mission (Days 9–10).

| Component | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| **Do Now** | "'With great power comes great responsibility.' Agree, disagree, or it depends? Defend in one sentence." | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |
| **Mini Lesson** | Introduce three ethical frameworks in plain language: (1) **Duty ethics** — what's the right thing to do, period? Even if it costs you. (Captain America turning in his shield rather than register.) (2) **Consequences ethics** — what produces the best result for the most people? (Sometimes called utilitarianism.) (3) **Character ethics** — what kind of person do my choices make me into? (Spider-Man choosing not to kill even when he could.) Real-world examples: a whistleblower exposing wrongdoing — duty-based or consequence-based? A teacher who breaks a school rule to help a student — character or consequences? | Slides |
| **Guided Practice** | Walk through one dilemma as a class on the board: *Your hero learns that a person who hurt their family is about to commit another crime. They can stop them with violence. Or they can call the police, who might not arrive in time.* What does each framework say? | Slides |
| **Independent Practice** | **Ethical Dilemma Cards.** Students get 3 dilemma cards. For each: (1) Identify the action options. (2) Apply each framework. (3) Choose a stance and defend it in 2–3 sentences. | Dilemma Cards handout |
| **Closing** | **Exit Ticket** — "Which framework feels closest to how you actually make decisions? Why?" | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |

**Differentiation Considerations:**
- Reduce to 2 dilemmas instead of 3 for students who need more time.
- Allow students to act out a dilemma in a 2-person scene if writing is hard.
- Sentence frame: *"Using ___ ethics, the right action is ___ because ___."*
- This lesson can surface real moral conflict for justice-involved youth. Be ready to handle disclosures with care.
- Advanced extension: research a real ethical case (the Pentagon Papers, the Snowden leaks, a local whistleblower case) and apply all three frameworks.

**Standards (Common Core ELA + C3 Framework for Social Studies):**
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.W.6-12.1** — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence.
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.6-12.1** — Engage effectively in collaborative discussions.
- **D2.Civ.7.6-8** — Apply civic virtues and democratic principles in school and community settings.
- **D2.Civ.10.6-8** — Explain the impact of civic participation in communities.

### Day 9 — Civic Mission: Drafting Day

**Lesson objectives:**
- Synthesize learning from Days 1–8 into a Civic Mission for an original hero.
- Identify a real community problem the hero is uniquely positioned to address.
- Connect the hero's lineage (Day 2), power (cross-curricular), and ethics (Day 8) to a single mission.

**Assessment:**
- Civic Mission One-Pager First Draft

**Key Points:**
- The Civic Mission One-Pager is the social studies capstone. It moves the hero from imagination into the real world — *what problem in a real community would this hero address, and why are they the one to do it?*
- The Mission requires four things: a **real community problem**, a clear **why this hero** statement, a **specific action** the hero takes, and a **link to the hero's lineage** (Day 2).
- This pairs with the **Community Mission** panel of the Hero Codex Trifold.

| Component | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| **Do Now** | "What's one real problem in a community you care about?" | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |
| **Mini Lesson** | Review the **social studies toolkit** from the past 8 lessons: (1) Heroes across cultures (Days 1–2); (2) American hero history (Days 3–5); (3) Framing & who-decides (Day 6); (4) Local Massachusetts heroes (Day 7); (5) Ethics of power (Day 8). Introduce the **Civic Mission One-Pager template**: Hero name; Community (real or imagined); The Real Problem (one sentence — be specific); Why This Hero (2–3 sentences linking power, lineage, and ethics); The Action (what does the hero actually do?); Connection to a real Massachusetts or community hero from this unit. Show one teacher exemplar. | Slides; Civic Mission template handout; teacher exemplar |
| **Independent Practice** | Students draft their Civic Mission One-Pager. Teacher circulates and conferences. Encourage students to pull from at least three of the eight lessons. | Template handout; science notebooks |
| **Closing** | **Exit Ticket** — "What real problem will your hero address? Why are they the right one for it?" | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |

**Differentiation Considerations:**
- Provide a fill-in-the-blank One-Pager template.
- Allow students to dictate to a partner who writes.
- Visual learners can include a labeled diagram or sketch of the community.
- Pair students who finish early with students who need support.
- Advanced extension: students name a real Massachusetts organization currently working on the problem they identified, and how the hero would collaborate (not replace).

**Standards (Common Core ELA + C3 Framework for Social Studies):**
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.W.6-12.2** — Write informative/explanatory texts.
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.W.6-12.4** — Produce clear and coherent writing.
- **D4.6.6-8** — Draw on multiple disciplinary lenses to analyze how a specific problem can manifest at local, regional, and global levels.
- **D4.7.6-8** — Assess their individual and collective capacities to take action and address problems.

### Day 10 — Civic Mission: Peer Review & Finalize

**Lesson objectives:**
- Give and receive structured peer feedback using the TAG protocol.
- Revise a Civic Mission draft based on feedback.
- Produce a final One-Pager for the Hero Codex portfolio.

**Assessment:**
- **Final product: Civic Mission One-Pager** (goes into the Hero Codex portfolio)
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- Today the drafts get peer-reviewed using the **TAG protocol** (Tell, Ask, Give), then revised, then finalized.
- The One-Pager joins the Hero Codex portfolio alongside the Origin Story (ELA), Stat Sheet (Math), Power Science Brief (Science), and Cultural Lineage. This is the final cross-curricular piece.
- The Civic Mission is the one panel of the Hero Codex Trifold that asks students to claim **agency, not just imagination.** Take it seriously.

| Component | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| **Do Now** | "Read your draft from yesterday. Underline your strongest sentence." | Yesterday's One-Pager draft |
| **Mini Lesson** | Introduce the **TAG peer review protocol**: **T**ell — one specific thing you liked (not "good job"). **A**sk — one clarifying question. **G**ive — one suggestion. Demonstrate live with a sample One-Pager. Show how to be specific. | Slides; Peer Review handout |
| **Guided Practice** | Students swap drafts and complete TAG feedback for their partner (10 min per draft). | Peer Review handout |
| **Independent Practice** | Students revise based on partner feedback. Teacher circulates and conferences. One-Pagers are finalized — handwritten on construction paper or typed and printed — for the Hero Codex portfolio. | Template handout; construction paper; markers; glue |
| **Closing** | **Exit Ticket** — "What's one real action you could take this week that's related to your hero's mission?" | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |

**Differentiation Considerations:**
- Provide TAG sentence stems: *"I liked how you ___."* / *"What did you mean by ___?"* / *"Have you tried ___?"*
- Pair students intentionally — strong writers with students who need support.
- Allow oral revision with a teacher before writing the final draft.
- Celebrate completion: display Civic Missions, present them at the Hero Codex showcase, or share with the ELA/Math/Science teachers.
- Advanced extension: students present their One-Pager as a 1-minute "Shark Tank" style pitch.

**Standards (Common Core ELA + C3 Framework for Social Studies):**
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.W.6-12.5** — Develop and strengthen writing by planning, revising, editing, rewriting.
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.6-12.1** — Engage effectively in collaborative discussions.
- **D4.6.6-8** — Use disciplinary and interdisciplinary lenses to analyze problems.
- **D4.7.6-8** — Assess capacities for individual and collective action.
- **D4.8.6-8** — Apply a range of deliberative and democratic strategies to make decisions and take action.
