# Superheroes: A 5-Week Cross-Curricular Initiative

**BreakFree-Ed · Summer Initiative**
Designed for Massachusetts juvenile justice schools · Grades 7–12 (ages 12–19)
MA Curriculum Framework-aligned · Trauma-informed · 50–55 minute class blocks

---

## The 5-Week Hero's Journey Arc

Use **Joseph Campbell's Hero's Journey** as the curriculum backbone. Each week is a stage, so every subject lines up around the same emotional/intellectual beat. Every Friday = a cross-curricular Codex Build Day where the subjects converge.

| Week | Hero's Journey Stage | Theme Question |
|------|----------------------|----------------|
| 1 | The Ordinary World & The Call | *Who counts as a hero? Who decides?* |
| 2 | Crossing the Threshold & Trials | *Where does power come from?* |
| 3 | The Abyss & Transformation | *What makes a hero different from a villain?* |
| 4 | **Allies, Mentors & The Team** *(new)* | *Why does no hero stand alone?* |
| 5 | Return with the Elixir | *How do I bring something back to my community?* |

---

## Capstone Product: The Hero Codex Trifold

A **cardboard trifold** (like a science-fair board, reframed as a hero artifact) that each student builds across the 5 weeks. Every Friday adds a panel section. By Week 5, the trifold is the centerpiece of a community showcase.

### Materials (all low-cost, no tech required)
- One cardboard trifold per student (~$3 each, or cut from large boxes)
- Construction paper, lined paper, printer paper
- Markers, colored pencils, glue sticks, scissors, tape
- **Optional tech:** phone camera + free QR code generator → links to a 60-second pitch video

### Six required components, mapped to each subject

| Panel Section | Subject | Writing Component |
|---|---|---|
| **Origin Story** (left panel, top) | ELA | 1-page narrative: the moment that made the hero |
| **Power Schematic** (left panel, bottom) | Science | Labeled diagram + 1-paragraph scientific explanation |
| **Stat Sheet & HQ Budget** (center, bottom) | Math | Numbers, ratios, budget table with reasoning |
| **Cultural Lineage** (right panel, top) | History | Short essay: which hero traditions this character descends from |
| **Hero Manifesto** (center, top — focal piece) | ELA / Civics | 5-sentence creed: what this hero stands for and refuses |
| **Community Mission** (right panel, bottom) | Civics / SEL | One real action the student commits to bringing back to their community |

### Why this product works for justice-involved youth
- **Physical and tangible** — takes up space, can't be lost in a notebook
- **Authorship is visible** — the trifold gets passed around at the showcase; students get to *be seen as the author*
- **The "Community Mission" section carries the most weight** — it asks them to claim agency, not just imagine it

---

## Lesson Plan Format

Each plan is built for a **50–55 minute block**:

| Segment | Time |
|---|---|
| Do Now | 5 min |
| Mini-lesson / direct instruction | 10–12 min |
| Activity | 25–30 min |
| Exit Ticket | 5 min |
| Codex check-in (Fridays only) | 3 min |

Grade band note: written for **grades 9–10** as the midpoint of the 12–19 age range, with differentiation notes for younger/older.

# WEEK 1 · The Ordinary World & The Call
**Theme question:** *Who counts as a hero? Who decides?*

## WEEK 1 · DAY 1 — ELA

### Title: What Is an Origin Story? Reading Miles Morales

**MA ELA Standards (Grades 9–10):**
- **RL.9-10.3** — Analyze how complex characters develop, interact, and advance the plot or theme.
- **RL.9-10.2** — Determine a theme and analyze its development.
- **W.9-10.3.a** — Engage and orient the reader by setting out a problem, situation, or observation.
- **SL.9-10.1** — Initiate and participate in collaborative discussions.

**Objectives (SWBAT):**
1. Identify three structural elements present in a superhero origin story.
2. Draft an opening paragraph of an original origin story that establishes setting, character, and an inciting moment.

**Materials:** Excerpt from *Miles Morales: Spider-Man* by Jason Reynolds (Chapter 1, ~3 pages) OR the first 8 pages of *Ms. Marvel #1* (Kamala Khan). Lined paper, pencils. Optional: short clip from *Into the Spider-Verse*, 0:00–3:00.

#### Do Now (5 min)
Posted on board: *"Name one person — real or fictional — you think is a hero. In one sentence, what's the moment that made them a hero?"*
Students write silently. Two volunteers share.

#### Mini-lesson (10 min)
Teacher introduces the **three pieces of an origin story**:
1. **The Ordinary World** — life before
2. **The Inciting Moment** — what broke the pattern
3. **The Choice** — what the character decided to do about it

Read aloud the first two pages of the chosen excerpt. Pause and annotate together: highlight where each of the three pieces appears.

#### Activity (30 min)
**Part A — Pair analysis (12 min):** Students finish reading the excerpt in pairs and label the three structural elements on their copy. Each pair writes a one-sentence answer to: *Whose perspective does this origin take seriously that other superhero stories ignore?*

**Part B — Origin draft (18 min):** Students draft the opening paragraph of *their own* origin story on lined paper. Required: at least one detail of the ordinary world, one inciting moment, no "powers" yet. Optional sentence starters posted on board:
- "Before everything changed, my days looked like…"
- "The day it started, I was…"

Teacher circulates. Saved in folder — this is **Codex draft #1** (Origin Story panel).

#### Exit Ticket (5 min)
> "If your origin story were being written by someone else — a reporter, a teacher, a judge — what would they get wrong? Write one sentence."

*This question is the lesson's real heart. It surfaces the central tension of the whole curriculum: who gets to define your story.*

#### Differentiation
- **Ages 12–13:** Provide a graphic organizer with three boxes (Ordinary / Inciting / Choice) instead of open paragraph.
- **Ages 17–19:** Add a second prompt — *whose origin story is missing from mainstream comics, and why?*

## WEEK 1 · DAY 1 — Math

### Title: Hero Stat Blocks: Ratios and Unit Reasoning

**MA Math Standards:**
- **7.RP.A.3** — Use proportional relationships to solve multi-step ratio and percent problems.
- **HSN.Q.A.1** — Use units as a way to understand problems; choose and interpret units consistently.
- **HSN.Q.A.3** — Choose a level of accuracy appropriate to limitations on measurement.

**Objectives (SWBAT):**
1. Convert between units of speed (mph, m/s, blocks per minute) accurately.
2. Use ratios to compare two heroes' relative abilities and justify which is "more powerful" based on a chosen metric.

**Materials:** Hero Stat Cards handout (printable — heroes with raw stats from canon), calculators (or phone calculators), lined paper. A Boston-area map photocopy is a great optional addition.

#### Do Now (5 min)
On board: *"The Flash can run 770 mph. The distance from the school to Fenway Park is about 4 miles. About how long would it take the Flash to get there?"*
Students attempt. One volunteer demonstrates.

#### Mini-lesson (12 min)
Teacher introduces **unit ratios** with one worked example: converting 770 mph into miles per second, then into "blocks per second" using a Boston block ≈ 1/20 mile.

Key teaching point: *raw numbers lie. The unit you choose changes the story.* Show how Spider-Man's swing speed (60 mph) sounds slow, but in NYC blocks per second, it's terrifying.

#### Activity (28 min)
**Hero Stat Battle.** Students get a sheet with 6 heroes and 4 stats each (top speed, lift strength in lbs, reaction time in ms, intelligence as IQ).

**Round 1 (10 min):** Each student picks two heroes and computes:
- The ratio of their top speeds
- How long each would take to cross the Mass Pike (138 miles)
- One stat where the "weaker-looking" hero actually wins, once converted

**Round 2 (15 min):** In pairs, students debate: *which of your two heroes is more powerful?* They must use at least two computed ratios as evidence. Pair shares with another pair (3 min).

**Codex prep:** Each student starts a **stat sheet for their own hero**, choosing 4 stats and assigning numbers with realistic units (saved to folder).

#### Exit Ticket (5 min)
> "Pick one stat for your own hero. Write the number AND the unit. Then write one sentence explaining why that unit matters more than the number."

#### Differentiation
- **Ages 12–13:** Restrict to whole-number ratios and provide a conversion chart.
- **Ages 17–19:** Add a stat with non-linear scaling (e.g., "strength doubles per cube of body mass") and ask them to derive the formula.

## WEEK 1 · DAY 1 — Science

### Title: What's in an Origin? DNA and the Science of Becoming

**MA STE Standards (High School Biology):**
- **HS-LS3-1** — Ask questions to clarify relationships about the role of DNA and chromosomes in coding the instructions for characteristic traits.
- **HS-LS3-2** — Make and defend a claim that inheritable genetic variations may result from new genetic combinations through meiosis, viable errors during replication, and/or mutations.
- **SP1** — Asking questions and defining problems.

**Objectives (SWBAT):**
1. Extract visible DNA from a strawberry using household materials and describe what they are seeing.
2. Explain in their own words how a mutation could (or could not) plausibly produce a superhero trait.

**Materials (per group of 3–4):** 1 strawberry, 1 zip-top bag, dish soap, table salt, rubbing alcohol (chilled in ice bucket), coffee filter, clear plastic cup, wooden stir stick or toothpick. Total cost ≈ $1.50/group.

#### Do Now (5 min)
On board: *"Spider-Man got his powers from a radioactive spider bite. Could that actually work? Yes / No / Maybe — and one sentence why."*
Students write. Quick hand-vote tally on board.

#### Mini-lesson (10 min)
Teacher introduces:
- DNA is in every cell. It's the instruction manual.
- **Mutation** = a change in those instructions. Most mutations do nothing. Some kill the cell. A few rare ones change a trait.
- Real example: **CCR5-delta32** mutation makes some people resistant to HIV — a real "superpower" caused by a real mutation.
- Today: pull DNA out of a strawberry so they can see it.

#### Activity (30 min)
**Strawberry DNA Extraction lab (20 min):**
1. Crush strawberry in bag with soap + salt (breaks cell membranes).
2. Filter into cup through coffee filter.
3. Slowly pour cold alcohol down the side. White stringy DNA precipitates at the boundary.
4. Lift DNA out with stir stick.

Each group sketches what they see in their science notebook and labels it.

**Lab Reflection (10 min):** In group, answer in notebook:
- What did you actually see? (observation, not interpretation)
- The strawberry had DNA. So do you. Name one trait of yours that the DNA codes for.
- *Codex prep:* List one realistic "power" that could plausibly come from a mutation. (This becomes the Power Schematic draft.)

**Safety note:** No flames near alcohol. Goggles if available. Pre-chill alcohol before class.

#### Exit Ticket (5 min)
> "True or false: A radioactive spider bite could rewrite your DNA to give you spider powers. Defend your answer in two sentences using something we did or said today."

#### Differentiation
- **Ages 12–13:** Provide a fill-in-the-blank lab notes sheet.
- **Ages 17–19:** Add a research extension — look up CRISPR and write a paragraph: *if we can edit DNA, who should decide which edits are allowed?*

## WEEK 1 · DAY 1 — History / Social Studies

### Title: Heroes Across Cultures: What a Culture's Heroes Tell You About the Culture

**MA History & Social Science Standards:**
- **HSS Practice Standard 3** — Organize information and data from multiple primary and secondary sources.
- **HSS Practice Standard 5** — Develop claims using evidence from multiple sources.
- **WHI.T1** — Analyze the impact of religion and culture in pre-modern world history.

**Objectives (SWBAT):**
1. Identify defining traits of three hero figures from three different cultures.
2. Articulate a claim about what each culture *valued* based on its hero stories.

**Materials:** Hero Card Set (printed) with one-paragraph summaries of: **Anansi** (West Africa), **Sun Wukong** (China), **Maui** (Polynesia), **Gilgamesh** (Mesopotamia), **Cú Chulainn** (Ireland), **Mulan** (China, historical/legendary). Graphic organizer with 4 columns: *Hero / Powers / Flaws / What Culture Valued.*

#### Do Now (5 min)
On board: *"Most American superheroes are strong, fast, and fight crime. List two — then write: what does it say about America that those are our heroes?"*
Two volunteers share. Don't moralize — just collect.

#### Mini-lesson (10 min)
Teacher's framing: *every culture invents the heroes it needs.* Show a chart on the board: 4 cultures, blank slots. Walk through one hero together — **Anansi the Spider** (West African trickster). Powers: cleverness, deception. Flaws: arrogance, greed. *What did West African societies value?* — wit, survival under power imbalance, the right of the small to outsmart the big.

Key teaching point: Anansi is a *hero* in his tradition. In an American comic, he'd be coded as a villain. **The label depends on who's holding the pen.** (Foreshadows Week 3.)

#### Activity (28 min)
**Jigsaw (20 min):** Class splits into 5 "home groups." Each group gets 1 of the remaining 5 hero cards. They have 8 minutes to fill out their row of the graphic organizer for that hero. Then groups reshuffle into "expert groups" (one from each home group meets) and share — 12 minutes for everyone to fill in all 6 rows.

**Quick discussion (8 min):** Whole class.
- Which hero would not work as a hero in a different culture? Why?
- *Codex prep:* Students mark which two heroes' traditions they want their own hero to descend from. (Seeds the Cultural Lineage panel.)

#### Exit Ticket (5 min)
> "Pick one hero from today. Name one thing their culture valued. Then write: do you think *your* community values the same thing? Why or why not?"

#### Differentiation
- **Ages 12–13:** Replace one hero card with a shorter version; allow drawing on the organizer.
- **Ages 17–19:** Add a 4th column — *what would this hero be called today by a court or the news?* Surfaces the framing-and-power theme early.

## WEEK 1 · DAY 2 — ELA

### Title: Voice in Origin Stories — Whose Voice Tells the Story?

**MA ELA Standards (9–10):**
- **RL.9-10.6** — Analyze how an author's choices regarding point of view create effects.
- **W.9-10.3.b** — Use narrative techniques (dialogue, pacing, description, reflection).
- **L.9-10.3** — Apply knowledge of language to understand how language functions in different contexts.

**Objectives (SWBAT):**
1. Identify how first-person voice shapes a reader's relationship to the narrator.
2. Revise their origin story draft to commit clearly to a point of view.

**Materials:** Excerpt from *Long Way Down* by Jason Reynolds (Will's opening monologue, ~2 pages) OR the first 4 pages of *American Born Chinese* by Gene Luen Yang. Each student's Day 1 origin draft.

#### Do Now (5 min)
On board: *"Rewrite this sentence in your own voice: 'It was the day everything changed.' Make it sound like you wrote it, not a textbook."*

#### Mini-lesson (10 min)
Read the *Long Way Down* opening aloud. Annotate together:
- Where does Will *break* standard English on purpose?
- What does that choice *do* to you as a reader?

Key idea: **voice is a power.** Writers who match how their community actually talks let readers in. Writers who flatten everyone's voice into the same "school English" lock people out.

#### Activity (30 min)
**Part A — Voice hunt (10 min):** In pairs, students find 3 places in the excerpt where the narrator's word choice, rhythm, or grammar would get marked wrong on a school essay — but make the writing *better*. Share out.

**Part B — Revision (20 min):** Students return to their Day 1 origin draft and revise it for voice. Required moves:
- At least one line of dialogue or internal speech in *their own* voice
- One sentence broken on purpose (fragment, run-on, repetition — used for rhythm)
- A clear "I" perspective

#### Exit Ticket (5 min)
> "If someone told you 'this isn't how you're supposed to write,' what would you say back? One sentence."

#### Differentiation
- **12–13:** Sentence-stem support: "I sound like myself when I say…"
- **17–19:** Add a second draft in *third-person* and reflect on which version is stronger and why.

## WEEK 1 · DAY 2 — Math

### Title: The Boston Hero Patrol

**MA Math Standards:**
- **7.RP.A.2** — Recognize and represent proportional relationships.
- **HSN.Q.A.1** — Use units to understand problems.
- **SMP.4** — Model with mathematics.

**Objectives (SWBAT):**
1. Calculate distance/time/speed problems involving real Boston-area locations.
2. Design a patrol route and justify it numerically.

**Materials:** Boston-area map (photocopied or hand-drawn — Dorchester, Roxbury, Mattapan, downtown, Cambridge). Ruler, calculator, lined paper.

#### Do Now (5 min)
On board: *"A hero needs to get from your school to the State House in 90 seconds. What's the minimum speed they need? Use any unit."*

#### Mini-lesson (12 min)
Walk through the **distance = rate × time** triangle. Then introduce a real-world wrinkle: heroes don't fly in straight lines — they have to follow streets, dodge buildings, account for traffic. Show: a "Manhattan distance" estimate (sum of N/S and E/W) vs. straight-line. The unit choice matters again.

#### Activity (28 min)
**Patrol Design Challenge.** Pairs receive a map with 5 marked locations across Boston (e.g., school, hospital, courthouse, Fenway, harbor). They must:
1. Measure straight-line distances between each pair of points (map scale provided).
2. Choose a top speed for their hero (in mph).
3. Calculate the time to reach every location from a central HQ.
4. Decide: *where should HQ be located to minimize the longest response time?*
5. Justify in 2–3 sentences using their numbers.

**Codex prep:** The chosen HQ location goes onto their Stat Sheet/Budget panel (Week 4 will revisit cost).

#### Exit Ticket (5 min)
> "Where did you put HQ, and what's the longest response time you'd accept before a hero is 'too late'? Defend with a number."

#### Differentiation
- **12–13:** Use only 3 locations and provide pre-measured distances.
- **17–19:** Add traffic — locations have different "response multipliers" for time of day.

## WEEK 1 · DAY 2 — Science

### Title: Punnett Squares & Inherited Powers

**MA STE Standards:**
- **HS-LS3-1** — Ask questions about the role of DNA in coding instructions for traits.
- **HS-LS3-3** — Apply concepts of statistics and probability to explain variation of traits.
- **SP5** — Using mathematics and computational thinking.

**Objectives (SWBAT):**
1. Construct a Punnett square to predict trait inheritance.
2. Use probability ratios to determine offspring trait likelihoods.

**Materials:** Two dice per pair, Punnett square worksheet, colored pencils.

#### Do Now (5 min)
On board: *"If one parent has super-strength (dominant) and the other does not (recessive), what's the chance their kid has super-strength?"*

#### Mini-lesson (12 min)
Quick refresh: **dominant vs. recessive alleles**, capital and lowercase letter notation. Build a Punnett square together for *Ss × ss* on the board. Read out ratios: 50% Ss (super), 50% ss (not).

Then complicate it: most "powers" wouldn't be single-gene. But Punnett squares are how we *start* thinking about who inherits what — and they're how scientists predict rare-trait inheritance in real medical genetics.

#### Activity (28 min)
**Hero Family Tree.** Each pair invents two hero parents with three traits each (e.g., flight, super-strength, telepathy). Decide for each trait whether it's dominant or recessive. Then:
1. Build three Punnett squares — one per trait.
2. Calculate the % chance their offspring inherits all three powers.
3. Roll dice to "simulate" the actual offspring's genotype.
4. Sketch the resulting child and label which traits they have.

**Codex prep:** Students jot down whether their hero's power is inherited, mutated, or environmental — feeds the Power Schematic.

#### Exit Ticket (5 min)
> "A trait can be inherited and *still* not show up. Explain why in one sentence."

#### Differentiation
- **12–13:** Provide pre-set parental genotypes.
- **17–19:** Add a second-trait dependency (e.g., flight only works *if* the strength gene is also present).

## WEEK 1 · DAY 2 — History

### Title: Who Made the Cut? The American Superhero

**MA HSS Standards:**
- **USII.T5** — Analyze the role of cultural change in 20th-century America.
- **Practice Standard 4** — Use evidence to argue a position.
- **Practice Standard 5** — Develop claims using evidence from multiple sources.

**Objectives (SWBAT):**
1. Analyze how the demographics of mainstream American superheroes changed (and didn't) from 1938–2018.
2. Make an evidence-based claim about *why* representation lagged.

**Materials:** Printed comic covers (or projected): *Action Comics #1* (1938 Superman), *Captain America #1* (1941), *Wonder Woman #1* (1942), *Fantastic Four #1* (1961), *Fantastic Four #52* (1966 — Black Panther's first), *Ms. Marvel #1* (2014 — Kamala Khan), *Miles Morales: Ultimate Spider-Man #1* (2011). Timeline handout.

#### Do Now (5 min)
On board: *"Name 5 American superheroes. Then circle the ones who are NOT white men."*

#### Mini-lesson (10 min)
Project the covers in chronological order. Walk through the dates:
- 1938: Superman (white, male, "alien" allegory for Jewish immigrants — the creators were Jewish)
- 1941: Captain America punches Hitler 9 months *before* the US enters WWII
- 1966: Black Panther is the first Black superhero in mainstream comics — **28 years** after Superman
- 2011: Miles Morales / 2014: Kamala Khan — mainstream lead heroes of color and Muslim heroes

Key teaching point: *who gets to be a hero in print tells you who America was willing to imagine as heroic at that moment.*

#### Activity (28 min)
**Timeline Investigation (jigsaw, 20 min):** Groups of 4. Each group gets one cover and a half-page "context card" with: year, publisher, the main social events that year (e.g., 1942: Japanese internment begins; 1966: Black Panther Party founded same year as the comic).

Groups answer on chart paper:
- What does this hero represent?
- Who is missing from the cover?
- What was happening in America that year that explains this?

Groups post their charts. Gallery walk (8 min) — students leave one post-it on each chart with a connection or question.

#### Exit Ticket (5 min)
> "Pick a hero from today. Write one sentence about who they *let* be a hero, and one sentence about who they *erased*."

#### Differentiation
- **12–13:** Provide a vocabulary key (representation, allegory, mainstream).
- **17–19:** Add a primary-source pairing — a 1940s editor's letter explaining why a certain hero "wouldn't sell."

## WEEK 1 · DAY 3 — ELA

### Title: Dialogue and Dialect — Speaking Your Truth

**MA ELA Standards:**
- **RL.9-10.4** — Determine word meaning, including connotative impact.
- **W.9-10.3.b** — Use dialogue, pacing, description.
- **L.9-10.3** — Apply knowledge of language.

**Objectives (SWBAT):**
1. Write a dialogue exchange that reveals character through word choice.
2. Distinguish between "dialect as authenticity" vs. "dialect as caricature."

**Materials:** Two short dialogue excerpts: one from *The Hate U Give* (Starr's home voice vs. school voice) and one example of stereotyped dialogue from an older comic. Lined paper.

#### Do Now (5 min)
On board: *"Write the same sentence — 'I'm not scared' — three ways: how you'd say it to a friend, to a teacher, and to a stranger you didn't trust."*

#### Mini-lesson (10 min)
Introduce **code-switching** — not as a deficit, but as a skill. Read the *Hate U Give* excerpt aloud. Then read the older stereotyped excerpt. Class identifies the difference: one writer wrote *with* the community, one wrote *at* it.

#### Activity (30 min)
**Hero Confrontation Scene.** Each student writes a 6–10 line dialogue between their hero and someone with authority over them (a teacher, a cop, a parent, a system). Requirements:
- Hero uses two different "voices" in the scene
- At least one line is in their authentic voice, not "translated"
- The exchange ends *without* the hero winning the argument — leave it real

**Codex prep:** The strongest line from this dialogue goes into the Origin Story panel as a callout quote.

#### Exit Ticket (5 min)
> "Who in your life makes you change how you talk? Why?"

#### Differentiation
- **12–13:** Provide a 4-line skeleton structure.
- **17–19:** Add a craft constraint: no dialogue tags ("he said," "she said") — voice has to do the work.

## WEEK 1 · DAY 3 — Math

### Title: Force, Strength, and Hercules's Labors

**MA Math Standards:**
- **HSN.Q.A.1** — Use units to understand problems.
- **HSA.CED.A.1** — Create equations in one variable.
- **SMP.4** — Model with mathematics.

**Objectives (SWBAT):**
1. Use F = ma to compute force in real-world units.
2. Evaluate the plausibility of a feat of strength using calculations.

**Materials:** Bathroom scale (one per group, optional), calculators, "Labors of Hercules" handout with weights and dimensions.

#### Do Now (5 min)
On board: *"A weightlifter lifts 500 lb over their head. A car weighs 4,000 lb. How many weightlifters would it take to hold up a car?"*

#### Mini-lesson (12 min)
Introduce **force = mass × acceleration**, units of newtons. Walk through one Hercules labor — the Nemean Lion (~600 lb). What force did Hercules need to choke it? Compare to: the world record bench press (~770 lb).

Key idea: heroes' feats are *interesting* exactly when we can measure them. "Super-strength" is meaningless without a number.

#### Activity (28 min)
**Strength Audit.** Each pair picks 3 famous hero feats (Hulk lifting a tank, Thor's hammer, Spider-Man stopping a train). For each, the pair:
- Looks up or estimates the real-world weight involved
- Computes the force required
- Compares to the strongest *real* human/animal capable of comparable force
- Writes a verdict: *plausible / requires breakthrough / impossible*

**Codex prep:** Students lock in their hero's strength stat — with a number and unit — and a written one-sentence justification.

#### Exit Ticket (5 min)
> "Your hero lifts X pounds. Defend the number — what real animal or person is the closest comparison?"

#### Differentiation
- **12–13:** Provide weights pre-converted to a single unit.
- **17–19:** Add scaling — what would your hero's strength be if they weighed half as much?

## WEEK 1 · DAY 3 — Science

### Title: Could a Real Person Develop Powers?

**MA STE Standards:**
- **HS-LS4-2** — Construct an explanation based on evidence for the role of natural selection.
- **HS-LS4-4** — Construct an explanation for how adaptations result from selection.
- **SP6** — Constructing explanations.

**Objectives (SWBAT):**
1. Explain natural selection in their own words.
2. Argue whether a human population could plausibly evolve a chosen "power" given enough time.

**Materials:** Paper clips (50/group), pieces of yarn cut to different lengths, stopwatch (phone OK), worksheet.

#### Do Now (5 min)
On board: *"Why don't humans have wings even though birds, bats, and bugs all evolved flight independently?"*

#### Mini-lesson (12 min)
**Natural selection in three sentences:**
1. Traits vary.
2. Some traits help you survive and reproduce more.
3. Those traits get more common over generations.

Walk through the *peppered moth* example briefly. Then ask: *what trait would a population need to be under pressure for, generation after generation, for humans to evolve a "power"?*

#### Activity (28 min)
**Predator-Prey Selection Simulation (20 min):**
- 30 paper clips scattered on the floor = "prey"
- 10 clips painted one color = "easy to see"
- "Predators" (students) have 15 seconds to grab prey, then count
- The clips left over "reproduce" — surviving colors multiply
- Run 3 generations. Track which color survives.

**Reflection (8 min):** In groups, answer:
- Which trait won?
- What real environmental pressure could select for *your* hero's power?
- Could that pressure realistically exist for humans long enough to evolve the power?

**Codex prep:** The "origin of power" gains a scientific explanation — *inherited mutation, environmental adaptation, accident, or technology.* Students lock this in for the Power Schematic.

#### Exit Ticket (5 min)
> "Pick your hero's power. Name one environmental pressure that *would* select for it. Be specific."

#### Differentiation
- **12–13:** Use only 2 generations and color-only selection.
- **17–19:** Add a tradeoff — every power has a cost. What does this power cost the body?

## WEEK 1 · DAY 3 — History

### Title: Outlaws, Folk Heroes, and Who Holds the Pen

**MA HSS Standards:**
- **USI Practice Standard 3** — Analyze multiple causation.
- **WHI Practice Standard 5** — Develop claims using evidence.
- **USI.T4** — Examine the abolition movement.

**Objectives (SWBAT):**
1. Identify how the same person can be called a "hero" or "criminal" depending on the source.
2. Analyze a primary source for bias and framing.

**Materials:** Three short primary source excerpts about Harriet Tubman: (1) an 1860s wanted-poster style notice, (2) Frederick Douglass's praise of her, (3) a modern textbook entry. Same treatment for Robin Hood (medieval ballad vs. sheriff's record) if time allows.

#### Do Now (5 min)
On board: *"Who decides who is a criminal? Is it the same person who decides who is a hero?"*

#### Mini-lesson (10 min)
Introduce **framing** in history. The same action — helping enslaved people escape — was a *federal crime* under the Fugitive Slave Act and a *heroic act* in the moral record. *Both descriptions are historically accurate. The difference is who's holding the pen.*

#### Activity (28 min)
**Source Triangulation (20 min):** In pairs, students read the three Harriet Tubman sources. They complete a chart:
- Who wrote this?
- What words do they use? (Hero, criminal, agitator, savior, conductor, fugitive?)
- What did the writer want the reader to do or feel?

Then pairs write a one-paragraph claim: *Was Harriet Tubman a criminal or a hero — and what's the right answer to that question?*

**Share out (8 min):** Several pairs read. Class discusses: can someone be both? Neither? Does it depend on the year?

**Codex prep:** Students decide whether their hero's actions would be *legal* in the world they live in. This seeds Week 3 (the villain question) and the Manifesto panel.

#### Exit Ticket (5 min)
> "Someone you admire was probably called a criminal by someone at some point. Who, and by whom?"

#### Differentiation
- **12–13:** Provide pre-highlighted key phrases in each source.
- **17–19:** Add a fourth source — a contemporary news article about someone framed similarly today (e.g., a whistleblower).

## WEEK 1 · DAY 4 — ELA

### Title: Sensory Detail — Show Don't Tell

**MA ELA Standards:**
- **W.9-10.3.d** — Use precise words, telling details, and sensory language.
- **L.9-10.5** — Demonstrate understanding of figurative language.

**Objectives (SWBAT):**
1. Revise a passage to replace "telling" with "showing."
2. Add at least three sensory details to their origin story.

**Materials:** Day 1/2 origin drafts. Two "before/after" example paragraphs (teacher-made or from mentor texts).

#### Do Now (5 min)
On board: *"Rewrite 'I was scared' WITHOUT using the words scared, afraid, or nervous. Show me what scared looks like in your body."*

#### Mini-lesson (10 min)
Show two paragraphs. Both describe the same scene. One uses adjectives ("It was a scary day"). The other uses sensory detail ("My shoelace was untied. I remember that — I was looking down at it when the door slammed."). Ask: *which one puts you there?*

Teach **the five-sense pass:** when revising, check that each scene has at least 2–3 of: sight, sound, smell, taste, touch.

#### Activity (30 min)
**Revision Lab (25 min):** Students return to their origin draft with three highlighters (or symbols):
- Mark every "telling" word (felt, was, seemed).
- Replace one with a sensory detail.
- Add one detail from a sense not yet in the piece.
- Add one specific, concrete object (not "a building" — "the Walgreens on the corner with the cracked sign").

**Pair share (5 min):** Read your strongest sensory line to a partner. Partner names which sense was used.

**Codex prep:** This draft is the version that will go into the Origin Story panel of the trifold.

#### Exit Ticket (5 min)
> "What is one object you'll never forget from a real day in your life? Don't tell me why — just name the object."

#### Differentiation
- **12–13:** Provide a sense-checklist they fill in as they revise.
- **17–19:** Add metaphor as a constraint — one sensory detail must double as a symbol.

## WEEK 1 · DAY 4 — Math

### Title: Probability of Being Chosen

**MA Math Standards:**
- **7.SP.C.5** — Understand that probability is a number between 0 and 1.
- **7.SP.C.6** — Approximate probability by collecting data.
- **HSS.CP.A.1** — Describe events as subsets of a sample space.

**Objectives (SWBAT):**
1. Compute compound probabilities for independent events.
2. Express the rarity of a "hero origin" as a probability.

**Materials:** Dice, coins, deck of cards (one per group), probability worksheet.

#### Do Now (5 min)
On board: *"Chance of being struck by lightning in your lifetime: about 1 in 15,000. Chance of being a billionaire: about 1 in 600,000. Chance of being a superhero: ?"*

#### Mini-lesson (12 min)
Walk through **multiplying independent probabilities.** Example: Spider-Man requires (1) being at a science demo × (2) the demo having a radioactive spider × (3) being bitten × (4) the bite mutating cells instead of killing you. Multiply rough estimates → astronomically small number.

Key idea: heroes are statistically *rare*. That's part of why we tell stories about them. But the math also reveals: *most "chosen ones" weren't chosen by chance. They put themselves in the room.*

#### Activity (28 min)
**Origin Probability Workshop (20 min):** Each student calculates the probability of their *own* hero's origin event happening. They break it into 3–5 component probabilities and estimate each one. Encourage realistic numbers.

Then: students write a one-sentence reflection — *given those odds, what part of your hero's becoming was actually chance, and what part was choice?*

**Group share (8 min):** Three volunteers share their probability and their choice/chance breakdown.

**Codex prep:** A line about choice vs. chance gets added to the Manifesto panel.

#### Exit Ticket (5 min)
> "If something is 1-in-a-million unlikely, but you put yourself in front of it every day, does it stay unlikely? Explain in one sentence."

#### Differentiation
- **12–13:** Pre-fill 3 of the 5 component probabilities.
- **17–19:** Introduce conditional probability — the second event becomes more likely given the first.

## WEEK 1 · DAY 4 — Science

### Title: The Brain on Fear — Courage as Neuroscience

**MA STE Standards:**
- **HS-LS1-2** — Develop a model to illustrate the hierarchical organization of systems.
- **HS-LS1-3** — Plan investigations to provide evidence that feedback mechanisms maintain homeostasis.

**Objectives (SWBAT):**
1. Diagram the brain's fear response (amygdala → adrenaline → body).
2. Explain why courage is not the absence of fear.

**Materials:** Brain diagram handout (label-the-parts), stopwatch, "fear scenarios" cards.

#### Do Now (5 min)
On board: *"Your heart is pounding. Your hands shake. Your stomach drops. Is this fear, excitement, or both? What's the difference?"*

#### Mini-lesson (12 min)
Build the chain on the board:
- Threat detected → **amygdala** fires
- Signal → **hypothalamus** → adrenal glands → **adrenaline + cortisol**
- Body: heart rate up, blood to muscles, digestion off, pupils dilate
- This is *fight, flight, or freeze.*

Key idea: **the fear response is not weakness. It's your body trying to save your life.** Courage isn't the *absence* of this response — courage is acting *while* it's firing. That's literally what every soldier, firefighter, and parent in a crisis does.

*This lesson lands especially hard for this audience. Don't rush it.*

#### Activity (28 min)
**Scenario Cards (15 min):** In groups of 3, students read 5 scenario cards (e.g., "speaking in front of a judge," "running into a burning building," "telling someone you love them"). For each:
- Mark whether the body's fear response would fire.
- Mark whether the action takes *courage*.

Notice the overlap: every act of courage has the body screaming "don't."

**Hero application (10 min):** Each student writes 2–3 sentences answering: *what does your hero fear, and what do they do anyway?*

**Codex prep:** The answer becomes a line on the Power Schematic panel — "what this hero is afraid of" sits next to "what this hero can do." That juxtaposition is the point.

#### Exit Ticket (5 min)
> "Name one thing that scared you this year that you did anyway."

#### Differentiation
- **12–13:** Provide a labeled brain diagram and shorter scenarios.
- **17–19:** Add a research extension — how does chronic stress alter the amygdala over time?

## WEEK 1 · DAY 4 — History

### Title: Massachusetts Heroes

**MA HSS Standards:**
- **USI.T2** — Roots of the Revolution in colonial Massachusetts.
- **USI.T4** — Abolition and the antislavery movement (Massachusetts as a center).
- **Practice Standard 5** — Develop claims using evidence.

**Objectives (SWBAT):**
1. Identify three Massachusetts figures whose actions changed history.
2. Argue which qualifies as a "hero" and on whose terms.

**Materials:** Hero Card Set (printed): **Crispus Attucks** (Boston Massacre), **Phillis Wheatley** (first published African American poet, Boston), **Frederick Douglass's Boston years**, **William Lloyd Garrison** (Boston abolitionist), **Mel King** (modern Boston organizer), **Mum Bett / Elizabeth Freeman** (sued for her freedom in MA, 1781). Local map of where each lived/worked.

#### Do Now (5 min)
On board: *"Name a hero from Massachusetts that *isn't* a Founding Father or a sports player."*

#### Mini-lesson (10 min)
Teacher introduces the idea that **Massachusetts has produced an outsized number of people who changed the rules of the country.** Brief intro to Mum Bett: in 1781, an enslaved woman in Sheffield, MA sued for her freedom under the new Massachusetts constitution — and won. Her case helped end slavery in MA.

Key teaching point: the original 13 colonies' first state to functionally end slavery did so because of a Black woman who didn't take "no" for an answer. *That's a Massachusetts hero story most students have never heard.*

#### Activity (28 min)
**Local Heroes Jigsaw (20 min):** Groups of 3. Each group gets one MA hero card. They fill out:
- What did they do?
- Whose rules did they break?
- Whose lives did they change?
- Where in MA did they do it?

Groups present 90-second summaries. Class places each hero on a shared MA map.

**Personal connection (8 min):** Each student writes 1–2 sentences: *which of these heroes do you think your community needs more of today, and why?*

**Codex prep:** Students select 1–2 of these MA heroes as part of their hero's "lineage" — the Cultural Lineage panel grows.

#### Exit Ticket (5 min)
> "What part of Massachusetts history did your school skip? Take one guess."

#### Differentiation
- **12–13:** Shorten hero cards and provide guiding questions in the margin.
- **17–19:** Add a contemporary MA hero (e.g., Monica Cannon-Grant or a local figure you trust) and have them debate whether the person qualifies.

## WEEK 1 · DAY 5 — CODEX BUILD DAY

Day 5 of each week is dedicated to building the physical Codex Trifold. Same 50-minute block, but the activity shifts from learning new content to *making the artifact.*

### WEEK 1 · DAY 5 — ELA: Origin Story Panel Final

**MA ELA Standards:**
- **W.9-10.5** — Develop and strengthen writing as needed by planning, revising, editing.
- **W.9-10.4** — Produce clear and coherent writing.

**Objectives (SWBAT):**
1. Produce a final, hand-lettered or typed Origin Story for the Codex panel.
2. Give and receive peer feedback using a structured protocol.

**Materials:** Cardboard trifolds (issued today), construction paper for panel, fine-tip markers or pens, peer review handout.

#### Do Now (5 min)
On board: *"Read your origin draft to yourself once. Underline your strongest sentence."*

#### Mini-lesson (8 min)
Introduce the **TAG peer review protocol:**
- **T**ell something specific you liked.
- **A**sk one clarifying question.
- **G**ive one suggestion.

Show how to apply it without being vague ("it was good" ≠ feedback).

#### Activity (32 min)
**Part A — Peer review (12 min):** Pairs swap drafts. Each gives written TAG feedback.

**Part B — Final transfer (20 min):** Students write or paste their final origin story onto a piece of construction paper, then glue it to the upper-left panel of their trifold. Encourage hand-lettering, color, doodled borders — this is *theirs*. If using tech: type and print.

#### Exit Ticket (5 min)
> "Look at your panel. What's one thing you want a stranger to feel when they read it?"

#### Differentiation
- **12–13:** Provide an origin story template they can paste in and fill blanks.
- **17–19:** Require a hand-drawn frame or visual motif that reinforces the story's theme.

### WEEK 1 · DAY 5 — Math: Stat Sheet Panel Final

**MA Math Standards:**
- **HSN.Q.A.3** — Choose a level of accuracy appropriate to limitations on measurement.
- **SMP.6** — Attend to precision.

**Objectives (SWBAT):**
1. Finalize a 4-stat hero card with numbers, units, and justifications.
2. Communicate quantitative reasoning clearly to a non-math audience.

**Materials:** Stat sheet template (printable), rulers, fine markers, construction paper.

#### Do Now (5 min)
On board: *"Pick one of your hero's stats. Say it out loud as you'd say it to someone who hates math."*

#### Mini-lesson (8 min)
Teach the difference between **a number** and **a number with meaning.** "Strength: 850" tells you nothing. "Strength: can lift 850 lbs, more than a polar bear, less than a Volkswagen" — that's communication.

Show one example of a clear stat sheet and one cluttered one. Ask which they'd rather read.

#### Activity (32 min)
**Stat Sheet Construction.** Students finalize a 4-row stat card:

| Stat | Number | Unit | What it's comparable to |
|---|---|---|---|
| Strength | | | |
| Speed | | | |
| Endurance | | | |
| (Choice) | | | |

Each row gets a one-sentence justification using calculations from earlier in the week (Day 2 patrol distances, Day 3 force calculations).

Final card is glued onto the center-bottom of the trifold.

#### Exit Ticket (5 min)
> "Which of your 4 stats is the one a reader will remember? Why?"

#### Differentiation
- **12–13:** Provide a fill-in stat template.
- **17–19:** Require one *negative* stat (a weakness with a number) — heroes are interesting because of what they can't do.

### WEEK 1 · DAY 5 — Science: Power Schematic Panel Final

**MA STE Standards:**
- **HS-LS3-2** — Make and defend a claim about genetic variation.
- **SP6** — Constructing explanations.
- **SP8** — Obtaining, evaluating, and communicating information.

**Objectives (SWBAT):**
1. Diagram their hero's power origin with scientifically grounded labels.
2. Write a 3–5 sentence scientific explanation of how the power works.

**Materials:** Blank paper for diagram, fine-tip markers, science notebooks (for reference back to week's labs), construction paper.

#### Do Now (5 min)
On board: *"If a scientist asked you to prove your hero's power could exist, where would you start?"*

#### Mini-lesson (8 min)
Teach the **schematic format**:
- A central labeled diagram (the body, organ, or device involved)
- 3–5 callouts pointing to specific parts
- A short paragraph beneath: *origin, mechanism, limits*

Show one science-textbook example (an eye, a heart) and explain why the *limits* matter as much as the mechanism. A power with no limit isn't a power — it's a story-killer.

#### Activity (32 min)
**Schematic Construction.** Each student draws their hero's power schematic:
- The biology/physics/chemistry of where it lives
- Labels for the 3–5 critical components
- One labeled *limit* (drains energy, breaks bones, requires sunlight, etc.)
- A paragraph below explaining the origin (inherited / mutated / environmental / technological) and the mechanism

Glued to the lower-left panel of the trifold.

#### Exit Ticket (5 min)
> "What's the *cost* of your hero's power? Every real thing in nature has one."

#### Differentiation
- **12–13:** Provide a body outline they can label.
- **17–19:** Require one citation of a real biological or physical phenomenon their power is modeled on.

### WEEK 1 · DAY 5 — History: Cultural Lineage Panel Final

**MA HSS Standards:**
- **Practice Standard 5** — Develop claims using evidence.
- **Practice Standard 7** — Determine next steps and take informed action.

**Objectives (SWBAT):**
1. Identify 2–3 hero traditions (cultural or historical) their hero descends from.
2. Write a short essay connecting their hero to that lineage.

**Materials:** Notes from Days 1, 3, and 4 (cultural heroes, folk heroes, MA heroes). Construction paper, markers.

#### Do Now (5 min)
On board: *"Every hero you create comes from somewhere. Name the hero you've learned about this week who's closest to yours."*

#### Mini-lesson (8 min)
Introduce the idea of **lineage** — every new hero stands on the shoulders of older ones. Spider-Man is a Greek-style "reluctant chosen one." Black Panther is in the lineage of African kings and warrior traditions. Miles Morales is in the lineage of *Spider-Man* itself, plus the Latino and Black neighborhoods of Brooklyn.

Key idea: claiming a lineage is *not* unoriginal. It's how stories get power.

#### Activity (32 min)
**Lineage Essay (20 min):** Each student writes a 1-paragraph response to:
- *Which 2–3 hero traditions does my hero descend from?*
- *What did I take from each one?*
- *What did I leave behind?*

Must reference: at least one cultural hero (Day 1), and one MA or real-world hero (Day 4).

**Panel construction (12 min):** Students mount the essay on construction paper, add a "family tree" sketch showing the lineage visually, and glue to the upper-right panel of the trifold.

#### Exit Ticket (5 min)
> "Whose story does your hero make sure is *not* forgotten?"

#### Differentiation
- **12–13:** Provide a "I took ___ from ___" sentence frame.
- **17–19:** Require a lineage that crosses at least two continents or eras.

## Design Notes for Justice-Involved Youth

- **Avoid the "saved by the hero" frame.** Position students as the heroes, not the rescued. They've been on the receiving end of systems that claim to save them.
- **Villain analysis is your secret weapon.** Killmonger, Magneto — these characters articulate real grievances. Letting students take them seriously, then think critically, treats them as adults. (Comes in Week 3.)
- **Real heroes anchor Week 4.** Community speakers — formerly incarcerated mentors, organizers, EMTs. The "return with the elixir" stage hits different when it's modeled.
- **Trauma-informed by design.** The hero's journey *includes* the abyss. Don't skip it, but build in choice — students decide how much of their own story they share.
