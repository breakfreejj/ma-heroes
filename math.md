# Origin Story — Math Lesson Plans

**10 mini-lessons · 30–45 min each**

Each lesson follows the BreakFree-style structure:
- **Do Now / Bellringer** (~3 min) — short hook problem
- **Mini Lesson** (~10–12 min) — teacher explains content with worked examples
- **Guided Practice** — pair or class work with teacher support
- **Independent Practice** — students apply on their own
- **Closing** — Exit Ticket with reflective stem

Each lesson includes a **Differentiation Considerations** block and is aligned to **Massachusetts Curriculum Framework for Mathematics (high school)** standards, with foundational links to middle-school RP standards noted where students may need recovery support. Audience: 15–18 year olds in juvenile justice classrooms; expect wide numeracy ranges within a single class — calculators are allowed throughout.

## MATH — 10 Mini-Lessons

**Arc:** A 10-lesson deep dive into **proportional reasoning** — ratios, proportions, percentages, and rates — applied to superhero design. Every lesson stays inside this one mathematical cluster (the way Unspoiled stayed inside linear equations and Unbeatable stayed inside statistics). Each lesson introduces one new skill while reinforcing the previous days', so a student who masters Day 1's "ratio as X out of Y" can ride the arc all the way to Day 10's full Hero Stat Sheet.

**Skill progression:** Day 1 ratios → Day 2 ratio-to-percent → Day 3 rates (a special ratio) → Day 4 solving proportions → Day 5 scale (proportions in geometry) → Day 6 percent change → Day 7 comparing ratios with unlike denominators → Day 8 partitioning into 100% → Day 9 percent of big numbers → Day 10 capstone synthesis.

**Final artifact for the Hero Pitch:** Hero Stat Sheet & Battle Analysis.

### Day 1 — The Hero Power Index

**Lesson objectives:**
- Define a *ratio* as a comparison of two quantities.
- Write the same ratio in three equivalent forms: fraction (X/Y), colon (X:Y), and "out of" language (X out of Y).
- Sum 5 stats into a single combined ratio over the maximum possible.
- Rank heroes by their combined ratio.

**Assessment:**
- Hero Power Index Worksheet
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- This is the foundation lesson for the whole unit. Every later lesson uses ratios; today's job is to make the notation second nature.
- Marvel publishes the 5 standard hero stats (Strength, Speed, Intelligence, Durability, Energy) on a 1–7 scale. Each stat is naturally a ratio (X out of 7).
- Students combine the 5 stats into a "Hero Power Index" — the sum expressed as a ratio over 35 (the max possible). This is a single tidy number that compares one hero to another.
- The lesson establishes three equivalent forms of ratio (fraction, colon, "out of"), which students will switch between throughout the unit.

| Component | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| **Do Now** | "Rank these 4 heroes (Miles, Shuri, Storm, Kamala) 1–4 on STRENGTH using your gut. Defend your #1 in one sentence." | Slides with 4 hero portraits; handout or journal |
| **Mini Lesson** | "What is a ratio?" Project the definition: a comparison of two quantities, written three equivalent ways — **X/Y**, **X:Y**, **X out of Y**. <br><br>Show Marvel's 5 standard hero stats (Strength, Speed, Intelligence, Durability, Energy) on a 1–7 scale. Demo: Miles Morales's Strength is **4/7** = **4:7** = **4 out of 7**. <br><br>Brief teacher demo: write 3 of Storm's stats in all three forms. Class chorally reads each form back. | Slides; Stat Cards handout (6 heroes with 5 stats each on the 1–7 scale) |
| **Guided Practice** | **Ratio Translation Drill.** Pairs receive 5 stats for one hero and must write each in all three forms. Whole-class share-out: teacher calls on 3 pairs. Teacher corrects notation errors visibly. | Stat Cards; Ratio Translation handout |
| **Independent Practice** | **The Hero Power Index.** Students sum each hero's 5 stats and express the total as a ratio over 35 (e.g., 27/35). Do this for all 6 heroes on the cards. Then: rank the 6 heroes from highest Power Index to lowest. Final question: does the ranking match who'd "win"? Why or why not? | Stat Cards; Hero Power Index handout |
| **Closing** | **Exit Ticket** — "Write the ratio 'four out of seven' in three different forms: ___, ___, ___. The hero with the highest Power Index is ___ with a ratio of ___ over 35." | Handout or journal |

**Differentiation Considerations:**
- **Translation Chart** handout with the three ratio forms side-by-side and 5 worked examples for students new to the notation.
- Visual learners: project a "ratio bar" diagram (a bar with X shaded segments out of Y total) for each stat as a parallel representation.
- **Fraction pre-teach** (5 min) for students missing the fraction floor: review of numerator, denominator, and whole.
- Calculator allowed throughout (though most arithmetic today is single-digit addition).
- Vocabulary preview: *ratio, equivalent, sum, index, maximum*.
- Exit ticket may be completed verbally with the teacher.

**Standards (Massachusetts Curriculum Framework for Mathematics, high school):**
- **N.Q.1** — Use units as a way to understand problems and to guide the solution of multi-step problems; choose and interpret units consistently in formulas.
- **N.Q.2** — Define appropriate quantities for the purpose of descriptive modeling.
- *Foundational (middle-school recovery): **6.RP.1** — Understand the concept of a ratio and use ratio language to describe a ratio relationship between two quantities.*

### Day 2 — Normalizing & Ranking

**Lesson objectives:**
- Convert a ratio to a percentage (X/Y → X/Y × 100%).
- Convert a percentage back to a ratio.
- Recognize that "percentage" means "out of 100" — a universal common base.
- Compare quantities on different scales (1–7 vs. 1–10 vs. 0–100) by normalizing them all to percentages.

**Assessment:**
- Conversion Carousel handout
- Re-Rank the Heroes worksheet
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- Today's move: convert a ratio (X/Y) to a percentage (X/Y × 100). The word *percent* literally means "per 100."
- Two methods: (1) divide and multiply by 100, or (2) set up as a proportion X/Y = ?/100 and solve.
- Percentages exist to make comparison easy. 5/7 vs. 14/20 looks impossible to compare; ~71% vs. 70% is instant.
- The "Re-Rank" activity shows students that gut rankings can be wrong when scales differ. Math reveals truth.
- This sets up percentages as the comparison tool used in Days 6, 7, 8, 9, 10.

| Component | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| **Do Now** | "Hero A scored 70 points out of 100. Hero B scored 14 points out of 20. Who scored higher? How do you know — without a calculator?" | Handout or journal |
| **Mini Lesson** | "How do you compare ratios on different scales?" Teach: a **percentage** is a ratio whose denominator is 100. To convert any ratio to a percentage, multiply by 100. <br><br>Two methods on slides: <br>**Method 1:** Divide, then multiply by 100. 5/7 = 0.714... × 100 = 71.4%. <br>**Method 2:** Set up a proportion. 5/7 = X/100. Cross-multiply: 7X = 500. X = 71.4. <br><br>Demo: convert Miles Morales's 4/7 strength to a percentage. Two more examples — one tidy (5/10), one messier (3/8). | Slides; Conversion Methods handout |
| **Guided Practice** | **Conversion Carousel.** 8 ratio-to-percentage problems on stations (or slides). Mix of "out of 7," "out of 10," "out of 20," "out of 8." Pairs work through them, show work. Quick whole-class share-out — call out 3 pairs, push back on rounding errors. | Conversion Carousel handout |
| **Independent Practice** | **Re-Rank the Heroes.** Given 4 heroes' stats on Marvel's 1–7 scale AND a competing universe's "X-Stats" on a 1–10 scale: students convert all stats to percentages, then re-rank each hero overall. Did anyone's rank change after normalization? Write 2 sentences on the biggest mover. | Hero Stats handout (two scales side-by-side); Re-Rank handout |
| **Closing** | **Exit Ticket** — "Convert 6 out of 8 to a percentage: ___%. After normalizing, the hero whose rank changed the most was ___." | Handout or journal |

**Differentiation Considerations:**
- Calculator allowed (and recommended) for all conversion work.
- **Step-by-step conversion checklist** handout for students new to the procedure.
- Sentence frame for the share-out: "I converted ___ to ___% by ___."
- Visual learners: ratio-bar diagrams shaded to show 25%, 50%, 75%, 100% as anchors.
- For students who already have percent fluency: extension — "what is each hero as a percentage of the MAX possible (35)?" (links forward to Day 7).
- Vocabulary preview: *percentage, normalize, common base*.

**Standards (Massachusetts Curriculum Framework for Mathematics, HS):**
- **N.Q.1** — Use units as a way to understand and guide multi-step problems.
- **A.CED.1** — Create equations and inequalities in one variable and use them to solve problems (the proportion a/b = X/100).
- *Foundational: **6.RP.3.c** — Find a percent of a quantity as a rate per 100; solve problems involving finding the whole, given a part and the percent.*

### Day 3 — Hero Speeds: Rate as Ratio

**Lesson objectives:**
- Define a *rate* as a ratio that compares two quantities with different units (e.g., miles per hour).
- Define a *unit rate* as the rate when the second quantity is 1.
- Calculate unit rates from raw distance-and-time data.
- Convert between rate units (mph, km/h, ft/sec, m/s) so different rates can be compared.

**Assessment:**
- Speed Conversion handout
- Who-is-Fastest ranking
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- A rate is a special kind of ratio — the two quantities have different units (e.g., "30 miles per 1 hour" = 30/1, but with units attached).
- Unit rate = the rate per 1 of something. "30 miles per hour" is already a unit rate; "90 miles in 3 hours" is not — divide to find the unit rate (30 mph).
- To compare speeds reported in different units, convert them all to the same unit. Same skill as Day 2 (common base), now applied to rates.
- This lesson sets up Day 4, where students will use rates inside proportions to solve catch-up problems.

| Component | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| **Do Now** | "Miles can swing 30 miles in 1 hour. How far can he swing in 2 hours? In half an hour? In 15 minutes?" | Handout or journal |
| **Mini Lesson** | "What is a rate?" Define: a rate is a ratio that compares two quantities with **different units**. "30 miles per hour" is the ratio 30 miles / 1 hour. <br><br>**Unit rate** = the rate when the second quantity is 1. Show: Miles swings 90 miles in 3 hours → 90/3 = 30 mph unit rate. <br><br>Demo rate conversion: 30 mph → ft/sec. 30 miles × 5,280 ft/mile = 158,400 ft in 1 hour. 158,400 ÷ 3,600 sec = 44 ft/sec. <br><br>Use a **Rate Conversion Chart** (1 mile = 5,280 ft, 1 hour = 3,600 sec, 1 mile ≈ 1.609 km, 1 m = 3.28 ft). Three more example conversions: km/h → mph; m/s → ft/sec; mph → km/h. | Slides; Rate Conversion Chart handout |
| **Guided Practice** | **Speed Conversion Drill.** Pairs convert 4 hero speeds (given in 4 different units) to a common unit (default: mph). Show work. Compare answers across pairs. Teacher pushes back on dropped units (always label the answer with its unit). | Speed Conversion handout |
| **Independent Practice** | **Who Is Fastest?** 5 heroes' speeds given in different units: Miles in mph, Storm flying in km/h, a regular human in m/s, a vehicle in ft/sec, an animal in mph. Students convert all to mph (or another chosen common unit) and rank from fastest to slowest. Justify the ranking in 2 sentences. | Hero Speeds handout (mixed units) |
| **Closing** | **Exit Ticket** — "The fastest hero on the list was ___ with a speed of ___ [common unit]. The trickiest conversion was ___ to ___ because ___." | Handout or journal |

**Differentiation Considerations:**
- **Rate Conversion Chart** handout always available — students should not be memorizing conversion factors.
- Calculator allowed (and encouraged).
- For students new to rate conversion: pre-teach with one fully-worked example *before* the Guided Practice.
- Visual: project a "speed bar" comparison showing all converted speeds on the same scale.
- Sentence frame for the ranking justification: "Hero ___ is faster than Hero ___ because their speed in mph is ___."
- **Extension** for early finishers: "If Miles got faster by 10%, what would his new mph be?" — previews Day 6 percent change.
- Vocabulary preview: *rate, unit rate, conversion factor*.

**Standards (Massachusetts Curriculum Framework for Mathematics, HS):**
- **N.Q.1** — Use units as a way to understand problems; choose and interpret units consistently in formulas.
- **N.Q.2** — Define appropriate quantities for the purpose of descriptive modeling.
- **A.CED.1** — Create equations to represent rate problems.
- *Foundational: **6.RP.3.b** — Solve unit rate problems including those involving unit pricing and constant speed.*

### Day 4 — The Catch-Up: Proportions in Motion

**Lesson objectives:**
- Set up a proportion (a/b = c/d) to represent a real-world catch-up scenario.
- Solve a proportion for an unknown using cross-multiplication.
- Apply proportions to rate-based "catch-up" problems.
- Verify a proportion solution by checking units and reasonableness.

**Assessment:**
- Catch-Up Worksheet (3 problems)
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- A *proportion* is an equation stating two ratios are equal: a/b = c/d.
- This is the first lesson where students **solve** for an unknown — a real step up from comparing and converting in Days 1–3.
- Two solving methods: (1) **cross-multiplication** (a × d = b × c), (2) **scaling** (multiply both sides of a ratio by a common factor).
- Catch-up problems are the classic application: villain distance = villain rate × time; hero distance = hero rate × time. They meet when the distances are equal.
- No algebra (no x's flying around). The math stays in arithmetic + proportion-solving.

| Component | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| **Do Now** | "If Miles can swing 30 miles in 1 hour, how long does it take him to swing 75 miles? Set it up as a proportion *before* solving." | Handout or journal |
| **Mini Lesson** | "What is a proportion?" Define: an equation stating two ratios are equal. Example: 30 miles / 1 hour = 75 miles / X hours. <br><br>Two ways to solve: <br>**Method 1 — Cross-multiplication:** 30 × X = 1 × 75 → X = 75/30 = 2.5 hours. <br>**Method 2 — Scaling:** 30 × 2.5 = 75. So time scales the same way: 1 × 2.5 = 2.5 hours. <br><br>Demo a worked catch-up problem: "A villain has a 10-minute head start at 40 mph. Miles is at 60 mph. When and where does Miles catch the villain?" <br>Walk through: <br>(1) Convert 10 min to hours: 1/6 hour. <br>(2) In time t (hours), villain is at distance 40 × (t + 1/6). Miles is at 60 × t. <br>(3) Set up the proportion (or equation): 40(t + 1/6) = 60t. <br>(4) Solve arithmetically: 40t + 20/3 = 60t → 20/3 = 20t → t = 1/3 hour = 20 minutes. <br>(5) Distance: 60 × 1/3 = 20 miles. <br>(6) Check units and reasonableness. | Slides; Worked Example handout |
| **Guided Practice** | **Set It Up Lightning Round.** 4 short scenarios on slides. Pairs set up the proportion (don't solve yet) and write it on a mini-whiteboard or sheet. Teacher cold-calls 2 pairs per scenario to show the setup. Push back on common errors (mismatched units, wrong order). | 4 Scenario slides; mini-whiteboards or paper |
| **Independent Practice** | **The Catch-Up.** 3 catch-up problems of increasing difficulty: <br>(1) Hero A heads out at 20 mph. Hero B follows 30 minutes later at 50 mph. When does B catch A? <br>(2) Storm flies at 80 km/h. A storm front moves at 25 km/h with a 1-hour head start. When does she reach it? <br>(3) Two heroes start from cities 120 miles apart, moving toward each other at 40 mph and 60 mph. When do they meet? <br>Students set up the proportion AND solve. Show all work. | Catch-Up Worksheet |
| **Closing** | **Exit Ticket** — "In a catch-up problem, the proportion I set up looked like ___ = ___. Solving for the unknown gave me ___. The check that told me my answer made sense was ___." | Handout or journal |

**Differentiation Considerations:**
- **Worked-Example handout** that walks through cross-multiplication step by step.
- **Setup Bank** — 8 example setups (without solutions) for students to study patterns before attempting.
- Calculator allowed (and recommended).
- Pair work for Independent Practice for students who need support.
- **Timeline diagram** option — for visual learners, draw both heroes' positions at each time interval before setting up the proportion.
- For students who finish: extension problem with three heroes (a 3-way race or chase).
- Vocabulary preview: *proportion, cross-multiplication, scaling, unknown*.

**Standards (Massachusetts Curriculum Framework for Mathematics, HS):**
- **A.CED.1** — Create equations and inequalities in one variable and use them to solve problems (including those arising from linear equations).
- **A.REI.3** — Solve linear equations and inequalities in one variable.
- **N.Q.1** — Use units as a way to understand problems and to guide the solution.
- *Foundational: **7.RP.2** — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities.*

### Day 5 — Scale & Geometry

**Lesson objectives:**
- Define a *scale factor* as the ratio of corresponding linear measurements between similar figures.
- Use a scale factor to scale dimensions UP (model → real) and DOWN (real → model).
- Apply proportional reasoning to 2D and 3D geometric problems.
- Recognize that scale factor k for length means area scales by k² and volume by k³ (preview — connects to Science Day 6's square-cube law).

**Assessment:**
- Blueprint Scaling Practice
- Hero Tech Design handout
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- Scale factor is just a proportion in disguise — 1:48 means 1 inch on a model = 48 inches on the real object.
- Solving scale problems uses Day 4's cross-multiplication.
- This lesson is the natural midpoint payoff for the unit — Days 1–4 skills come together in a tactile, geometric context that's easier for many students than abstract rate problems.
- Brief intro to a deeper truth: linear scale factor k → area scales by k² → volume by k³. (Doubling all dimensions multiplies volume by 8×.) Used as a teaser for Science Day 6's biology of size.

| Component | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| **Do Now** | "Ant-Man is 6 feet tall and shrinks to ½ inch. By what factor did he shrink? (Hint: convert both heights to the same unit first.)" | Handout or journal |
| **Mini Lesson** | "What is a scale factor?" Define: the ratio of corresponding sides of similar figures. <br><br>Show: a model of Shuri's hover bike at 1:48 scale. If the model is 8 inches long, the real bike is 8 × 48 = 384 inches = 32 feet. <br><br>Demo two example problems using Day 4 proportion-solving: <br>(1) Given model dimensions and scale, find real dimensions. <br>(2) Given real dimensions and scale, find model dimensions. <br><br>Brief mention: if scale factor for length is k, **area** scales by k² and **volume** by k³. Use Ant-Man's shrink as a teaser: if Ant-Man shrinks to 1/144 his original height, his weight (volume × density) shrinks to (1/144)³ — about 1/3,000,000 of his original weight. (Science Day 6 picks this up.) | Slides; Scale Examples handout |
| **Guided Practice** | **Blueprint Translation.** Pairs work through 4 scale-conversion problems with Shuri's blueprints (a Wakandan tech suit, a hover bike, a kimoyo bead, a building). Set up a proportion, solve, label units. | Blueprint Practice handout |
| **Independent Practice** | **Design Your Hero's Tech.** Students choose one piece of tech for their hero (gauntlet, transport, weapon, communication device). Given the real-world dimensions, calculate the model size at 1:24 scale AND at 1:48 scale. Sketch the model at one of those scales with labeled dimensions. | Hero Tech Design handout (with sketch space) |
| **Closing** | **Exit Ticket** — "If a hero is 6 feet tall and their action figure is at 1:12 scale, the figure is ___ inches tall. The hardest part of scaling was ___." | Handout or journal |

**Differentiation Considerations:**
- **Pre-made scale-factor diagrams** with labeled examples for students new to similarity.
- Calculator allowed (decimal handling — many scale problems land on non-integer answers).
- **Unit conversion warm-up** (5 min) for students struggling with feet ↔ inches.
- Sketching allowed at any scale that's roughly proportional — accuracy of measurement (not artistic quality) is the priority.
- **Extension** for early finishers: the 3D volume question — "if a normal-size Ant-Man weighs 180 lbs, what would he weigh at ½ inch tall?" (using k³).
- Vocabulary preview: *scale factor, similar figures, proportion, dilation*.

**Standards (Massachusetts Curriculum Framework for Mathematics, HS):**
- **G.SRT.1.a/b** — Verify experimentally the properties of dilations given by a center and a scale factor (a dilation takes a line not passing through the center to a parallel line; the dilation of a line segment is longer or shorter in the ratio given by the scale factor).
- **G.SRT.2** — Use the definition of similarity in terms of similarity transformations to decide if two given figures are similar.
- **N.Q.1** — Use units to guide solution.
- *Foundational: **7.G.1** — Solve problems involving scale drawings of geometric figures.*

### Day 6 — The Web Swing: Comparing Routes

**Lesson objectives:**
- Define *percent change* as (new − old) / old × 100%.
- Calculate percent increase and percent decrease.
- Apply percent change to a real "compare two options" decision.
- Recognize the most common percent-change error: dividing by the wrong number.

**Assessment:**
- Increase-or-Decrease practice
- Route Comparison worksheet
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- Percent change is the most common application of percentages in real life — sales, raises, growth, savings, losses.
- Formula: (new − old) / old × 100%. The **denominator is the ORIGINAL value**. This is the #1 trip-up. Flag it explicitly.
- The Web Swing context: Miles has two ways to cross Brooklyn — one longer but faster, one shorter but slower. Comparing them requires percent change.
- Cross-links: Day 2 (percentage) and Day 3 (rate). Day 6 lets students apply BOTH together.

| Component | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| **Do Now** | "Miles can swing the long way to a fight in 5 minutes. The short way (through traffic) takes 7 minutes. By what PERCENT does the short way take longer than the long way?" | Handout or journal |
| **Mini Lesson** | "What is percent change?" Define on slides: <br><br>**percent change = (new − old) / old × 100%** <br><br>Demo: a hero's speed goes from 30 mph to 36 mph. Change = +6. Old = 30. 6/30 = 0.20 = **20% increase**. <br>Second example: a city's response time drops from 8 minutes to 6 minutes. Change = −2. Old = 8. −2/8 = −0.25 = **25% decrease**. <br><br>Important: the denominator is the **original** value (the "old"), not the new one. Common mistake to flag: students often divide by the wrong number. Walk through one example WRONG (dividing by 36 instead of 30) and show the error. | Slides; Percent Change Formula handout (with worked examples) |
| **Guided Practice** | **Increase or Decrease?** 6 short scenarios on slides. Pairs calculate percent change for each. Mix of increases and decreases. Some have tidy answers, some don't. Teacher cold-calls and pushes back on common errors (especially: dividing by the new value instead of the original). | 6-Scenario handout |
| **Independent Practice** | **The Web Swing: Comparing Routes.** Two routes for Miles through Brooklyn: <br>**Route A:** 3.5 miles at 22 mph (the long, fast route — rooftops). <br>**Route B:** 2.1 miles at 14 mph (the short, slow route — through traffic). <br>Students calculate: <br>(a) time for each route (Day 3 skill: distance / rate). <br>(b) percent saved (or lost) by choosing the faster route. <br>(c) which route Miles should pick if speed is the priority, and why. | Route Comparison handout (with a simple Brooklyn map graphic for context) |
| **Closing** | **Exit Ticket** — "The faster route saves ___% of the time. The most common mistake in percent change is dividing by the ___ value instead of the ___ value." | Handout or journal |

**Differentiation Considerations:**
- **Formula reference handout** with the percent-change formula and 4 worked examples — keep on desk.
- Sentence frame for the comparison: "Route ___ takes ___ minutes. Route ___ takes ___ minutes. The percent saved is ___%."
- Calculator allowed.
- Visual: bar diagram showing both route times and the savings as a shaded portion.
- **Extension** for early finishers: "If Miles's top speed increased by 15%, what would his new mph be? Then what would the route times be?"
- Vocabulary preview: *percent change, increase, decrease, original value*.

**Standards (Massachusetts Curriculum Framework for Mathematics, HS):**
- **N.Q.1** — Use units to guide solution.
- **A.CED.1** — Create equations and inequalities to model percent-change scenarios.
- *Foundational: **7.RP.3** — Use proportional relationships to solve multi-step ratio and percent problems, including percent increase and decrease.*

### Day 7 — Hero Win Rates

**Lesson objectives:**
- Express a win/loss record as a ratio AND as a percentage.
- Compare two records with **unlike denominators** by converting both to a common form.
- Identify which of two heroes has the better win rate (and by how much).
- Recognize that "more wins" does not always mean "better win rate."

**Assessment:**
- Win Rate Match-Ups handout
- Hero Power Rankings worksheet
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- Win rate = wins / total fights. Easy to express as a fraction OR a percent.
- The cognitive move: comparing 18 wins out of 25 vs. 22 wins out of 30 is NOT obvious. You can't just look at "22 > 18 so Hero B is better." Convert both to percentages, then compare.
- This lesson is a deliberate consolidation moment — re-uses Days 1, 2, 6 skills in a fresh context, then adds the one new wrinkle (comparing ratios with unlike denominators). Confidence-builder before harder Day 8.
- Real-world parallel: sports rankings use winning percentage for exactly this reason.

| Component | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| **Do Now** | "Hero A has 18 wins out of 25 fights. Hero B has 22 wins out of 30 fights. Who has the BETTER record? (Hint: don't just compare 18 vs. 22.)" | Handout or journal |
| **Mini Lesson** | "Comparing fractions with unlike denominators — why direct comparison fails." Demo: 18 < 22, but the totals are different (25 vs. 30), so we can't trust raw wins. <br><br>Two methods on slides: <br>**Method 1 — Convert to percentages** (most common, cleanest): 18/25 = 72%. 22/30 ≈ 73.3%. Hero B is slightly better. <br>**Method 2 — Find a common denominator**: 18/25 = 108/150. 22/30 = 110/150. Hero B wins by 2 out of 150. <br><br>Percentages are usually cleaner, but the common-denominator method shows WHY percentages work — it's all about a common base. <br><br>Three more example match-ups with mixed denominators. | Slides; Comparison Methods handout |
| **Guided Practice** | **Win Rate Match-Ups.** Pairs compare 4 head-to-head pairs of records, all with unlike denominators. For each: express both as percents, identify the winner, note the margin in percentage points. | Win Rate Match-Ups handout |
| **Independent Practice** | **Hero Power Rankings.** 5 heroes, each with a different W/L record (all unlike denominators — e.g., 14/20, 19/27, 31/40, 8/12, 23/30). Students convert each to a percent and rank from highest win rate to lowest. Then: write 2 sentences answering — "Does the hero with the MOST wins necessarily have the highest win rate? Why or why not?" | Power Rankings handout |
| **Closing** | **Exit Ticket** — "The hero with the best win rate was ___ at ___%. To compare records with different totals, the key move is ___." | Handout or journal |

**Differentiation Considerations:**
- **Percentage Conversion Chart** handout (review from Day 2).
- Calculator allowed.
- For students struggling: 5-min pre-teach reviewing percentage conversion (Day 2 callback).
- Visual: bar diagram showing each hero's win rate as a shaded portion of a 100-unit bar.
- Sentence frame for the comparison: "Hero ___ has a higher win rate (___%) than Hero ___ (___%) by ___ percentage points."
- **Extension** for early finishers: "If Hero A loses 3 more fights without winning any, what does their new win rate become?"
- Vocabulary preview: *win rate, percentage points, denominator, normalize*.

**Standards (Massachusetts Curriculum Framework for Mathematics, HS):**
- **N.Q.1** — Use units to guide multi-step proportional comparisons.
- **A.CED.1** — Create equations to compare proportional quantities.
- *Foundational: **6.RP.3.c** — Find a percent of a quantity as a rate per 100; **7.RP.3** — Multi-step percent problems.*

### Day 8 — Hero Gear Budget

**Lesson objectives:**
- Allocate parts of a whole as percentages that sum to exactly 100%.
- Solve "partition" problems with proportional constraints.
- Calculate the dollar amount of each category from a percentage of a total budget.
- Reason about trade-offs when budget allocations must total 100%.

**Assessment:**
- Fix-the-Broken-Budget handout
- Hero Gear Budget worksheet
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- A budget is a *whole* (100%, or $X total). Each category is a *part* — a percentage of the whole. ALL parts must sum to exactly 100%.
- This is the most APPLIED lesson so far — students design a hero's gear allocation across 5 categories. Each allocation has a real trade-off: if you spend 40% on costume, only 60% is left for everything else.
- Two-step calculation: percentage allocation → dollar amount (e.g., 30% of $5,000 = $1,500).
- Sets up Day 9 (large-number percentages on real city budgets) and Day 10 (capstone synthesis).

### Day 9 — The Cost of Heroics

**Lesson objectives:**
- Apply percentage operations to real-world large numbers (city budgets, property damage estimates).
- Estimate sensibly when working with very large numbers.
- Choose an appropriate level of accuracy for the context.
- Connect percentage allocations to civic impact (cross-link to Social Studies Day 10 — civic mission).

**Assessment:**
- Battle Cost Breakdown handout
- Your Hero's Battle worksheet
- Exit Ticket

**Key Points:**
- Real-world applications use BIG numbers — millions of dollars of damage, billions of dollars in city budgets. Percentages work the same way with big numbers, but the cognitive load is higher.
- Estimation matters: sometimes you don't need exact answers, just "is this 1% or 10% of the budget?"
- Real Deloitte-style estimates of *Avengers* movie property damage make the lesson tangible (the 2012 Deloitte / Kinetic Analysis report on Manhattan damage in *The Avengers* is publicly available — referenced widely).
- Cross-link: this lesson sets up the Social Studies Day 10 Civic Mission One-Pager. Heroes cause damage; communities pay. That's a civic question.

| Component | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |

**Lesson objectives:**

- Reflect on which proportional-reasoning skill was most useful across the unit.

**Assessment:**

- Exit Ticket

- Students don't learn new math today — they APPLY everything from Days 1–9 to their hero.
