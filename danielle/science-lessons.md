# Superheroes — Science Lesson Plans

**5 mini-lessons · 30–45 min each · Grades 6–12 (ages 12–19)**
**Final artifact: Power Science Brief** — students explain their original hero's power using real science.

---

## Science Arc

**Driving question:** *Could this power actually work?*

Across 5 lessons, students learn the science that *almost* makes superpowers possible — then explain their own hero's power with real science vocabulary.

# Superheroes: Lesson 1
## DNA & Mutations — Where Powers Come From

### Lesson objectives:
- Identify DNA as the genetic instruction manual found in every cell.
- Define mutation and explain how mutations can change a trait.
- Extract visible DNA from a strawberry and describe the observation.
- Evaluate whether a comic-book "origin" (radioactive bite, lab accident) is scientifically plausible.

### Assessment:
- Completed Worksheet
- Exit Ticket

### Key Points:
- Many superhero origins (Spider-Man, X-Men, Hulk) are grounded in a single idea: their DNA changed. This lesson asks students to take that idea seriously and test it against real biology.
- Students learn that DNA is the instruction manual inside every cell, and that mutations are changes to those instructions — most do nothing, some are harmful, a rare few change a trait in a useful way.
- Students perform a strawberry DNA extraction — a low-cost, dramatic lab that lets them physically see DNA.
- Students apply the science by judging whether common comic-book origins are *plausible*, *requires breakthrough*, or *impossible*.

| Component: | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| Do Now | Question: *"Spider-Man got his powers from a radioactive spider bite. Could that actually work? Yes / No / Maybe — one sentence why."* Students write, then class hand-vote tally. | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |
| Introduction of Superheroes | Introduce the science unit: across 5 lessons, students will learn the science behind powers — and by Lesson 5, write a Power Science Brief for their own hero. | Slides |
| Mini-Lesson | - DNA is the instruction manual inside every cell. <br>- A **mutation** is a change in those instructions. Most do nothing. Some kill the cell. A few rare ones change a trait. <br>- Real example: the **CCR5-delta32** mutation makes some people resistant to HIV — a real "superpower" caused by a real mutation. <br>- Today: pull DNA out of a strawberry so we can see it. | Slides |
| Guided Practice | **Strawberry DNA Extraction Lab.** In groups of 3–4: <br>1. Crush strawberry in zip-top bag with dish soap + salt (breaks cell membranes). <br>2. Filter into clear plastic cup through coffee filter. <br>3. Slowly pour chilled rubbing alcohol down the side of the cup. <br>4. Watch white stringy DNA precipitate. Lift it out with a stir stick. <br>**Safety:** No flames near alcohol. Goggles if available. | Slides, Handouts, strawberries, zip-top bags, dish soap, table salt, rubbing alcohol (chilled), coffee filters, plastic cups, stir sticks |
| Independent Practice | **Origin Plausibility Sort.** Students get a handout with 6 comic-book origins (radioactive spider, gamma rays, super-serum injection, lab accident, alien DNA, lightning strike). They sort each into *Plausible / Requires Breakthrough / Impossible* and write a one-sentence reason. | Handouts |
| Closing | Exit Ticket: *"True or false: A radioactive spider bite could rewrite your DNA to give you spider powers. Defend your answer in two sentences using something we did or said today."* | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |

### Differentiation Considerations:
- Provide a fill-in-the-blank lab notes sheet for students who need scaffolding during the extraction.
- Students may work in groups of 3–4 or with extra teacher support during the lab.
- Origin Plausibility Sort can be reduced to 3 origins instead of 6 for students who need to focus.
- Advanced extension: students research CRISPR and write a paragraph: *if we can edit DNA, who should decide which edits are allowed?*
- Lab can be demonstrated by teacher front-of-class if hands-on group work is not feasible.

### Standard(s):

**Next Generation Science Standards**
- **MS-LS3-1** — Develop and use a model to describe why structural changes to genes (mutations) located on chromosomes may affect proteins and may result in harmful, beneficial, or neutral effects to the structure and function of the organism.
- **HS-LS3-1** — Ask questions to clarify relationships about the role of DNA and chromosomes in coding the instructions for characteristic traits passed from parents to offspring.
- **HS-LS3-2** — Make and defend a claim that inheritable genetic variations may result from new genetic combinations, viable errors during replication, and/or mutations.

**Common Core Standards ELA**
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RST.6-8.1** — Cite specific textual evidence to support analysis of science and technical texts.
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.6-12.1** — Initiate and participate effectively in a range of collaborative discussions.

# Superheroes: Lesson 2
## Genes, Heredity & Punnett Squares

### Lesson objectives:
- Define dominant and recessive alleles.
- Construct a Punnett square to predict trait inheritance.
- Use probability ratios to predict the likelihood of offspring traits.
- Apply the model to a fictional hero family.

### Assessment:
- Completed Worksheet
- Exit Ticket

### Key Points:
- This lesson builds on Lesson 1: now that students know DNA carries instructions, they learn how those instructions get passed from parent to child.
- Students learn dominant/recessive alleles, the capital/lowercase letter notation, and how to build a Punnett square.
- They apply the model by designing a "hero family tree" — two hero parents with three traits each — and predicting which traits the next generation inherits.
- Critical takeaway: a trait can be inherited and *still* not show up. Heredity is probabilistic, not guaranteed.

| Component: | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| Do Now | Question: *"If one parent has super-strength (dominant) and the other does not (recessive), what's the chance their kid has super-strength?"* | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |
| Mini-Lesson | - Refresh: DNA → genes → traits. <br>- Define **dominant** and **recessive alleles**. Notation: capital S = dominant super-strength, lowercase s = no super-strength. <br>- Build a Punnett square together on the board for *Ss × ss*: 50% Ss (super), 50% ss (not). <br>- Complicate it: most real powers wouldn't be single-gene. But Punnett squares are how scientists predict rare-trait inheritance in real medical genetics. | Slides, Handouts |
| Guided Practice | Teacher walks through one full example with the class: *Ss × Ss*. Class predicts ratios: 1 SS : 2 Ss : 1 ss = 75% super-strong, 25% not. Why? Because S is dominant. | Slides, Handouts |
| Independent Practice | **Hero Family Tree Activity.** In pairs, students invent two hero parents with three traits each (e.g., flight, super-strength, telepathy). For each trait: <br>1. Decide if it's dominant or recessive. <br>2. Build a Punnett square. <br>3. Calculate the % chance their offspring inherits all three powers. <br>4. Roll dice to "simulate" the actual offspring's genotype. <br>5. Sketch the resulting child and label their traits. | Slides, Handouts, dice (2 per pair), colored pencils |
| Closing | Exit Ticket: *"A trait can be inherited and still not show up. Explain why in one sentence."* | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |

### Differentiation Considerations:
- Provide pre-set parental genotypes for students who struggle to invent their own.
- Allow students to work in groups of 3–4 instead of pairs to share the cognitive load.
- Visual learners can be given a printed Punnett square template to fill in rather than drawing from scratch.
- Advanced extension: introduce a second-trait dependency (e.g., flight only works *if* the strength gene is also present) and challenge students to model it.
- Sentence stems for the exit ticket: "A trait can be inherited but not show up because…"

### Standard(s):

**Next Generation Science Standards**
- **MS-LS3-2** — Develop and use a model to describe why asexual reproduction results in offspring with identical genetic information and sexual reproduction results in offspring with genetic variation.
- **HS-LS3-3** — Apply concepts of statistics and probability to explain the variation and distribution of expressed traits in a population.
- **SP5** — Using mathematics and computational thinking.

**Common Core Standards Math**
- **CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.7.SP.C.7** — Develop a probability model and use it to find probabilities of events.

**Common Core Standards ELA**
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.6-12.1** — Initiate and participate effectively in a range of collaborative discussions.

# Superheroes: Lesson 3
## Materials Science — Vibranium & Beyond

### Lesson objectives:
- Identify three real-world advanced materials (Kevlar, titanium, carbon nanotubes) and their properties.
- Explain how a material's structure determines its function.
- Design a fictional material with three specified properties and propose a real-world use.

### Assessment:
- Completed Worksheet
- Exit Ticket

### Key Points:
- Not all superpowers come from biology — many come from **materials**. Shuri's Vibranium, Iron Man's suit, Spider-Man's webs, Captain America's shield: all materials science.
- Students learn that real engineers design materials for specific jobs. Kevlar stops bullets because of how its molecules are aligned. Titanium is strong because of how its atoms pack. Carbon nanotubes are stronger than steel and lighter than paper because of geometry.
- Vibranium is a "thought experiment" — *what if* a material could do all those things at once?
- Students design their own fictional material with 3 properties and identify a real-world problem it could solve.

| Component: | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| Do Now | Question: *"Name 3 things stronger than human bone."* Quick share-out. | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |
| Mini-Lesson | - Powers don't always come from the body. They can come from **what the hero wears, holds, or builds.** <br>- Show 3 real-world materials: **Kevlar** (bullet-resistant — used in vests), **titanium** (strong + light — used in jet engines), **carbon nanotubes** (stronger than steel, lighter than paper — being researched for space elevators). <br>- Each is special because of its *structure*. Materials science = designing structure to get desired properties. <br>- Vibranium is fictional, but its properties (absorbs vibration, lightweight, nearly unbreakable) are what real engineers chase. <br>- Connect to Shuri (Black Panther's sister, Wakanda's chief engineer): the hero as scientist. | Slides |
| Guided Practice | Class brainstorm on chart paper: *"If a material could have any 3 properties, what would they be?"* Collect 10–15 student suggestions. Examples: lightweight, fireproof, flexible, transparent, magnetic, self-healing, edible. | Slides, chart paper |
| Independent Practice | **Material Design Challenge.** Each student (or pair) designs a fictional material: <br>1. Give it a name. <br>2. Choose 3 properties (from the class list or new). <br>3. Name 1 real-world problem it solves (e.g., a fireproof, flexible, lightweight fabric could save firefighters). <br>4. Sketch the material in use — diagram with labels. | Handouts, colored pencils |
| Closing | Exit Ticket: *"Pick one real-world material from today (Kevlar, titanium, or carbon nanotubes). Name one job it does. What property makes it good at that job?"* | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |

### Differentiation Considerations:
- Provide a property menu (with 8–10 pre-listed properties) for students who struggle with brainstorming.
- Allow students to draw their material in use rather than write a description.
- Sentence frames: "My material is called ___. It is ___, ___, and ___. It solves the problem of ___."
- Advanced extension: research a real cutting-edge material (graphene, aerogel, shape-memory alloy) and present a 1-minute explainer.
- Pairs or small groups for students who benefit from collaborative idea generation.

### Standard(s):

**Next Generation Science Standards**
- **MS-PS1-3** — Gather and make sense of information to describe that synthetic materials come from natural resources and impact society.
- **HS-PS2-6** — Communicate scientific and technical information about why the molecular-level structure is important in the functioning of designed materials.
- **MS-ETS1-1** — Define the criteria and constraints of a design problem with sufficient precision to ensure a successful solution.

**Common Core Standards ELA**
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RST.6-12.4** — Determine the meaning of symbols, key terms, and other domain-specific words and phrases used in a specific scientific context.
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.6-12.1** — Initiate and participate effectively in a range of collaborative discussions.

# Superheroes: Lesson 4
## The Brain on Fear — Courage as Neuroscience

### Lesson objectives:
- Label the parts of the brain involved in the fear response (amygdala, hypothalamus).
- Describe the fight/flight/freeze response and the physical changes it triggers.
- Explain why courage is *acting while* the fear response is firing, not the absence of fear.
- Apply the model to their own hero: what does the hero fear, and what does the hero do anyway?

### Assessment:
- Completed Worksheet
- Exit Ticket

### Key Points:
- Not all powers are biological or material — some live in the brain. The most universal hero trait is **courage**, and courage has real neuroscience behind it.
- Students learn the fear response chain: amygdala detects threat → hypothalamus signals adrenal glands → adrenaline + cortisol flood the body → heart rate up, blood to muscles, fight/flight/freeze.
- Critical reframe: **the fear response is not weakness. It's your body trying to save your life.** Courage is acting *while* this is firing.
- This lesson lands especially hard for students who have been told (or told themselves) that fear means failure. **Teachers should not rush this lesson.**

| Component: | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| Do Now | Question: *"Your heart is pounding. Your hands shake. Your stomach drops. Is this fear, excitement, or both? What's the difference?"* | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |
| Mini-Lesson | Build the chain on the board: <br>- Threat detected → **amygdala** fires <br>- Amygdala signals → **hypothalamus** → adrenal glands → **adrenaline + cortisol** <br>- Body: heart rate up, blood to muscles, digestion off, pupils dilate <br>- This is **fight, flight, or freeze**. <br><br>Key idea: **the fear response is not weakness. It's your body trying to save your life.** Courage isn't the *absence* of this response — courage is acting *while* it's firing. That's literally what every soldier, firefighter, EMT, and parent in a crisis does. | Slides, brain diagram handout |
| Guided Practice | Pass out brain diagram. As a class, label the amygdala and hypothalamus. Briefly note: the prefrontal cortex (above the eyes) is what *overrides* the fear response. That's the part you train through practice and reflection. | Brain diagram handout |
| Independent Practice | **Fear Scenario Activity.** In groups of 3, students read 5 scenario cards (e.g., "speaking in front of a judge," "running into a burning building," "telling someone you love them," "asking for help," "standing up to a friend who's wrong"). For each: <br>- Mark whether the body's fear response would fire. <br>- Mark whether the action takes *courage*. <br><br>Notice the overlap: every act of courage has the body screaming "don't." <br><br>Then individually, each student writes 2–3 sentences answering: *what does your hero fear, and what do they do anyway?* | Slides, scenario cards, Handouts |
| Closing | Exit Ticket: *"Name one thing that scared you this year that you did anyway."* (Students may write privately — this is not for share-out unless they volunteer.) | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |

### Differentiation Considerations:
- Provide a pre-labeled brain diagram with shorter scenarios for students who need scaffolding.
- Allow students to whisper or draw their exit ticket response if writing about personal fear is difficult.
- Be mindful: this lesson can surface real emotional material. Have a plan for students who may need a break or one-on-one time.
- Advanced extension: research how chronic stress alters the amygdala over time, and what protective practices (mindfulness, exercise, social support) help reverse those changes.
- Group size flexibility: 3–4 for richer discussion, individual work if group dynamics are off that day.

### Standard(s):

**Next Generation Science Standards**
- **MS-LS1-3** — Use argument supported by evidence for how the body is a system of interacting subsystems composed of groups of cells.
- **HS-LS1-2** — Develop and use a model to illustrate the hierarchical organization of interacting systems that provide specific functions within multicellular organisms.
- **HS-LS1-3** — Plan and conduct an investigation to provide evidence that feedback mechanisms maintain homeostasis.

**Common Core Standards ELA**
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.6-12.1** — Initiate and participate effectively in a range of collaborative discussions.
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.W.6-12.10** — Write routinely over extended and shorter time frames.

# Superheroes: Lesson 5
## Power Science Brief — Explain Your Hero's Power

### Lesson objectives:
- Synthesize learning from Lessons 1–4 to explain a fictional hero's power with real science.
- Use domain-specific vocabulary (mutation, allele, materials, amygdala, etc.) accurately in writing.
- Identify the **mechanism** and **limit** of a power, not just its effect.
- Participate in peer review using a structured protocol.

### Assessment:
- **Final product: Power Science Brief (1 page)** — goes into the Hero Codex portfolio
- Exit Ticket

### Key Points:
- This is the synthesis lesson. Students have spent four lessons learning the science toolkit. Now they apply it.
- The Power Science Brief is a one-page explainer that names a hero's power, identifies which scientific category explains it (genetics / materials / neuroscience / a blend), describes the *mechanism*, and names a *limit*.
- Critical concept: **every real power has a cost.** A power with no limit isn't a power — it's a story-killer. The Brief must include at least one realistic constraint.
- Peer review uses the **TAG protocol** (Tell something specific, Ask a clarifying question, Give one suggestion).

| Component: | Teacher & Student Actions | Materials |
|---|---|---|
| Do Now | Question: *"What's your hero's #1 power? Write one sentence on how it might work."* | Piece of paper, journal or Handouts |
| Mini-Lesson | Review the **science toolkit** from the past 4 lessons: <br>1. **Genetics & mutation** (Lesson 1) <br>2. **Heredity & inheritance** (Lesson 2) <br>3. **Materials science** (Lesson 3) <br>4. **Neuroscience & courage** (Lesson 4) <br><br>Introduce the **Power Science Brief template** (provided as a handout): <br>- Hero name <br>- Power (one sentence) <br>- Scientific category (which lesson's science explains it?) <br>- Mechanism (how does it work in 3–5 sentences, using real science vocabulary) <br>- Limit (what's the cost or constraint?) <br>- Real-world example (what existing material, animal, or phenomenon is the closest comparison?) <br><br>Show one teacher exemplar: a finished Brief for a sample hero. | Slides, Brief template handout, teacher exemplar |
| Independent Practice | Students draft their Power Science Brief. Teacher circulates and conferences. | Brief template handout, science notebooks (for reference back to lessons) |
| Partner Practice | Students swap drafts with a partner and use the **TAG protocol**: <br>- **T**ell: one specific thing you liked. <br>- **A**sk: one clarifying question. <br>- **G**ive: one suggestion. | Peer Review handout |
| Closing | Students revise based on TAG feedback. Briefs go into the Hero Codex portfolio for the final showcase. Exit Ticket: *"What's the cost of your hero's power? Every real thing in nature has one."* | Final Brief, Handouts |

### Differentiation Considerations:
- Provide a fill-in-the-blank Brief template for students who need maximum scaffolding.
- Allow students to dictate their Brief to a partner who writes it down, then revise together.
- Visual learners can include a labeled diagram alongside or in place of the written paragraph.
- Advanced extension: students cite one real scientific source (textbook, article, encyclopedia entry) in their Brief.
- Pair students who need support with stronger writers for the peer-review step.

### Standard(s):

**Next Generation Science Standards**
- **HS-LS3-2** — Make and defend a claim that inheritable genetic variations may result from new genetic combinations, viable errors during replication, and/or mutations.
- **SP6** — Constructing explanations.
- **SP8** — Obtaining, evaluating, and communicating information.

**Common Core Standards ELA**
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.W.6-12.2** — Write informative/explanatory texts to examine a topic and convey ideas, concepts, and information through the selection, organization, and analysis of relevant content.
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.W.6-12.4** — Produce clear and coherent writing in which the development, organization, and style are appropriate to task, purpose, and audience.
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.W.6-12.5** — Develop and strengthen writing as needed by planning, revising, editing, rewriting.
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RST.6-12.4** — Determine the meaning of symbols, key terms, and other domain-specific words and phrases used in a specific scientific context.
