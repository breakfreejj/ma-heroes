## Mythology & World Heroes (History Day 1)

Suggested concise summaries — teachers should source 1-paragraph versions per hero card. Good public-domain sources include:

- **Anansi (West Africa):** Trickster-spider hero from Akan oral tradition (Ghana). Wisdom-keeper. See Virginia Hamilton, *A Ring of Tricksters* (Blue Sky Press, 1997).
- **Sun Wukong (China):** The Monkey King from Wu Cheng'en's *Journey to the West* (16th century). Available in Anthony C. Yu's English translation, University of Chicago Press.
- **Maui (Polynesia):** Demigod across Hawaiian, Maori, and other Polynesian traditions. See *Hawaiian Mythology* by Martha Beckwith (University of Hawaii Press, 1970).
- **Gilgamesh (Mesopotamia):** *The Epic of Gilgamesh*, translated by Stephen Mitchell (Free Press, 2004). World's oldest surviving epic.
- **Cú Chulainn (Ireland):** The Ulster Cycle hero. See *The Táin* translated by Thomas Kinsella (Oxford University Press).
- **Mulan (China):** *The Ballad of Mulan*, anonymous, 6th century CE. Many translations available online.

## Primary Sources

### History Day 3 — Harriet Tubman triangulation

1. **Fugitive notice / advertisement** for the recapture of escaped enslaved persons aided by Tubman. Newspapers of the period (1850s–60s). The Library of Congress and the National Underground Railroad Freedom Center have searchable archives.

2. **Frederick Douglass, letter to Harriet Tubman, August 29, 1868.** Excerpt: *"The midnight sky and the silent stars have been the witnesses of your devotion to freedom and of your heroism."* Full text available in *Life and Times of Frederick Douglass* (Park Publishing, 1881).

3. **Modern textbook entry** — Use any current MA-adopted U.S. history textbook's Tubman section, OR Catherine Clinton's *Harriet Tubman: The Road to Freedom* (Little, Brown, 2004).

### History Day 4 — Massachusetts Heroes

- **Crispus Attucks:** Primary source from John Adams's defense of British soldiers at the Boston Massacre trial (1770) — Adams's own words describing Attucks reveal how a hero can be slandered in the historical record.
- **Phillis Wheatley:** *Poems on Various Subjects, Religious and Moral* (1773). Several poems available in the public domain via Project Gutenberg.
- **Frederick Douglass:** *Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass, an American Slave* (1845), Chapter X covers his time in Massachusetts.
- **William Lloyd Garrison:** Opening editorial of *The Liberator*, January 1, 1831 ("I am in earnest — I will not equivocate — I will not excuse — I will not retreat a single inch — AND I WILL BE HEARD.").
- **Mum Bett / Elizabeth Freeman:** *Brom and Bett v. Ashley* (1781). Court records available via the Massachusetts Historical Society.
- **Mel King:** *Chain of Change: Struggles for Black Community Development* (South End Press, 1981).

## Suggested supplementary reading for teachers

These don't appear in the lesson plans, but they shape the curriculum's design and would deepen any teacher's preparation:

- **Joseph Campbell,** *The Hero with a Thousand Faces* (Pantheon, 1949). The hero's journey monomyth that structures the 5-week arc.
- **bell hooks,** *Teaching to Transgress* (Routledge, 1994). Pedagogy of liberation; relevant to designing curriculum for marginalized youth.
- **Bryan Stevenson,** *Just Mercy* (Spiegel & Grau, 2014). Essential reading for anyone teaching justice-involved youth.
- **Geoffrey Canada,** *Fist Stick Knife Gun* (Beacon Press, 1995). Memoir of growing up amid violence; helpful for understanding the lived context many students bring.
