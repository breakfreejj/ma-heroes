# Origin Story — Lesson Plan Outlines

**A cross-curricular PBL mini-unit on superheroes**
**40 mini-lessons · 10 per subject · 30–45 min each**

Each lesson follows the standard structure:
- **Do Now / Bellringer** (2 min)
- **Mini Lesson** — teacher explains content
- **Mini Activity** — students apply what they just learned

---

## Anchor Heroes

| Hero | Why this one | Curricular doors |
|---|---|---|
| **Miles Morales (Spider-Man)** | Afro-Latino Brooklyn teen; *Spider-Verse* is hugely current | Physics of motion · Identity / origin writing |
| **Shuri (Black Panther universe)** | Brilliant Black female engineer/scientist, princess of Wakanda | Engineering · Materials science · Afrofuturism |
| **Storm (Ororo Munroe)** | Iconic Black female X-Men leader, weather powers | Earth science / weather · Civil rights allegory |
| **Ms. Marvel (Kamala Khan)** | Pakistani-American teen, comic-book fan turned hero | ELA voice · Global mythology · Identity |

# MATH — 10 Mini-Lessons

**Arc:** Students build the mathematical toolkit to design and defend their own hero. Each lesson adds a piece to a "Hero Stat Sheet & Battle Analysis" submitted as their math artifact in the final pitch.

### Day 1 — The Hero Power Index
- **Do Now:** Rank these 4 heroes 1–4 on strength using your gut. Defend your #1.
- **Mini Lesson:** Introduce the 5 standard hero stats (Strength, Speed, Intelligence, Durability, Energy). Show how Marvel actually publishes these on a 1–7 scale. Quick review of mean / median.
- **Activity:** Students get stat cards for 6 heroes and find the mean stat for each. Whose mean is highest? Does that match who'd "win"?

### Day 2 — Normalizing & Ranking
- **Do Now:** If Hero A has 70 points out of 100 and Hero B has 14 points out of 20, who scored higher?
- **Mini Lesson:** Percentages and ratios — making different scales comparable. Demonstrate converting raw stats to percentages.
- **Activity:** Convert the Marvel 1–7 stats to percentages and re-rank the heroes. Did anyone's rank change?

### Day 3 — Linear Equations: Hero Speed
- **Do Now:** Miles can swing 30 mph. How far in 2 hours? In 15 minutes?
- **Mini Lesson:** y = mx + b for distance over time. Slope as speed. Plot on a graph.
- **Activity:** Given speeds for Miles, Storm (flying), and a regular human, students write each hero's distance equation and graph all three on the same axes.

### Day 4 — Systems of Equations: The Chase
- **Do Now:** Look at yesterday's graph. Where do any two lines cross? What does that point *mean*?
- **Mini Lesson:** Intersection of two lines = when two heroes meet. Solve a system algebraically.
- **Activity:** A villain has a 10-minute head start at 40 mph. Miles is at 60 mph. When and where does Miles catch the villain? Solve algebraically AND graph.

### Day 5 — Geometry & Scale
- **Do Now:** Ant-Man is 6 ft tall and shrinks to ½ inch. By what factor did he shrink?
- **Mini Lesson:** Scale factors, ratio, proportion. Brief tease of why volume changes faster than length (Science Day 6 picks this up).
- **Activity:** Story problems with Shuri's blueprints — if her Wakandan tech needs to be 1:48 scale for a model, what are the model dimensions?

### Day 6 — Projectile Motion (the Web Swing)
- **Do Now:** If you throw a ball straight up, draw the path. Now imagine throwing it forward — what shape is the path?
- **Mini Lesson:** Parabolic arc, intro to quadratics conceptually. Where is the peak? Where does it land?
- **Activity:** Given a simple quadratic for Miles's web-swing height, find peak height and landing distance using a table of values.

### Day 7 — Probability: Single Matchups
- **Do Now:** Coin flip — what's the chance of heads? Of two heads in a row?
- **Mini Lesson:** Basic probability, independent events. Frame as "if Hero A wins 60% of fights with Hero B, what's the chance of A winning 2 in a row?"
- **Activity:** Given win-rate matchups for our 4 anchor heroes, calculate 2-fight and 3-fight win probabilities.

### Day 8 — Tournament Brackets
- **Do Now:** With 4 heroes in a bracket, how many total matchups happen?
- **Mini Lesson:** Compound probability across a bracket. Tree diagrams.
- **Activity:** Build a 4-hero bracket and calculate the probability each hero wins the whole tournament given pairwise win rates.

### Day 9 — Real-World Math: Cost of Heroics
- **Do Now:** Estimate the cost to repair a skyscraper window. To replace a city block?
- **Mini Lesson:** Reading large numbers, working with budgets and percent. Use real Deloitte-style estimates of *Avengers* movie property damage.
- **Activity:** Students get a "battle scene" damage list and calculate total cost, then figure out what percent of a hero's $X budget that represents.

### Day 10 — Build Your Hero's Stat Sheet (Math Artifact)
- **Do Now:** What two stats matter most for *your* hero's mission? Why?
- **Mini Lesson:** Quick review — students will now combine everything: stats with justification, a speed equation, a probability of winning their toughest matchup, and a damage budget.
- **Activity:** Students complete the **Math One-Pager** for their original hero (stat sheet + speed equation + win probability + budget). Goes into the pitch portfolio.

# SCIENCE — 10 Mini-Lessons

**Arc:** "Could this power actually work?" Students learn the science that *almost* makes superpowers possible, then explain their own hero's power with real science. Artifact: **Power Science Brief**.

### Day 1 — What Makes a Power "Plausible"?
- **Do Now:** List 3 superpowers. Star the one you think is most scientifically possible. Why?
- **Mini Lesson:** Intro to scientific plausibility — hypothesis, evidence, mechanism. Real vs. fictional.
- **Activity:** Sort 10 superpower cards into "Could Happen / Far Off / Impossible" with one-sentence reasoning each.

### Day 2 — Genetics & Mutation
- **Do Now:** Why do some siblings look so different?
- **Mini Lesson:** Genes, DNA, mutation. X-Men as the "mutation" hero family. Real mutations (lactose tolerance, sickle cell trait) — mutation isn't always bad.
- **Activity:** Students design a "mutation card": which gene mutated, what changed, is it helpful or harmful?

### Day 3 — Forces & Motion (Newton's Laws)
- **Do Now:** Push your textbook. Now push it twice as hard. What changes?
- **Mini Lesson:** Newton's 3 laws using hero examples (Miles's web pulls him = equal/opposite reaction).
- **Activity:** Match 6 hero moments to the correct Newton's law and explain why.

### Day 4 — Energy & Work
- **Do Now:** Which has more energy: Storm's lightning bolt or a thrown brick?
- **Mini Lesson:** Kinetic vs. potential energy; conservation of energy. Real numbers — joules in a lightning bolt vs. a punch.
- **Activity:** Energy ranking task: order 6 hero attacks by joules. Justify with the formulas at a conceptual level.

### Day 5 — Materials Science (Vibranium & Beyond)
- **Do Now:** Name 3 things stronger than human bone.
- **Mini Lesson:** Real materials science — what makes Kevlar, titanium, carbon nanotubes special. Shuri's Vibranium as a "thought experiment" version of these.
- **Activity:** Students design a fictional material with 3 properties (e.g., flexible, fireproof, lightweight) — what real-world problem could it solve?

### Day 6 — Biology of Strength & Speed (Why Hulk Can't Exist… Yet)
- **Do Now:** Could a human really lift a car? Why or why not?
- **Mini Lesson:** Square-cube law (callback to Math Day 5), muscle physiology, biomechanics. Why scaling a body up breaks biology.
- **Activity:** Students calculate or estimate: if Hulk is 8 ft tall and weighs 1,000 lb, what would his actual heart need to do? (Order-of-magnitude OK.)

### Day 7 — Weather & Atmosphere (Storm's Science)
- **Do Now:** Why does lightning happen?
- **Mini Lesson:** Atmospheric layers, weather formation, electric charge separation. Storm's powers as Earth science lesson.
- **Activity:** Storm wants to make it rain over a drought-stricken farm. Students diagram what *actually* needs to happen in the atmosphere for that rain.

### Day 8 — Chemistry of Super-Serums
- **Do Now:** What's a medicine you've taken? What did it do in your body?
- **Mini Lesson:** Basic pharmacology — how molecules interact with receptors. Captain America's serum as a "what if?" version.
- **Activity:** Students design a serum: pick 1 enhancement (strength, healing, focus) and describe what system in the body it would have to target, plus 1 realistic side effect.

### Day 9 — Engineering Design (Shuri's Lab)
- **Do Now:** Sketch a gadget that would help someone you care about in their daily life.
- **Mini Lesson:** Engineering design cycle (problem → constraints → prototype → test). Shuri and Iron Man as engineers, not just powered heroes.
- **Activity:** Mini design sprint — students sketch a hero gadget with labeled parts and a one-sentence "what problem it solves."

### Day 10 — Explain Your Hero's Power (Science Artifact)
- **Do Now:** What's your hero's #1 power? Write one sentence on how it might work.
- **Mini Lesson:** Review the science toolkit (genetics, forces, energy, materials, biology, chemistry, engineering). Students pick which one(s) explain their power.
- **Activity:** Students complete the **Power Science Brief** — 1 page explaining their hero's power with real science vocabulary. Into the portfolio.

# ENGLISH / LANGUAGE ARTS — 10 Mini-Lessons

**Arc:** Joseph Campbell's Hero's Journey as the literary spine. Students read short excerpts (graphic novel pages, comic origin pages), map heroes onto the Journey, and write their own hero's origin story. Artifact: **Origin Story (1–2 pages)**.

### Day 1 — What Is a Hero?
- **Do Now:** Write the name of one real-life hero (not a superhero). Why them?
- **Mini Lesson:** Definitions of hero across cultures and time. Brainstorm hero traits as a class.
- **Activity:** Students draft a personal "hero traits" list (5 traits) and rank them. Share-out.

### Day 2 — Hero's Journey Overview
- **Do Now:** Watch/read a 2-min summary of one anchor hero's origin (Miles Morales recommended). What changes in him from start to end?
- **Mini Lesson:** Campbell's Hero's Journey — 8 simplified stages. Visual diagram.
- **Activity:** Students map Miles Morales onto the 8 stages as a class with a graphic organizer.

### Day 3 — The Ordinary World & Call to Adventure
- **Do Now:** Describe your "ordinary world" in 3 sentences.
- **Mini Lesson:** Stage 1 — establishing a character's normal life makes the change matter. Show examples from Kamala Khan and Shuri.
- **Activity:** Students draft the "Ordinary World" paragraph of their own hero.

### Day 4 — Refusal, Mentor, Crossing the Threshold
- **Do Now:** Name a time you didn't want to do something hard. What changed your mind?
- **Mini Lesson:** Stages 2–4. Mentors matter (Uncle Ben, T'Chaka, Professor X). Crossing the threshold = no going back.
- **Activity:** Students name a mentor figure for their hero and draft the "moment everything changes" scene.

### Day 5 — Trials, Allies, Enemies
- **Do Now:** Who are 3 people in your life who help you? Who are 3 obstacles?
- **Mini Lesson:** Stage 5 — character building through conflict. Allies and enemies reveal who the hero is.
- **Activity:** Students design a 3-character supporting cast for their hero (1 ally, 1 enemy, 1 complicated person).

### Day 6 — The Ordeal & Transformation
- **Do Now:** Recall a moment that changed how you see yourself.
- **Mini Lesson:** Stage 6 — the lowest point. Why heroes have to *lose* before they win. Read a short excerpt (e.g., Spider-Man losing Uncle Ben).
- **Activity:** Students draft their hero's "darkest moment" — what's the loss, and what do they learn?

### Day 7 — Return & The Gift
- **Do Now:** If you could give one gift to your community, what would it be?
- **Mini Lesson:** Stages 7–8 — the hero returns changed, with something to give. *Heroism is what you do with what you've been through.*
- **Activity:** Students draft their hero's "gift to the world" — what they bring back.

### Day 8 — Graphic Novel Literacy
- **Do Now:** Look at a comic page (provided). What do you "read" first — image or words?
- **Mini Lesson:** Panels, gutters, framing, dialogue boxes vs. narration. How visual storytelling works.
- **Activity:** Students sketch a 4-panel sequence of one scene from their hero's story (stick figures fine — focus on panel choices).

### Day 9 — Drafting the Origin Story
- **Do Now:** Skim your Days 3–7 drafts. Which part is strongest?
- **Mini Lesson:** Combining scenes into a flowing 1–2 page origin story. Show a model origin story.
- **Activity:** Students draft the full origin story in class, pulling from the pieces they've built.

### Day 10 — Revision & Peer Feedback (ELA Artifact)
- **Do Now:** What's one thing a good reader would tell you to fix in your draft?
- **Mini Lesson:** Peer feedback protocol — 2 stars (what's working) and 1 wish (what to develop).
- **Activity:** Pairs trade and give feedback. Students do one revision pass. Final origin story goes into the pitch portfolio.

# SOCIAL STUDIES — 10 Mini-Lessons

**Arc:** What does heroism mean across cultures, history, and civic life? Students examine real heroes and design a civic mission for their own. Artifact: **Hero's Civic Mission (one-pager)**.

### Day 1 — Heroes Across Cultures
- **Do Now:** Name a hero from a story your family or culture tells.
- **Mini Lesson:** Global hero traditions — Anansi (West African), Sun Wukong (Chinese), La Llorona / Quetzalcoatl (Mesoamerican), Indigenous trickster figures. Heroes don't all look the same.
- **Activity:** Each student picks one global hero from a provided set and writes a 3-fact summary.

### Day 2 — American Comic Origins & WWII
- **Do Now:** Why do you think Captain America wears the flag?
- **Mini Lesson:** Captain America #1 (1941) punched Hitler before the U.S. entered WWII. Comics as propaganda and morale-building. Primary source analysis.
- **Activity:** Students compare two WWII comic covers and identify the persuasive techniques used.

### Day 3 — Civil Rights Allegory (X-Men)
- **Do Now:** What does it mean to be "feared for what you are"?
- **Mini Lesson:** X-Men as a 1960s civil rights allegory. Stan Lee's own words on it. Storm as a Black female leader of the team.
- **Activity:** Students annotate a short X-Men excerpt for civil-rights parallels.

### Day 4 — Two Paths, Same Pain (Magneto & Killmonger)
- **Do Now:** Can a villain be "right" about something?
- **Mini Lesson:** Magneto (Holocaust survivor) vs. Professor X. Killmonger (raised in Oakland, witnessed injustice) vs. T'Challa. Same wound, different response.
- **Activity:** Students fill out a T-chart for one pair — what they agree on, where they split.

### Day 5 — Government & Oversight (Civil War)
- **Do Now:** Should heroes have to follow government rules?
- **Mini Lesson:** The Sokovia Accords / *Civil War* storyline as a real lesson on accountability, due process, individual rights vs. collective safety.
- **Activity:** Structured debate — half the room argues for oversight, half against. Switch sides after 5 min.

### Day 6 — Community Heroes
- **Do Now:** Who in your life or community is a hero, and why?
- **Mini Lesson:** Heroism without a cape — teachers, organizers, elders, mentors. Read a short profile of a real community hero.
- **Activity:** Students draft a 1-paragraph profile of someone they know who deserves the title "hero."

### Day 7 — Culture & Economics (The Black Panther Moment)
- **Do Now:** Have you seen a movie that made you feel proud of who you are? Which one?
- **Mini Lesson:** *Black Panther* (2018) — cultural impact, box office, representation. Why representation in media matters as a civic issue.
- **Activity:** Students read 2 short data points (box office, audience demographics) and write a 3-sentence response to "why did this matter?"

### Day 8 — Ethics of Power
- **Do Now:** "With great power comes great responsibility." Agree, disagree, or it depends?
- **Mini Lesson:** Ethical frameworks at a kid level — duty, consequences, character. Real-world parallels (whistleblowers, civic leaders).
- **Activity:** 3 ethical dilemma cards — students choose a stance and defend it in writing.

### Day 9 — Mapping Your Hero's Community
- **Do Now:** Where is your hero from? What's that place like?
- **Mini Lesson:** Place matters — Miles in Brooklyn, Shuri in Wakanda, Kamala in Jersey City. Setting shapes mission.
- **Activity:** Students sketch a "map" of their hero's community — who lives there, what problems exist, what's worth protecting.

### Day 10 — Your Hero's Civic Mission (Social Studies Artifact)
- **Do Now:** What's one real problem in a community you care about?
- **Mini Lesson:** A hero's mission is rooted in a real problem. Connect their power (Science), backstory (ELA), and resources (Math) to a *civic* mission.
- **Activity:** Students complete the **Civic Mission One-Pager** — the real-world problem, why their hero is the one to address it, and what action they take. Into the portfolio.

# The Final Project — The Hero Pitch

By the end of the 40 lessons, every student has four artifacts that *are* the pitch:

1. **Math:** Hero Stat Sheet & Battle Analysis
2. **Science:** Power Science Brief
3. **ELA:** Origin Story
4. **Social Studies:** Civic Mission One-Pager

**Format:** Each student delivers a 3–5 minute "Hero Pitch" (Shark Tank–style), presenting their original hero using all four artifacts.

**Judging:** Craft of each artifact + integration across them.

**Levels:** Classroom champion → school-level final. Optional: showcase booklet of all finalists.
